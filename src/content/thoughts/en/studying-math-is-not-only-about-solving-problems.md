> What truly matters is not formulas, but the ability to understand problems and reason.

After reading Letter 042, "Why Study Mathematics?" from Wu Jun's *Silicon Valley Letters 2: The Google Methodology*, my biggest reflection was this: many people mistakenly think the purpose of learning math is to calculate, solve exam problems, and take tests. But the real value of math is not whether a formula can still be used later in life. It is that math trains a person to understand problems, build logic, and derive conclusions.

Many people have this question: much of the math learned in middle school and high school seems rarely used directly later in life. Geometry, functions, equations, and many other topics may gradually be forgotten after graduation, and many people may not be able to solve those problems again. So some people think learning so much math is meaningless and only exists for exams. But this letter reminds me that if we judge the value of math only by whether a specific knowledge point will be directly useful later, we are understanding math too narrowly. The meaning of math is not only remembering knowledge, but training a way of thinking.

Wu Jun mentions that doing well in math is not simply "learning math," but "testing well in math," and that the key has three parts: first, reading comprehension; second, building a complete mathematical knowledge system; third, using logic well. This is very enlightening. Many students cannot solve math problems not because they cannot calculate at all, but because they have not truly understood the question. Every condition in a problem may matter, but if one reads too hastily, it is easy to miss the key. Math problems seem to test numbers on the surface, but they also test reading and comprehension.

For example, if a geometry problem gives an "equilateral triangle," it is not only saying that the three sides are equal. It also means all three angles are 60 degrees, and it may imply specific relationships involving distances from an interior point to the three sides. A person who can think will not stop at the literal wording, but will dig out the hidden information behind the problem. This ability matters not only in math, but also in real life. Many problems look simple on the surface, but to solve them, one must first understand the conditions, distinguish the main from the secondary, and discover hidden information.

The second important ability is building a knowledge system. Many people learn math by drilling problems and memorizing methods. When they meet a certain type of problem, they apply a certain trick. This may work in the short term, but without a complete knowledge system, a slight change in the problem can cause them to lose direction. The letter points out that mathematical knowledge is of course important, but the knowledge itself is not the final purpose. It is a tool for training thought. When a person learns the basic concepts, theorems, and reasoning methods in geometry, they are not only learning to solve a few geometry problems. They are gaining a foundation for analyzing structural problems.

The third ability is logical reasoning. The core training of math is deriving conclusions step by step from known conditions. This training may look dry, but it is extremely important. Many judgments in the real world need a similar ability. A person cannot only say "I feel this is true." They need to explain why and derive a reasonable conclusion from facts and conditions. Math trains exactly this: not skipping steps, not guessing randomly, not judging by emotion, but making every step have a basis.

I also think the deeper point of this letter is that it asks us to reflect on today's education. Many students fear math not necessarily because they truly lack ability, but because math has been overly turned into an exam tool. Schools and parents often look only at scores, while ignoring the understanding and logic math should train. As a result, students do many problems without necessarily knowing why they are learning them. They memorize many formulas without truly forming thinking ability. This kind of math learning easily causes pain and also makes people forget everything after graduation.

The core reflection this letter left me with is that the purpose of learning math is not to make everyone a mathematician, nor to make people remember every formula for life. It is to give people a clearer thinking ability. Reading comprehension helps us understand problems. A knowledge system gives us tools to solve problems. Logical reasoning lets us move from conditions to conclusions. What math truly leaves behind is not the answer to a particular problem, but the ability to remain calm, analyze, reason, and judge when facing complex problems.
