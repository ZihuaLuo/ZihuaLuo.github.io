> Looking only at price leads to misjudgment; real cost includes time, risk, trouble, and opportunities given up.

In Lecture 015, Xue Zhaofeng continues the idea of cost. Many people think economics cares only about money. Good economic thinking does the opposite: it looks at total cost. Money is only one part. Time, energy, risk, information, trouble, and opportunity are also costs.

A common mistake is assuming cheap means low cost. Buying second-hand goods may reduce the cash price, but searching, comparing, and bearing the risk of fake or unsuitable goods all take time and uncertainty. These invisible parts may exceed the money saved. Buying at a convenience store costs more money, but may reduce total cost by saving time and trouble.

Living far from work is similar. Rent may be lower, but commuting takes more time and energy and compresses learning, rest, and relationships. If we only look at rent, it seems economical. Once travel fatigue and opportunity cost are included, the real cost may not be low.

Conscription shows the issue more sharply. If a country drafts soldiers and pays low wages, government cash spending looks lower. But the young people drafted might otherwise become engineers, doctors, musicians, or entrepreneurs. Society may gain a cheap soldier while losing a higher-value worker. A volunteer army looks more expensive in wages, but may attract those whose opportunity cost is lower and who fit military work better.

This shows that less money does not always mean cheaper, and more money does not always mean more expensive. A choice must be judged by what resources it consumes and what resources it releases. Saving cash while wasting more valuable time and ability is not saving cost; it is hiding cost in another form.

Middlemen are also misunderstood. A bottle of water may leave the factory cheaply and sell for much more in a store. People then think middlemen took the profit. But without distribution, consumers would have to find factories, arrange transport, screen quality, store inventory, and bear risk. The store price includes convenience, logistics, screening, and availability.

The same is true for vegetables, fruit, and medicine. Farmgate or factory prices are not the same as consumer prices because channels, inventory, cold chain, information, and risk-bearing are real costs. The right question is not how to remove all middlemen, but whether institutions allow enough channel competition.

For personal decisions, the lesson is to ask what else I paid. Free information may require massive filtering. Cheap services may bring uncertainty. Low-priced products may create repeat purchases. Mature judgment is not always buying the cheapest option. It is choosing after seeing total cost.
