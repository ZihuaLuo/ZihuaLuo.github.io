> What science truly leaves behind is not knowledge points, but the ability to verify and reason.

After reading Letter 044, "Why Study Science?" from Wu Jun's *Silicon Valley Letters 2: The Google Methodology*, my biggest reflection was this: studying science is not about making everyone a scientist, nor about memorizing how much physics, chemistry, or biology. It is about giving us a more reliable method for understanding the world. Many scientific facts may be forgotten after graduation, but if a person learns verification, reasoning, skepticism, and respect for facts through studying science, that ability will influence how they see problems for a long time.

In this letter, Wu Jun says the first purpose of studying science is to master a way of seeing the world and solving problems. Human beings have many ways to understand the world. They can rely on experience, believe authority, or follow parents, teachers, and social customs. But all these methods have limitations: experience may be narrow, authority may be wrong, and tradition may become outdated. What makes science special is that it requires people to approach more reliable conclusions through observation, experiment, and verification. Science does not necessarily claim to possess absolute truth, but it provides a method that can be repeatedly tested and continuously corrected.

This matters in real life as well. Many times, when we encounter problems, we are used to judging by feeling or believing whatever others say. But in an age of information overload, there are too many opinions and a mix of truth and falsehood. Without basic scientific literacy, it is easy to be led by emotion, rumors, and pseudoscience. The letter mentions that science helps us know the boundaries of human knowledge. In other words, it not only tells us what can be believed, but also reminds us what still needs to be questioned. A person who understands science may not know everything, but at least they will ask: where is the evidence? Can this conclusion be verified? Are there other explanations?

Wu Jun also mentions that the second purpose of science is to cultivate basic scientific literacy. Today there are many people who blindly believe and many who blindly doubt. Some people believe everything and are easily deceived by supplements, rumors, and pseudoscience. Others doubt everything, but cannot distinguish reasonable skepticism from random speculation. The meaning of scientific literacy is that it lets people neither believe too easily nor doubt chaotically. Real skepticism is not opposition for the sake of opposition. It is careful judgment built on evidence and logic. A person without scientific literacy may look opinionated, but may actually be controlled by their own bias.

The third purpose is that science helps humanity achieve cumulative progress. Art, literature, and many personal abilities often depend on talent and individual creation, so they are difficult to accumulate steadily from generation to generation. Science, technology, and engineering are different. They can continue to move forward on top of previous achievements. Today's doctors can surpass the best doctors from decades ago precisely because medical knowledge, technology, and experience have accumulated. If an engineer can use methods and tools already verified by previous generations, they can focus energy on new problems instead of reinventing low-level things. This cumulative progress is an important reason human civilization keeps moving forward.

This also makes me think of personal growth. If a person always relies only on inspiration, feeling, and temporary performance, they may occasionally succeed, but it is hard to improve continuously. Truly effective growth should be like science: it needs method, feedback, and correction. If something is done wrong, one must know why it was wrong. If something is done right, one must also know why it was right. Only then can experience become more than scattered memory; it can become reusable ability. The value of scientific training is that it teaches us not to be satisfied with "I feel," but to learn to say, "this is why I judge it this way."

Finally, the letter mentions that studying science can also make a person reasonable. I find this especially important. Being reasonable is not merely being able to debate, nor using grand principles to pressure others. It means being willing to discuss problems on the basis of shared facts and logic. Science itself is a process of reasoning: a question should have real meaning; a judgment should be supported by evidence; a conclusion should withstand verification. A person trained in science may not be right about everything, but at least they are more likely to respect facts, admit boundaries, and revise their view.

The core reflection this letter left me with is that the meaning of studying science is not memorizing many knowledge points, but becoming a clearer, more rational person with better judgment. Knowledge may be forgotten, but method remains. Formulas may not be used, but the spirit of verification remains. Real science education is not training people to take exams. It is helping people have less blind obedience and more judgment in a complex world; less emotional reaction and more evidence awareness; less stubbornness and more courage to revise themselves.
