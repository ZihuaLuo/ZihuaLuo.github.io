> Real competitiveness is not holding everything in your own hands, but creating the most critical value.

After reading Letter 186 from Wu Jun's *Silicon Valley Letters 2*, I realized that many people misunderstand the phrase core technology. They treat it as if a company must own every component and every technical detail to be competitive. But in many modern industries, core competitiveness is not the same as doing everything yourself. It is the ability to create the most important value and organize the best resources around that value.

The letter refers to the well-known bet between Dong Mingzhu and Lei Jun. Gree represented a manufacturing mindset built around control of products, factories, and supply chains. Xiaomi represented an information-age mindset built around users, data, software, ecosystem, and resource integration. This comparison does not mean manufacturing is unimportant. It means the source of competitive advantage has changed.

In the industrial age, controlling production capacity could be decisive. In the information age, customer understanding, product definition, distribution, platform effects, and ecosystem coordination may be more important than owning every part. A company can outsource components and still remain powerful if it controls the user relationship, brand trust, design direction, and data feedback loop. Conversely, a company can own many technologies but still fail if it cannot create value that customers recognize.

This idea also applies to individuals. Many people believe their core competitiveness is a specific skill: coding, writing, finance, design, or analysis. These skills matter, but none of them alone is enough. A person's real competitiveness is the ability to solve important problems, integrate resources, communicate clearly, and keep learning as the environment changes. A skill is a tool. Competitiveness is the system that makes the tool useful.

The risk of worshiping core technology is that it can lead to false security. A person or company may believe that because they hold a certain capability, they cannot be replaced. But markets do not reward ownership for its own sake. They reward value creation. If another organization can provide a better user experience, lower transaction cost, or stronger ecosystem, it may win even without owning every technology.

The lesson for me is that competitiveness should be judged from the outside in. What important problem is being solved? What value is being created? What resources can be coordinated? What part must truly be controlled, and what part can be borrowed, partnered, or outsourced? Real strength is not grasping everything tightly. It is knowing what cannot be lost and building around it.
