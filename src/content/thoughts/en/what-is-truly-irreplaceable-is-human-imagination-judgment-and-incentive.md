> Technology can process existing data, but society's hardest problems often involve discovering new questions, creating new dimensions, and bearing consequences.

The Week 10 Q&A asks whether artificial intelligence and big data can replace human judgment in predicting future supply and demand. At first glance, stronger computing and larger datasets seem to make prediction easier. But economic life is difficult precisely because future demand is often unknown.

Data is strongest when the question is already defined and the pattern is relatively stable. It can find correlations, summarize history, and improve execution. But many important human needs do not exist as clean data before someone imagines them.

Before a new product appears, users may not know they want it. Before a new industry forms, historical data may describe only the old world. Before a new institution is created, there may be no direct precedent. In these moments, imagination and judgment matter more than calculation.

Human beings also carry incentives and responsibility. A model can recommend, but people decide what risks to bear, what tradeoffs to accept, and what values to protect. Prediction without responsibility can become dangerous because it separates decision from consequence.

This does not mean technology is unimportant. Good tools can expand human ability, reveal patterns, and reduce avoidable errors. But they do not remove the need for people who can ask better questions, interpret context, and choose under uncertainty.

For personal development, the lesson is not to compete with machines at mechanical processing. It is to strengthen what remains deeply human: framing problems, understanding incentives, imagining alternatives, and taking responsibility for judgment.

What is truly irreplaceable is not raw information processing. It is the ability to create meaning, choose direction, and bear the cost of choice.
