> Property cannot be violated lightly, but resources should not remain in low-value uses forever because transaction costs are too high.

In Lecture 026, Xue Zhaofeng applies the Coase theorem to land acquisition. The issue is difficult because it involves two important values: private property must be protected, while social development sometimes needs a way through individual negotiation barriers.

If we only emphasize property protection, we can say that wind may enter, rain may enter, but the king may not enter. This matters because power should not casually violate private property. Stable expectations make long-term investment, construction, and operation possible.

But pushed to the extreme, this principle also creates problems. If someone deliberately buys a narrow strip of land to block roads, urban construction, or public works, society may pay a large cost because of one holdout. The issue is transaction cost.

In theory, if all parties could negotiate cheaply, land would move toward higher-value use. In reality, land transactions involve many people, high stakes, complex negotiation, missing owners, refusal to communicate, and strategic price raising. Legal or government intervention can have some justification when transaction costs block value creation.

Justification does not mean unlimited power. Kelo v. City of New London showed how eminent domain can be abused. Government used economic development, jobs, and tax revenue to justify transferring private homes for a corporate project. The homes were destroyed, and the promised project did not materialize. When public use expands without limit, private property can move from ordinary people to more powerful organizations.

That is the central difficulty. Completely banning land acquisition can block public projects; allowing government to define public interest freely weakens property protection. A healthy system needs boundaries: it should prevent holdout behavior, but also prevent governments and strong capital from bypassing individual rights.

Hong Kong's compulsory sale mechanism offers one idea: once a high proportion of owners agree to sell, minority owners may be required to sell under rules and compensation. The point is not whether the exact threshold is perfect, but that the process tries to reduce negotiation costs through clear rules rather than ad hoc power.

Property protection does not mean resources never move. It means movement must be clear, legitimate, and compensated. Without property protection, society lacks security; without reasonable transfer mechanisms, resources cannot enter higher-value uses.
