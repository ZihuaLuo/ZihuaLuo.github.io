> A truly mature investor does not avoid volatility, but understands it.

After reading the supplemental letter "On Global Stock Market Volatility" from Wu Jun's *Silicon Valley Letters 2: The Google Methodology*, my biggest reflection was this: a market decline itself is not frightening. What is truly frightening is not knowing why the decline happened, and not knowing where the risk in one's own assets lies. Many people believe they have strong judgment when stocks are rising, but once the market fluctuates sharply, they are dominated by fear. Mature investing is not predicting every rise and fall. It is distinguishing, inside volatility, which movements are short-term technical corrections and which are real fundamental risks.

In this letter, Wu Jun first points out that the global stock market plunge at the time was more of a technical adjustment than a serious deterioration in economic fundamentals. The U.S. stock market had risen for a long time and had not experienced a meaningful correction for quite a while, so a degree of market adjustment was not surprising. This judgment is important because many people immediately associate a large drop with an economic crisis and even assume the entire market is about to collapse. But if corporate earnings remain decent, economic growth remains stable, and unemployment has not obviously worsened, then a market correction should not be simply equated with a recession.

The letter also mentions that market adjustments often need a trigger, and one important cause this time was a change in monetary policy. Long-term quantitative easing had previously given markets abundant liquidity and pushed up many asset prices. But quantitative easing is a double-edged sword. It can stimulate the economy and the stock market, but it can also lift asset prices. When the economy recovers to a certain stage, the Federal Reserve needs to reduce money supply, raise interest rates, and gradually normalize policy. In other words, a stock market decline does not necessarily mean the economy is getting worse. Sometimes it is simply the market readjusting to changes in the cost of capital.

This made me realize that investing cannot look only at price movements. It must understand the funding environment behind prices. Low interest rates and loose monetary policy lift asset valuations because money is cheaper and investors are willing to pay higher prices for future earnings. But when interest rates rise and capital costs increase, valuations naturally come under pressure. If investors only see that stock prices have fallen without understanding rates, liquidity, and policy changes, they may easily mistake a normal adjustment for a disaster.

The discussion of diversification in the letter is also enlightening. Many people think diversification means putting money into different baskets. But if global financial markets are highly connected, when one basket falls, other baskets may also be affected. In the past, when stocks fell, capital might flow into gold, the U.S. dollar, or government bonds. But this time many assets fell together, showing that as global markets become more interconnected, traditional diversification may not always work completely. Real diversification is not just buying assets with different names. It is understanding whether those assets truly have different sources of risk during a crisis.

The letter also mentions that machine trading may have accelerated the decline. Today, many trades are executed automatically by computers. When some stocks fall enough to trigger preset programs, automatic selling may occur, creating an avalanche-like decline. This reminds me that the risks in modern financial markets come not only from the economy itself, but also from trading structures and market mechanisms. Technology improves trading efficiency, but it can also amplify volatility. When artificial intelligence and algorithmic trading are immature, market emotion may be further amplified by machines, causing sharp short-term fluctuations.

I think the deeper point in this letter is that we should not blindly expect a "national team" to rescue the market. If stock market volatility has not damaged economic fundamentals, governments and central banks may not immediately intervene. A mature market is not one where someone rescues every decline. It is one where investors can bear normal volatility. Treating every decline as a crisis requiring rescue actually shows an immature market psychology. Investors cannot outsource risk management to external forces. They should prepare their own asset allocation and risk tolerance in advance.

This also reminds me of the most common mistake in personal investing: ignoring risk on the way up and overreacting with fear on the way down. Many people begin thinking about risk only after the market has already fallen sharply. But real risk management should happen before buying. Why am I buying this asset? Is its valuation reasonable? If it falls 20 percent, can I still bear it? Will I need this money in the short term? If these questions have not been answered clearly, then once the market fluctuates, investors will easily be pushed around by emotion.

The core reflection this letter left me with is that market volatility is not an exception to investing. It is part of investing. Mature investors should not expect markets to rise forever, nor should they panic at every decline. What truly matters is seeing where risk comes from and understanding the relationship among fundamentals, monetary policy, asset correlation, and market mechanisms. Only then, when facing volatility, can one avoid being driven only by fear and instead treat volatility as a process of reassessing assets, controlling risk, and even finding opportunity.
