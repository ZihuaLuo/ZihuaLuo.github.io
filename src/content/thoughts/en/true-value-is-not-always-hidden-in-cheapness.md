> Good investing is not about whether the price is low, but whether the value is large enough.

After reading Letter 056, "The Secret to Investment Master Bill Miller's Success," from Wu Jun's *Silicon Valley Letters 2: The Google Methodology*, my biggest reflection was this: one of the easiest misunderstandings in investing is mistaking "cheap" for "valuable." Many people think stocks with low PE ratios, falling prices, or poor market sentiment are value investments. But Bill Miller's investment philosophy reminds me that true value is not a low price. It is whether a company has a large enough future space, and whether today's price is worth exchanging for tomorrow's growth.

The letter mentions that the Legg Mason Value Trust managed by Bill Miller once beat the S&P 500 for 15 consecutive years. This is extremely rare, because most funds find it difficult to outperform an index for even a few years in a row. Miller achieved this not because he could predict short-term market moves every time, but because he could identify companies misunderstood by the market yet possessing huge long-term value. He invested in Yahoo, Amazon, and Google not because their financial data looked good at the time, but because he saw the possibility that they represented future business structures.

The letter also mentions Peter Lynch's PEG ratio, which divides PE by growth rate to judge whether the valuation of a growth company is reasonable. This indicator matters because looking at PE alone can easily mislead. A company with low PE is not necessarily cheap, because it may have no growth. A company with high PE is not necessarily expensive, because it may be in a phase of rapid growth. Like Lynch, Miller did not look only at static prices. He understood price inside future growth. What investing really needs to judge is not "is it expensive today?" but "does today's price match future value?"

But Miller's value investing differs from traditional value investing. Traditional value investing often prefers low valuations, stable earnings, and clear cash flow. Miller was more willing to search for what he called companies with "maximum value." Maximum value here does not mean the cheapest on the books, but companies that may create the greatest future impact and return. Amazon, for example, was not profitable in its early days, and its PE was even absurdly high. By traditional value-investing standards it was almost unacceptable. But Miller believed Amazon's real value lay in the future business model it represented. Similarly, Google was not favored by everyone around its IPO, but Miller saw its long-term value as an entry point to the internet.

This made me realize that value investing is not a fixed template, but an ability to judge value. Some people find value in undervalued assets. Some find value in growth speed. Others find value in future market structures. The key is not whether a stock "looks cheap," but whether it is truly worth the money. Something may have a low price but no future, making it a trap. Something may have a high price but enormous long-term value, and may still be worth investing in. The hardest part of investing is distinguishing cheapness from undervaluation.

The letter also mentions that Miller did not buy only one kind of stock. He looked for opportunities across different value systems. He could buy high-PE growth stocks and low-PE value stocks, because what he really cared about was whether those stocks were mispriced by the market. This way of thinking is important. Many investors easily restrict themselves to one style, such as only buying low-valuation stocks or only buying high-growth stocks. But markets change constantly, and truly valuable opportunities may not always appear in the same place. If an investor believes only in one style, it is easy to miss other kinds of opportunities.

However, Miller's investment philosophy also carries risk. The letter mentions that he later stopped participating in stock investing and turned to assets such as Bitcoin. This shows that even top investors cannot adapt to every market environment forever. Investing is not a formula that works eternally, but a judgment inside constant change. A method working in one era does not mean it will always work. An investor's past success does not mean future mistakes will not happen. Therefore, learning from Miller does not mean copying his holdings. It means understanding the thinking behind them: do not look only at surface price; look at the real source of value.

This also reminds me of many choices in life. We often judge things by whether they are expensive or whether they are a "good deal," without seriously asking whether they are worth it. Some things are cheap, but consume time and energy, and ultimately are not worth it. Some choices have high short-term costs but bring long-term growth, and are therefore more valuable. This is true in investing, and also in learning, career, and life choices. What matters is not spending less, but putting limited resources into the places with the greatest long-term value.

The core reflection this letter left me with is that true value investing is not buying bargains, but judging whether value is underestimated. Bill Miller's success tells me that price is only the surface; value is the core. Low valuation is not a guarantee of safety, and high valuation does not necessarily mean a bubble. A truly good investor does not mechanically apply an indicator. They see long-term value that others have not yet understood inside market noise. In the end, investing does not test whether someone can calculate PE or PEG. It tests whether they can judge whether a company truly deserves long-term belief.
