> What creates distance is not only intelligence, but direction, training, and persistence.

After reading Wu Jun's Reader Q&A 07, "Smart People And Slow People," from *Silicon Valley Letters 2: The Google Methodology*, my biggest reflection was this: whether a person can do something well does not depend entirely on whether they are naturally smart. It depends on whether they find the right direction, receive training, and are willing to keep making up for their weaknesses over the long term. Many times, we are too quick to attribute success to talent and failure to not being smart enough, while ignoring that ability itself can be trained.

In this Q&A, a reader mentions being unable to answer Google and Microsoft interview questions and worries whether such questions are judging whether someone has the talent to become an engineer. Wu Jun's answer is direct: talent does not mean whatever a person is born with will remain fixed forever. There may be people in the world with naturally high IQs, but without suitable training, talent may not appear. Historical figures such as Mozart and Beethoven were indeed rare musical geniuses, but they did not suddenly become geniuses one day. They began practicing piano very young and received long-term training. Talent may determine a person's starting point, but training determines whether that talent can truly be expressed.

This view is very helpful. When many people face difficulty, their first reaction is to doubt whether they are suited for it. This is especially true when they see others learn quickly, react quickly, and perform well. It becomes easy to explain the gap as "others are smart, and I am slow." But Wu Jun reminds us that many abilities are not formed in a day or two. If someone did not receive the relevant thinking training in the past, being unable to solve certain problems today does not mean they lack intelligence. It only means they still lack training in that area. A person should not completely deny themselves because they temporarily cannot do something, nor should they assume that another person's current performance is entirely innate.

This also makes me think of our usual misunderstanding of "smartness." We often understand smartness as fast reaction, accurate answers, and easy learning, as if truly smart people solve problems without much effort. But in reality, much of what looks like smartness is fluency formed by long-term training. A person may react quickly because they have seen similar problems before. A person may seem to have a clear approach because they have already trained analytical methods. A person may look relaxed because they have accumulated a lot where others cannot see. If we only see the result and not the training process, it becomes easy to mystify other people's ability and absolutize our own temporary backwardness.

Of course, this does not mean everyone is suitable for the same thing. Wu Jun also mentions that everyone has their own talents. Some people are sensitive to mathematics, some have strong language ability, some are suited to engineering, and some may be more suited to art, writing, media, or sports. Mature self-knowledge is not blindly believing that "as long as I work hard, I can do anything." It is knowing where I have advantages, where I need to improve, and which directions may not fit me. Effort is important, but effort needs direction. If the direction is wrong, the harder I work, the more tired I may become. If the direction is right, even slower progress can still bring me closer to the goal.

The Q&A also contains another important point: intelligence does not equal success, and slowness does not equal failure. Wu Jun is not saying smart people cannot succeed. He is reminding us that without discipline, direction, and systems, intelligence can become arrogance and instability. By contrast, someone who believes they are not smart enough, but is willing to go more slowly and steadily and confirm the direction before continuing to drive, may avoid many detours. The driving metaphor is vivid: if your speed is only 20 percent of someone else's but the direction is correct, you can still arrive later. If the direction is wrong but you drive very fast, you will only move farther away from the target.

This made me realize that the truly frightening thing in life is not being insufficiently smart, but not knowing where I am going. A person who admits they are ordinary will be more willing to accept training, listen to feedback, and accumulate step by step. But a person who overly believes in their own cleverness may skip necessary training, ignore rules and systems, and even move farther in the wrong direction. The advantage of "slow people" is not low intelligence. It is that they more easily remain cautious, use methods to compensate for weaknesses, and are less dragged down by arrogance.

The core reflection this Q&A left me with is this: do not treat "I am not smart enough" as a reason to stop improving, and do not treat "I am very smart" as an excuse to skip training. What determines long-term development is not only talent, but whether the direction is right, whether training continues, and whether the mindset is stable. Everyone has their own starting point, advantages, and limitations. The important thing is not to rush to prove that I am exceptionally talented, but to find a road that suits me and then train, accumulate, and grow stronger on that road.
