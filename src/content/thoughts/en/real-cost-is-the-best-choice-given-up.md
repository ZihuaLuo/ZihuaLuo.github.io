> Cost is not the money already spent, but the largest value given up when choosing one path.

In Lecture 013, Xue Zhaofeng uses a quarry story to define cost. A quarry owner bought an empty piece of land near the quarry. It had fish ponds and trees and could have become housing, tourism, or another business. But after buying it, he left it empty.

At first glance this looks wasteful, especially where land is expensive. But the owner was not only thinking about what the land could become. If someone built housing there, future residents might complain about quarry noise. Even if the quarry might win legally, lawsuits and uncertainty could disrupt business. Buying the land protected stable quarry operations.

The story changes our intuition about cost. Many people think cost is the money spent. In economics, the real cost is the largest value given up. If the land is used to protect the quarry, it cannot be used for housing. If it is used for housing, it may damage the quarry. The better use depends on which creates more value.

Resources have no naturally correct use. Land can become housing, a park, a factory, or a buffer protecting nearby business. Outsiders may easily call one use wasteful or selfish, but resource use is decided by value comparison among alternatives. A choice that seems to do nothing may actually avoid a larger loss.

The quarry owner is not only buying land for himself. He is guessing what future consumers will value. Buyers of stone may build roads, buildings, or museums; the final consumers pay for those structures and services. Entrepreneurs use their own money to judge where resources should go on behalf of future consumers.

This is the role of entrepreneurs. They are not merely resource owners; they make bets under uncertainty about what people will pay for. Profit is the reward for guessing demand correctly. Loss is the signal that resources were allocated wrongly.

The same concept applies to daily life. Before a major exam, a student's time is expensive, so family may excuse all chores. After the exam, taking out trash is normal. The task has not changed; the opportunity cost of time has changed.

Real cost is not always written on a bill. The cost of studying one major is the other major not studied. The cost of taking one job is the career path given up. The cost of entertainment is the learning, rest, or relationship that did not happen. To understand cost is to ask not only what I pay, but what I give up.
