> Truly valuable people are not those with the most information, but those who turn information into consensus, cooperation, and results.

After reading Letter 189 from Wu Jun's *Silicon Valley Letters 2*, I understood more clearly why difficulty itself is often the source of value. Many people dislike complicated coordination, unclear interests, and hard communication. But precisely because these things are difficult, the people who can solve them become valuable.

The letter continues the discussion of common knowledge and public information. Having information is not enough. A useful person must often help different sides recognize the same facts, trust the process, and move toward action. In many situations, the bottleneck is not a lack of data. It is a lack of shared understanding, aligned incentives, and reliable coordination.

This explains why intermediaries continue to exist in many industries. Investment banks, real estate agents, arbitrators, consultants, and advisors are not valuable simply because they pass messages. Their value comes from reducing transaction costs, making interests explicit, building trust, and helping parties reach agreements that would be difficult to reach alone. When the problem is hard enough, coordination becomes a real skill.

The same is true in everyday relationships. Sometimes people help others without clarifying compensation, responsibility, or boundaries. At first this may look generous, but unclear expectations can later create conflict. In some cultures, drinking or social rituals serve as a crude way to create trust. They are imperfect, but they reflect the same underlying problem: people need mechanisms to turn private intentions into dependable cooperation.

Companies also create value by solving hard coordination problems. Google, for example, did not become important merely by possessing information. It organized information, reduced search cost, and made enormous resources usable. The value came from making a difficult problem easier for everyone else.

The lesson for me is that difficulty should not be treated only as resistance. It is often a signal of where value can be created. If a person can clarify confusion, align interests, reduce friction, and produce trustworthy outcomes, they become useful beyond the information they hold. In work and life, the more meaningful question is not "why is this so hard?" It is "what value becomes possible if I can solve this difficulty?"
