> Kindness fits small circles, markets fit large societies; a society cannot run only on goodwill, but needs mechanisms for stable cooperation among strangers.

In Lecture 006 on Adam Smith's view of human nature, Xue Zhaofeng addresses a common misunderstanding. Some people think economics is built on selfishness, so it applies only to selfish people. If people have love and morality, they assume economics no longer applies. This reads Adam Smith too simply.

Smith did not say people are only selfish. In The Theory Of Moral Sentiments, he recognized sympathy: the human ability to feel the pain and happiness of others. A normal person feels sorrow when others suffer and is willing to help family and friends. The problem is that human sympathy is real but limited.

Smith's famous example is uncomfortable but true. A gentleman in London may hear about an earthquake in distant China, feel regret, and express sympathy. But soon he returns to his own life. If he cuts his finger the same day, that small wound may feel more immediate than a distant disaster. This is not pure coldness; it is the natural limit of emotional radius.

Yet our daily life depends constantly on strangers. Food, clothes, houses, phones, and transportation all involve people who do not know us and do not love us. We still receive their service not because they feel sympathy for us, but because markets connect our interests.

That is the meaning of Smith's classic line about dinner not coming from the benevolence of the butcher, brewer, or baker. It sounds cold, but it reveals the value of commerce. Markets allow strangers to serve one another without loving one another, and to cooperate through prices, transactions, and contracts.

Kindness is more reliable in families, friendships, and small communities. But if the moral standards of a family are forced onto an entire society, problems appear. We cannot demand that strangers treat us like parents, or that merchants, workers, drivers, and farmers sacrifice themselves for us. Large societies need sustainable cooperation, not the exhaustion of a few people's goodwill.

The reverse is also true. Market rules should not be brought into every intimate relationship. If families and friends calculate everything like a market, warmth disappears. Small circles can rely more on love and responsibility; the larger world must rely on rules and markets.

A mature view of Smith does not place self-interest and morality in simple opposition. People care about themselves and also care about those near them. Small circles are sustained by affection; the larger world is sustained by market cooperation. Seeing the difference helps us avoid treating business as too cold or kindness as all-powerful.
