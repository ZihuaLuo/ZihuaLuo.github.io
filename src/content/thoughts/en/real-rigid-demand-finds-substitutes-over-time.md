> What looks choice-less in the short term often creates new choices in the long term.

Lecture 035 is about elasticity. People often call something a rigid demand when they feel they have no choice: housing, medicine, education, transportation, or basic services. In the short run, this feeling can be true. But over time, even rigid demand often finds substitutes.

The reason is simple: people react to pressure. When a price rises, they may not immediately leave, switch, or reduce consumption. But if the pressure lasts, they begin to search. They change locations, reduce usage, find alternatives, reorganize habits, or wait for new technology and new suppliers.

This is why short-term and long-term elasticity are different. A commuter may have to pay a higher fare tomorrow because work is still in the same place. But over years, that person may move, change jobs, buy another form of transport, or support new infrastructure. A patient may have to pay for a drug today, but high prices also attract substitutes, generics, insurance design, and new treatment paths.

Calling something rigid demand can therefore be dangerous if it becomes an excuse for ignoring human response. People are not passive containers of need. They compare, adjust, delay, replace, and invent. The more a cost remains high, the more energy people put into escaping it.

For businesses, this means pricing power is rarely absolute. A company may believe customers cannot leave, but if it keeps pushing costs onto them, it teaches them to search for alternatives. A strong product can become vulnerable once users have enough time to change habits.

For personal choices, the lesson is similar. Many situations feel impossible because the current alternatives are bad. But the next useful question is: what would become possible if I had more time, more preparation, or a different arrangement? Elasticity often comes from time.

Rigid demand is not a permanent prison. It is often a short-term condition before people discover or create substitutes. Mature judgment sees both sides: immediate constraint and long-term adjustment.
