> What has already been paid should not hold future decisions hostage.

In Lecture 016, Xue Zhaofeng discusses sunk cost and marginal cost. A sunk cost is a payment already made and impossible to recover. Because it cannot be changed, it should not continue to control future choices. What should matter is how much must be invested from now on and what return that new investment can bring.

The idea sounds simple, but it is hard to practice. If a person buys a movie ticket and realizes after ten minutes that the movie is terrible, the rational choice is to leave. The ticket money is gone either way. Staying only to prove the ticket was not wasted wastes more time. Yet many people stay because they feel not finishing would be a loss.

The same psychology appears in bigger choices. A student continues an unsuitable major because one or two years have already been spent. A person stays in an unsuitable relationship because much time and emotion have been invested. A worker stays in a stagnant job because of past accumulation. The problem is not lack of awareness; it is unwillingness to admit that past investment has sunk.

Sunk cost is dangerous because it lets the past hold the future hostage. Money, time, and emotion already spent cannot be recovered, but people keep investing more to make the past look worthwhile. The result is not recovery of loss, but expansion of loss. The right question is not how much I have already invested, but whether continuing from now is still worth it.

Marginal cost and marginal benefit answer that question. When two plans are available, we should not only ask which has the higher total return from the beginning. We should ask, from the current point, how much additional input is required and how much additional return it produces. A plan that was not ideal at first may be worth completing if most work is already done.

The air conditioner example is intuitive. If one is buying in summer, paying extra for heating and cooling may be a good choice. But if a cooling-only unit has already been bought, the old payment is sunk. Replacing the whole unit in winter may cost much more than buying a small heater. A past mistake does not require a bigger mistake to correct it.

This means ignoring sunk cost does not always mean overturning the past. Sometimes stopping loss is right; sometimes continuing with compromise is right. The key is not whether the past was right, but whether future marginal benefit exceeds future marginal cost.

Many people turn both persistence and quitting into moral labels. If success follows, persistence is praised as grit; if failure follows, it is condemned as stubbornness. What matters is whether conditions have changed. Am I continuing because future returns are worth it, or because I cannot accept that the past was wasted?

Mature choice is responsibility to the future, not proof to the past. Past mistakes have happened. The best response is not pretending they were right or adding more for the sake of pride, but treating them as tuition already paid. Clear-minded people are not those without loss. They are those who do not let losses that have already happened keep deciding the future.
