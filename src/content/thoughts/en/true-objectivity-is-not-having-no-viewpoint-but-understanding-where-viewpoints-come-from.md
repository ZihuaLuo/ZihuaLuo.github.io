> Mature judgment does not eliminate subjectivity; it sees the standards, evidence, and positions behind it.

After reading Letter 092, "On Subjectivity And Objectivity," from Wu Jun's *Silicon Valley Letters 2: The Google Methodology*, my biggest reflection was this: we often assume that "objective" means correct and "subjective" means biased, but reality is not that simple. Many supposedly objective conclusions are actually built on certain subjective standards. Many truly valuable insights come precisely from a person's subjective judgment, shaped by position, experience, and thought. Mature thinking does not completely eliminate subjectivity. It understands where a viewpoint comes from, what it is based on, and where its limits are.

In this letter, Wu Jun first explains the difference between objectivity and subjectivity. Objectivity means observing and judging something while trying not to be influenced by personal feelings, interests, or experience. Subjectivity means looking at things from a personal perspective. On the surface, objectivity seems more reliable and subjectivity seems more dangerous. But Wu Jun reminds us that so-called objective standards are often designed by people. For example, search engine rankings appear to be objective results, but ranking algorithms are designed by humans. Google's PageRank judges quality based on links and votes between web pages. If we accept the validity of this kind of "voting," then it is objective. If we do not accept it, it is only the result of a human-made algorithm.

This example made me realize that what we often see as an "objective result" is actually the result of a certain set of rules running. The rules may look neutral, but why the rules are designed that way already contains human judgment. The same is true for school rankings, company rankings, and project rankings. They may use data, indicators, and models, but which indicators are chosen, how weights are distributed, and which factors are ignored all involve subjective choices. So instead of blindly trusting an "objective ranking," it is better to ask further: what is its standard? What is it trying to prove? Has it left out important factors?

Another point in the letter that impressed me deeply is that many so-called objective descriptions are often completely correct but completely useless. They merely repeat information that will not cause controversy, while providing no real judgment. By contrast, some subjective views may contain bias, but they can provide deeper angles of analysis. For example, column articles in *The Wall Street Journal* may each carry the author's own position, but it is precisely because they have positions that they can make clear judgments. What readers truly need to do is not accept only one person's view, but compare different views and then form their own understanding.

This also made me reflect on my own learning and growth. In the past, I sometimes pursued "standard answers" too much, hoping to find a completely objective judgment that would not be wrong. But in many real-world problems, especially career choices, application planning, investment judgment, relationships, and social observation, there is no single correct answer. If a person only relies on so-called authoritative standards, it is easy to stop thinking. What truly matters is finding logic among different views, judging the weight of different pieces of evidence, and ultimately forming one's own position.

Wu Jun also mentions that subjectivity is actually an important feature that distinguishes humans from animals. Animals rely more on instinct, while humans have value precisely because they have their own consciousness, judgment, and expression. If a person never dares to express a subjective view and only repeats what others consider safe and correct, then they may seem objective, but in reality they may simply not be thinking deeply. Subjectivity is not frightening. What is frightening is subjectivity without evidence. Objectivity is not necessarily superior. What is frightening is laziness wearing the coat of objectivity.

The core reflection this letter left me with is that absolute objectivity does not exist. We should not accept so-called authoritative standards without thinking. True objectivity does not mean having no viewpoint at all. It means understanding different viewpoints and then making a judgment as fairly and justly as possible. We need to listen to subjective opinions, and we also need the courage to express our own subjective views. We cannot blindly trust authority, nor can we stubbornly hold only our own views. We need to respect facts, and we also need to see the interpretive framework behind facts.

In the future, whether I am reading, learning, or making career and life judgments, I hope I will not only ask, "Is this conclusion objective?" I want to ask further: what standard is it based on? Who set that standard? Are there other explanations? Do I truly understand different positions? When I can think with these questions, subjectivity and objectivity will no longer be simple opposites. They will become two tools that help me get closer to the truth.
