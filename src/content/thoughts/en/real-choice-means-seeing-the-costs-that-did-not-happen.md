> Mature judgment sees not only what has happened, but also the possibilities given up by a choice.

Xue Zhaofeng's third lecture, based on Bastiat's distinction between the seen and the unseen, gives a simple but powerful standard for judgment. A bad economist sees only the immediate consequence. A good economist also sees the consequences that did not appear directly.

The broken window story is the classic example. A child breaks a window, the owner pays someone to repair it, and some people say the event created economic activity because the repair worker earned income. But this only looks at what is visible.

What is invisible is the alternative use of the money. If the window had not been broken, the owner might have bought shoes, books, or something else that improved life. After the repair, society only returns to the original state while losing the new value that could have been created.

This mistake appears often. Disasters are said to stimulate GDP through reconstruction. Wars are said to create growth through rebuilding. Inefficient work is defended because it creates jobs. These arguments focus on visible activity while ignoring the better uses of resources that were crowded out.

Unseen costs are dangerous because they do not protest. The industry that never emerged, the ability never built, the time never recovered, and the opportunity never tried do not stand in front of us. Visible results trigger emotion; invisible losses require reasoning.

The same logic applies to personal life. Procrastination gives visible comfort while hiding future pressure. Short videos give visible relaxation while fragmenting attention. A safe but stagnant path gives visible stability while hiding lost growth.

Mature choice means asking not only what I gained, but also what I gave up. Economics does not make people colder. It helps us see choices more completely. The real cost of a decision often lives in what never had the chance to happen.
