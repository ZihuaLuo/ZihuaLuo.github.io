> A good communicator does not tell every detail, but knows how to help others grasp the point.

After reading Letter 192 from Wu Jun's *Silicon Valley Letters 2*, I understood that good expression is not simply making language vivid. It is making complexity understandable while keeping the logic honest. A metaphor is useful only when it clarifies structure. If it creates a false impression, it may become more harmful than a dry explanation.

The letter uses examples such as Bezos's wealth and private planes, the way horses conserve energy, and Gu Hongming's problematic analogy. These examples show that comparisons must be chosen carefully. A good metaphor should preserve the essential relationship of the problem. A bad metaphor may sound memorable, but it shifts attention to the wrong place.

Good expression often requires reasonable extension. Saying that one dollar invested since George Washington's era could become an enormous amount today is not meant to give an exact investment plan. It helps people feel the power of compounding. The value of the expression lies in helping the listener grasp the order of magnitude and the direction of the idea.

At the same time, expression requires knowing which details can be temporarily ignored. If every caveat is included at once, the listener may lose the main thread. If too many details are removed, the statement becomes misleading. The skill is to choose the level of precision that fits the purpose. A teacher, consultant, or writer must constantly balance accuracy and understandability.

The letter also made me think of debates about electric vehicles and pollution. A useful explanation should not merely compare one visible exhaust pipe with another invisible source of power. It should help people understand system-level tradeoffs: where energy comes from, how emissions are measured, what changes over time, and which comparison is fair.

The lesson for me is that good expression is a form of judgment. It is not enough to speak fluently. I need to know what should be simplified, what must remain precise, what analogy fits, and where the analogy stops. The purpose of communication is not to display language, but to help another person understand reality more clearly.
