> Housing prices are only an echo of urban opportunity. The real object of analysis is the opportunity structure behind the price.

The Week 13 Q&A appears to discuss housing prices, but it is really about why cities rise and why they decline. When people talk about housing prices, their first reaction is usually that prices are high, their second is to push them down, and their third is to blame a specific actor. Economic thinking does not rush to take sides. It first asks what the price is reflecting.

Rising housing prices in a city do not necessarily mean the city is failing. Very often they mean opportunities are increasing, people are moving in, and education, health care, business, culture, and professional networks are becoming denser. People are not paying only for concrete and walls. They are paying for hospitals, schools, companies, information, relationships, public services, and future possibilities behind that location.

For that reason, it reverses cause and effect to treat high housing prices simply as a sign that a city has lost competitiveness. High prices do not make a city attractive. A city becomes attractive first, and that attractiveness raises prices. Fever is not the disease itself; it is a signal from the body. The problem is not the number on the thermometer, but the cause behind it. Likewise, the deeper issues are housing supply, land mobility, urban planning, and the binding of public resources to location.

This is why simply suppressing housing prices often fails to solve housing problems. Demand does not disappear when prices are pushed down, while supply may shrink. People will still compete through other channels: household registration, connections, eligibility, queues, school districts, status, and policy loopholes. Once price competition is suppressed, non-price competition appears, and it is often less transparent, less efficient, and less fair.

The essence of a city is dense human cooperation. A city prospers not merely because it has towers, subways, and shopping centers, but because people with different abilities, occupations, and income levels can serve one another. The idea of keeping only high-end people violates the logic of cities. Without couriers, repair workers, cleaners, drivers, and restaurant workers, high-end industries cannot function efficiently. A real city is not a showroom for a single elite group. It is an ecosystem of complex specialization.

This also informs personal choice. Often what a person cannot afford is not only a house, but an opportunity. Young people choose large cities and appear to accept higher rents and living costs. In reality, they are buying denser information, richer possibilities, and a larger growth platform. This choice is not right for everyone, but it cannot be judged only by whether the price is high. The question is whether the price is matched by a higher return in opportunity.

Of course, rising housing prices are not always good. If they come from real increases in city value, they reflect concentration and efficiency. If they come from land supply restrictions, household registration barriers, scarce education and health resources, monetary expansion, or institutional arbitrage, they reflect distortion. The key is not the rise or fall itself, but the force driving the change.

The most valuable training here is a sense of causality. Many social issues look like price issues, but price is only the result. Real economic thinking does not stop at anger when prices are high or satisfaction when prices are low. It keeps asking: why are they high, why are they low, who is demanding, who is supplying, what rules restrict movement, and what resources are wrongly tied together?

Cities rise and fall according to internal laws. A city's price is not decided by slogans. It is jointly determined by opportunity, population, institutions, and resource allocation. Mature judgment does not demand that a city become cheap. It asks whether the city is worth it. It looks beyond housing prices and asks whether the city can continue creating opportunities.
