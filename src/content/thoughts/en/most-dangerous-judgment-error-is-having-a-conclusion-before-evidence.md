> A person is most likely to err not when they lack information, but when they have already been persuaded by their own bias.

After reading Wu Jun's Letter 209 on faulty logic, sports betting, and self-movement, my strongest takeaway was that many wrong judgments do not begin in the argument itself. They begin at the first assumption. Once a person reaches a conclusion first, the rest of the reasoning often becomes an effort to defend it.

The example of Yi Zhongtian being questioned by a student illustrates this problem clearly. The student did not first observe the facts and then reach a judgment. He first assumed that Yi was trying to gain attention, and then interpreted every action through that assumption. Once the conclusion was fixed, evidence became decoration.

This kind of error is dangerous because it can make people feel logical. If someone dislikes a person, they can always find facts that make that person look hypocritical. If someone distrusts an industry, every success can be explained as manipulation. If someone believes a system is unfair, every accident can be treated as proof. The reasoning looks complete, but its direction was decided before the evidence appeared.

Sports betting and match-fixing theories work in the same way. Some people first believe all matches are controlled by gambling interests, so every upset, large score, or accident becomes evidence. But a gambling system does not need to control every match to profit. It often relies on probability, odds, and the structure of the game.

Conspiracy thinking is attractive because it lowers the cost of thinking. Instead of studying probability, incentives, institutions, and structures, it offers one simple hidden cause. It also protects the ego: if I failed, someone suppressed me; if others succeeded, there must have been a hidden deal.

Another risk is being moved by one's own unexamined emotions. A person can be sincere and still be wrong. Mission, justice, anger, and superiority can all make a conclusion feel morally powerful before it becomes intellectually sound.

Mature thinking starts from the opposite question: what evidence would prove me wrong? If I dislike someone, can I still admit where they are right? If I believe an argument, can I seriously examine the strongest facts against it? A judgment that cannot be challenged by any evidence is not a judgment. It is a belief protecting itself.
