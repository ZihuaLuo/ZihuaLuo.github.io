> Long-term wins come not from clever flexibility, but from standing on the side of probability, keeping principles, and avoiding fatal mistakes.

After reading Letter 188 from Wu Jun's *Silicon Valley Letters 2*, I was reminded that long-term competition is often a competition of probability rather than clever prediction. Many investors want to guess the market's next move or infer the intention of leaders and institutions. But this desire to guess often pulls people away from principles and into dangerous games.

In investing, relative performance matters more than being right every day. A person does not need to capture every rise or avoid every fall. They need to avoid decisions that can remove them from the long game: excessive leverage, short selling beyond their capacity, concentrated speculation, and treating the market like a casino. These behaviors may look flexible, but they often put survival at risk.

The letter uses game theory and bookmaker odds to explain why standing on the side of probability matters. A bookmaker does not need to predict every match perfectly. The structure of the odds gives them an advantage over time. Similarly, an ordinary investor should not try to win by guessing every fluctuation. A more rational approach is to build a position where probability, time, and discipline are on one's side.

For most ordinary investors, long-only index investing is closer to this logic. The stock market's long-term gains are often concentrated in a small number of strong days. If one frequently jumps in and out because of guesses, it is easy to miss those days and damage long-term return. Market timing feels active, but it can quietly reduce probability.

The same logic applies to guessing "the will from above." Some people try to infer hidden intentions from every signal and adjust themselves constantly. But in politics, business, and organizations, over-guessing often leads to distorted behavior. More reliable principles are honesty, respect, trust, compliance with rules, and not doing things that create fatal risk.

The lesson for me is that principles are not rigidity. They are a way to reduce catastrophic mistakes in uncertain systems. Do not guess what cannot be known. Do not use cleverness to fight probability. Build decisions on rules that can survive changing moods, changing markets, and changing narratives. In the long run, staying in the game matters more than looking smart for a moment.
