> Boundaries are not merely limits; they are the starting point of rational action.

After reading Letter 006, "Working Within Boundaries," from Wu Jun's *Silicon Valley Letters 2: The Google Methodology*, my biggest reflection was this: many failures do not happen because people are not working hard enough. They happen because people have not clearly seen the boundaries of the problem. We often like to talk about "breaking boundaries," as if progress only comes from breaking limits. But this letter reminded me that a truly mature way of working is not to begin by trying to overthrow boundaries. It is to understand where the boundaries are, and then do the best possible work within them.

This is very different from how we usually understand "limitations." When many people hear the word boundary, they immediately think of constraint, conservatism, or impossibility. But the example in the letter shows that boundaries are not necessarily bad. They can help us find the right direction. Early laser discs had limited capacity, and engineers could improve the disc technology and the precision of the player to increase that capacity, which eventually led to the DVD. But once DVDs ran into the theoretical limit of the red laser wavelength, continuing to improve in the same direction became inefficient, even futile. The real breakthrough did not come from crashing harder into the old boundary, but from switching to a shorter-wavelength blue laser, which eventually produced Blu-ray DVDs. This example made me realize that effort itself is not always valuable. The direction of effort must be based on an understanding of boundaries.

This also reminds me of many similar situations in real life. In career development, for example, some people believe that as long as they work harder and push themselves more, they will definitely get better results. But if an industry has limited room for growth, or if one's ability structure no longer fits the requirements of a new role, simply working overtime and repeating execution may not create real improvement. It is like continuing to squeeze more capacity out of the red-light DVD framework: there may be small short-term gains, but in the long run the ceiling appears quickly. At that point, instead of complaining that opportunities are limited, it is better to re-evaluate the boundaries: where is my ability boundary? Where is the industry boundary? Where is the boundary of my current method? Only after seeing these questions clearly can I know whether to keep optimizing or change paths.

Still, I think the deeper point of the letter is not that we should accept every limitation. It is that we should distinguish between boundaries that can be improved and boundaries that cannot be broken for now. If we treat all boundaries as unchangeable, we become conservative. But if we treat all boundaries as easy to break, we become blind. A truly rational attitude is to acknowledge objective limits in the world while searching for the best solution within them. For example, when designing mobile communication standards, it is impossible to simultaneously achieve the highest transmission rate, lowest cost, largest number of users, and perfect stability, because resources, power, cost, and technology all create boundaries. Mature engineering thinking does not promise a perfect solution. It makes trade-offs among multiple constraints and finds the relatively best answer.

This is also personally useful to me. In the past, when facing study, work, or planning, I sometimes pursued "better" too abstractly without first asking, "better under what conditions?" When doing something, time, resources, and ability are all limited. If I refuse to acknowledge those boundaries and only pursue perfection, I may end up doing nothing well. By contrast, if I define the boundaries first, I can allocate energy more clearly and know what deserves investment, what should be abandoned, and what needs a different method.

The core reflection this letter left me with is this: boundaries are not an excuse for failure. They are the starting point of rational action. Truly capable people are not those who always shout about breaking boundaries. They are those who can accurately judge boundaries, respect them, and make the best choices within them. Many times, maturity does not mean giving up breakthroughs. It means understanding that only after seeing the boundary clearly can a breakthrough avoid becoming blind consumption.
