> When someone has benefited from education, platforms, and social systems, they should not treat them merely as background to personal success.

Wu Jun's supplementary letter about Michael Bloomberg's large donation made me think about what real feedback means. The important point is not only that a wealthy person donated money. It is that a person who benefited from education and institutions chose to help keep those systems alive for others.

Many people like to describe success as purely personal effort. Effort matters, but nobody grows in a vacuum. Education, scholarships, teachers, cities, platforms, rules, and public trust all shape the opportunities a person can receive. When someone succeeds, those systems should not be treated as invisible background.

Bloomberg's donation is meaningful because it points back to the system that once supported him. Good education is expensive not only because of buildings and teachers, but because it gives people a chance to enter a higher-quality environment. If only those who already have resources can access it, social mobility becomes narrower.

Real feedback is different from simple charity. Charity may solve an immediate need. Feedback tries to strengthen the mechanism that helped create opportunity in the first place. It asks how more people can receive training, standards, and platforms that allow them to grow.

This also applies to ordinary people. Giving back does not always mean donating a large amount of money. It can mean mentoring someone, sharing reliable information, protecting a fair rule, contributing to a community, or refusing to damage a system from which we once benefited.

The mature attitude is not to deny personal effort, but to see effort inside a larger structure. A person should be proud of their work, while also remembering the systems that made the work possible. The best return is not only gratitude in words. It is helping the next person stand on the same platform.
