> The most important role of price is not labeling cheap or expensive, but transmitting countless dispersed needs, changes, and judgments.

Lecture 038 turns to Hayek's idea of knowledge in society. The key insight is that knowledge is not concentrated in one place waiting to be collected. Much of what matters is dispersed among countless people in concrete situations.

A farmer knows local soil and weather. A shop owner knows what customers are asking for this week. A worker knows which process wastes time. A buyer knows how urgently they need something. These pieces of knowledge are often temporary, local, and difficult to write into a central report.

Prices allow this scattered knowledge to be used without first gathering it into one command center. When demand rises or supply falls, the price moves. That movement tells people to save, substitute, produce more, search harder, or redirect resources.

The power of price is not that it explains everything. It is that it gives people enough signal to act. A person does not need to know every reason copper became more expensive. The higher price already tells them copper has become more costly to use and may deserve conservation or substitution.

This is why decentralized order can outperform centralized design. It does not require everyone to understand the whole system. Each person only needs to respond to signals relevant to their own decisions, and the system coordinates through those responses.

When prices are suppressed or distorted, society loses this communication channel. People may still act, but they act with weaker signals. Shortages, queues, waste, and hidden competition often appear because the real information has been blocked.

Real effective order does not collect all knowledge into one mind. It builds mechanisms that let dispersed knowledge influence action.
