> The real question is not whether a policy sounds kind, but where it directs human behavior.

Lecture 044 discusses what happens after price controls are imposed. A rule may be designed with good intentions, such as making housing affordable, but economics asks what people will do once that rule changes their incentives.

If rent is capped below the market-clearing level, landlords may reduce maintenance, screen tenants more harshly, ask for side payments, convert units to other uses, or leave the market. Tenants may stay in apartments they no longer need because the controlled price is too attractive to give up.

The policy may therefore protect some people who already obtained housing while making it harder for new renters to enter. The visible benefit is concentrated; the hidden cost is spread across people who never get access.

This pattern appears in many areas. When rules suppress price without addressing scarcity, people find other ways to compete. When rules punish one visible behavior, the behavior may move into another form. When rules ignore incentives, reality pushes back.

Good policy therefore needs humility. It cannot stop at moral intention. It must ask how suppliers, consumers, intermediaries, and gatekeepers will respond. It must also ask what unintended costs will appear and who will carry them.

This does not mean policy should do nothing. It means policy must respect human response. A good rule aligns incentives with the desired result instead of pretending people will behave exactly as the designer hopes.

The value of policy is judged not by how kind it sounds at announcement, but by the behavior and outcomes it creates after people adapt.
