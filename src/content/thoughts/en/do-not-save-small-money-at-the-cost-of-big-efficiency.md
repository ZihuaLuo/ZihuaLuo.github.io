> A person's scope is often revealed not in grand claims, but in how they handle seemingly small choices.

After reading Letter 130, "A Sheet Of Paper Determines Your Math Score And Efficiency In Making Money," from Wu Jun's *Silicon Valley Letters 2: The Google Methodology*, the phrase that stayed with me most was generosity of mind. This does not mean spending money carelessly or enjoying waste. It means knowing what is worth saving and what must never be saved. Many people seem frugal and careful, but they are actually using small cleverness to damage larger goals. They appear to save a little cost, while wasting far more valuable time, opportunity, and attention.

The letter mentions that Zhang Guoxian once invited Wu Jun to eat at one of the most expensive restaurants in Beijing. Wu Jun's first reaction was that the money might have been more useful if given to him directly. Zhang's point, however, was not the meal itself. He wanted Wu Jun to see a broader world and understand what level life and business could reach. A few hundred yuan could solve an immediate problem, but a widened view could change the ceiling of a person's future.

An even more representative example is Zhang's comment when reviewing database software: in the office, never save paper. Wu Jun had tried to put several sales descriptions on one sheet to save paper. It looked efficient and economical, but Zhang saw the real problem. In a business setting, the cost of paper is low, while professional presentation, clear communication, and customer trust are highly valuable. Spending energy on saving paper means treating small things as large and large things as small.

The same logic applies to study. Some students are reluctant to use clean draft paper when solving math or physics problems. They squeeze calculations into half-used sheets, newspapers, or messy margins. On the surface, this looks thrifty. In reality, it interrupts thinking. Difficult problems require clear reasoning, and clean scratch work helps the brain organize logic. One sheet of paper is cheap, but one exam opportunity is expensive.

This kind of small saving and large loss appears often in work and life. Some people buy poor tools to save a little money, then spend far more time correcting mistakes. Some refuse to organize files for a few minutes, then waste hours searching for information. Some try to prove they are capable by doing everything themselves, only to delay the tasks that actually matter. Low efficiency is often not caused by a lack of resources, but by a poor judgment of cost.

A generous person is not someone who ignores details. A generous person knows that details should serve the larger objective. Contracts should be clear, drafts should be clean, communication should be professional, and work should be done properly. These may look like small matters, but they quietly shape a person's standard over time. If someone is used to cutting corners where quality matters, they will eventually lose competitiveness in larger matters too.

So a person's scope is not only shown in grand decisions. It is also shown in one sheet of paper, one meal, and one way of doing work. People who always calculate small costs can easily miss the larger account. What is truly worth cultivating is the ability to place resources where they amplify results and place attention on what actually affects the future.
