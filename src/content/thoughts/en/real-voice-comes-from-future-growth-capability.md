> When judging a country, organization, or person, do not only ask what they have now; ask whether they can keep creating in the future.

Wu Jun's Letter 210 compares the long-term prospects of Russia and India, but the deeper value of the discussion is not the country comparison itself. It offers a method of judgment: do not only look at current stock. Look at future increment.

Russia can easily give people the impression of a great power. It has vast land, nuclear power, natural resources, and historical status. But historical status is not the same as future productivity. If existing assets cannot be turned into new creation, they may slowly become burdens rather than advantages.

India looks much messier at first glance. Poverty, infrastructure gaps, linguistic fragmentation, social complexity, and administrative inefficiency are all real problems. But the key question is whether these problems are gradually being improved through development, education, market participation, and global integration.

This distinction between stock and increment is also useful for individuals. A person's current credentials, resources, and achievements do not completely determine their future. The more important questions are whether they are still learning, whether they can enter competitive environments, whether they can adapt, and whether their problems are shrinking or deepening over time.

Many judgment errors come from worshiping past strength. We see that a person once succeeded and assume they will keep succeeding. We see that an organization once dominated and assume it is only temporarily weak. We see military force or resources and assume voice will naturally follow. But the world rewards continuing creation more than old possession.

The reverse is also true. We should not deny future possibility simply because something is messy today. Growing systems are often not clean or orderly. Young people, companies, and countries can look unstable while still containing real future energy. The question is whether the disorder is the pain of growth or the sign of decay.

The lesson for me is to judge less by static power and more by dynamic trajectory. Real voice does not come only from what has been accumulated. It comes from the ability to keep creating new value.
