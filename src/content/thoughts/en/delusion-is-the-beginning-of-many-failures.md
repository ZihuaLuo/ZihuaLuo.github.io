> Reliable judgment does not pursue impossible perfection; it respects rules, risk, and personal boundaries.

After reading Letter 133 from Wu Jun's *Silicon Valley Letters 2*, I felt that many failures begin not with lack of effort, but with delusion. In investing especially, people often want a result that violates basic rules: high return, low risk, stable cash flow, and principal protection all at once. Desire itself is not wrong, but desire that refuses constraints becomes dangerous.

The letter mentions a Goldman Sachs example in which clients hoped for around 15 percent annual return while also wanting their principal protected. A professional answer should be honest: this cannot be promised. If a product claims to provide high return without meaningful risk, either the risk is being hidden, or the promise is false. Finance does not become safer because people dislike uncertainty.

Many people use exceptional cases to comfort themselves. Buffett succeeded. Apple created enormous returns. Some early investors in great companies became wealthy. These examples are real, but they are not guarantees. They are survivorship stories. If one uses rare outcomes to justify ordinary decisions, one may confuse possibility with probability.

Delusion becomes especially dangerous when it meets leverage. A person who accepts realistic limits may lose money but survive. A person who believes they have found a perfect opportunity may borrow, concentrate, ignore warnings, and refuse to stop. This is how ordinary desire can turn into catastrophic loss. Ponzi schemes and fraudulent products often exploit exactly this psychological weakness.

The same principle applies outside investing. In career, relationships, and life choices, people often want maximum gain with no cost. They want freedom without responsibility, high status without hard tradeoffs, and certainty without discipline. But every real system has rules. Refusing to see them does not remove them. It only delays the moment when the cost arrives.

The lesson for me is to place desire inside rules. Good judgment begins by asking what is possible, what risk must be borne, and what boundary cannot be crossed. A reliable person does not chase impossible perfection. They respect probability, admit uncertainty, and refuse offers that sound too good because they understand that reality always settles the account.
