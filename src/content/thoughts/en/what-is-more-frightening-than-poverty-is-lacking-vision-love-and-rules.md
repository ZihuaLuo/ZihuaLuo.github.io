> What truly changes a person's fate is not only money, but how they see the world, treat people, and do things.

After reading Wu Jun's supplemental letter "What Is More Frightening Than Poverty?" from *Silicon Valley Letters 2: The Google Methodology*, my biggest reflection was this: poverty certainly limits a person's starting point, but what is more frightening than poverty is the lack of vision, lack of love and care, and lack of rule awareness that can form during long-term poverty or scarcity. Material poverty may create temporary difficulty. But if a person lacks vision, they cannot see the larger world. If they lack love, they struggle to build healthy relationships. If they lack rules, they repeatedly hit walls in social cooperation.

In this letter, Wu Jun first answers a practical question: are there definitely fewer people today who rise from poor families than before? His answer is not simply "yes" or "no." Instead, he reminds us to look at the denominator. In the past, the rural population made up a very large proportion of the population. If several people from the countryside entered top universities, people would feel that there were many outstanding students from poor families. Today, the proportion of the rural population has declined, and the proportion of rural students in top universities has also decreased. On the surface, it looks like opportunities for poor families have declined, but behind it are more complex demographic changes. This example makes me realize that many social issues cannot be judged only by intuition or by a single number. We must look at the structure behind the numbers.

Still, the problem of uneven educational resources remains. Children from poor families do not only have less money. From childhood, they may also lack better teachers, broader information, richer reading environments, and wider vision. A lack of money can sometimes be eased through financial aid, but the gap in vision is more hidden and harder to make up. If a child never knows that the world contains more choices, it is difficult for them to prepare for those choices. The truly frightening part of poverty is not only having few immediate resources, but that it may make people believe they can live only in a very small world.

The second question in the letter is also sharp: if someone is poor in childhood, will they be poor for life? Wu Jun does not agree with this claim. He believes many people who were poor as children understand life's difficulty more deeply and have stronger motivation to move upward. Poverty itself is not a virtue, but it may make a person understand earlier that life is not easy and learn to value opportunities. By contrast, if a person grows up with material abundance but lacks goals, responsibility, and self-discipline, they may not truly go far either. Therefore, poverty is not the only factor that determines fate. The key is whether a person can turn experience into upward force.

I agree with this point. A person's background is indeed important, but background is not the end point. What often determines the gap is whether a person receives enough cognition while growing up, whether someone guides them, and whether they form a stable way of doing things. Poverty can make people suffer, but suffering does not automatically create growth. Only when a person learns resilience, restraint, and responsibility from difficult days can the experience of poverty become a kind of wealth. Otherwise, it may become resentment, narrowness, and self-abandonment.

What touches me most in this letter is the discussion of "what is more frightening than poverty." Wu Jun believes that what is more frightening than poverty is lacking vision, lacking love, and lacking rules. Lacking vision prevents a person from understanding the larger world and from judging which opportunities truly matter. If a person has no vision, even when an opportunity appears before them, they may not understand it, seize it, or may even treat a good opportunity as trouble.

Lacking love is equally frightening. The letter mentions that pettiness is actually a sign of lacking love. If a person was not properly cared for as a child, they may find it difficult to learn how to care for others. If they have not experienced stable love, they may also struggle to build stable relationships. Even if their material conditions improve later, their inner world may still remain scarce. A truly rich person is not only someone who owns a lot, but someone willing to share and able to invest money, time, and goodwill in others.

Finally, there is the lack of rules. Rules are not shackles. They are the foundation of social cooperation. A person without rule awareness may gain small advantages in the short term, but in the long run they lose other people's trust. The letter gives the example of an elderly person throwing coins into an aircraft engine. On the surface, this looks like ignorance. Behind it is a lack of awareness of the rules of modern society. If a person does not know what can and cannot be done, even without intending to harm others, they may cause great loss.

This makes me think that real education is not only teaching knowledge, but cultivating vision, love, and rules. The meaning of university is not only getting a diploma. It is allowing a person to encounter a larger world, learn to interact with different people, and understand how society operates. Why do children from poor families try to change their fate through education? It is not because university can immediately make a person rich, but because it can help them walk out of a smaller environment and gain new vision, new relationships, and new ways of behaving.

The core reflection this letter left me with is that the most frightening part of poverty is not only material shortage, but the cognitive, emotional, and rule-related scarcity it may create. To truly escape poverty, a person must not only earn more money. They must expand their vision, learn to love people, and learn to follow rules. Money can improve living conditions, but vision determines how far a person can go, love determines who can walk with them, and rules determine whether they can be trusted by society over the long term. True richness is ultimately not the money in one's pocket, but the breadth and order of one's inner world.
