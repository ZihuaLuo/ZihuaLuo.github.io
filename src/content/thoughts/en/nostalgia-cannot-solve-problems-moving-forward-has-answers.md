> Real optimism is not believing the past was perfect, but believing the future can be improved.

After reading Letter 067, "The Future Will Be Better Than the Past: The Confidence Each of Us Should Have," from Wu Jun's *Silicon Valley Letters 2: The Google Methodology*, my biggest reflection was this: many people become pessimistic not because they have truly seen the future clearly, but because they imagine the past too beautifully. Nostalgia looks gentle and even carries a sense of romance. But if a person always believes "the past was better than today," they can easily lose the courage to face reality and change the future.

In this letter, Wu Jun mentions that nostalgia is common in both Chinese and Western history. Many civilizations imagine some era in the past as a golden age, believing that people were purer then, society was more stable, and life was better. The problem is that this so-called golden age is often only a filter in later people's memories. The past certainly had things worth missing, such as closer relationships between people, a slower pace of life, or a sense of order in certain traditional cultures. But this does not mean the past was better overall than today. The past also had poverty, disease, hunger, war, pollution, and rigid social classes. These sufferings are often softened in nostalgic narratives.

The letter mentions that many people miss life before industrialization and believe the natural environment was better then and people's lives were simpler. But if one truly returned to an agricultural society, ordinary people's lives would not be romantic. Most people had to survive through physical labor, productivity was low, the ability to resist disease and disaster was weak, and there was little freedom to choose careers and lifestyles. Today we complain about work pressure, information overload, and social competition. These problems are real, but they cannot make us deny the enormous progress modern society has brought in medicine, education, transportation, communication, and personal choice.

This makes me realize that pessimism is often related to selective memory. People easily remember the beautiful parts of the past but forget the heavy costs of the past. For example, some people miss the age before smartphones, feeling that people were more focused and more human then. But they may forget that access to information was more difficult, communication costs were higher, and many ordinary people lacked opportunities to express themselves and change their circumstances. Technology certainly brings new problems, but it also brings new freedom. The real question is not whether we should have technology, but whether we can use technology more maturely.

The letter also says that every era has a group of brave, discerning people willing to do things. Pessimists easily see only bad news and feel the world is getting worse. But those who push society forward are often precisely the people unwilling to remain in complaint. Turning points in the world are not completed in one day. They are slowly pushed forward over a long process by people willing to act. Many times, it is easy to complain that the past has been ruined. What is truly difficult is continuing to build inside an imperfect reality.

The deeper point of this letter is a reminder: what has been lost in the past usually cannot simply be recovered. In nature, once a forest has been turned into farmland, even if trees are planted again, it is difficult to restore the original ecosystem. Social change is similar. Once industrialization, urbanization, and digitization have happened, we cannot truly return to the past. Trying to completely go back to an old era is usually unrealistic. A more mature approach is not fantasizing about reversal, but building new order in the new environment and reducing the harm caused by new problems.

This also inspires me personally. When people face pressure, they easily miss the past: student days, a more relaxed stage, or a time before difficult choices were made. But the past often looks beautiful because it has already ended, and we no longer have to carry the anxieties and difficulties of that time. What matters is not returning to the past, but admitting that we are already standing in a new stage and asking ourselves: under today's conditions, how can I still make life better?

The core reflection this letter left me with is that nostalgia can be an emotion, but it cannot become a method for judging the world. The past was not perfect, today is not perfect, and the future will not automatically be perfect either. Real and powerful optimism is not naively believing that everything will get better, but believing that people can make things better little by little through action. We cannot live by missing the past, and we cannot move forward by fearing the future. A truly mature person both sees today's problems and admits today's progress; they neither idealize the past nor give up on the future.
