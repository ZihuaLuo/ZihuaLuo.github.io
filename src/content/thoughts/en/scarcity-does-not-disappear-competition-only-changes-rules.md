> A society never chooses whether to have competition, but what rules people will compete under.

Lecture 041 lists different rules of competition, but its deeper point is simpler: as long as resources are scarce, competition cannot be abolished. Society can only choose the rules under which people compete.

If goods are allocated by price, people compete through willingness and ability to pay. If price is blocked, competition does not disappear. It may move to queuing, relationships, privilege, lottery, violence, seniority, political access, or other standards.

This matters because people often dislike visible competition and imagine that removing it creates fairness. But removing one rule usually creates another. The new rule may be less transparent, less productive, and more dependent on connections.

Every allocation rule rewards certain traits. Exams reward preparation and test ability. Markets reward payment willingness and production ability. Queues reward time. Connections reward social access. Force rewards power. No rule is neutral.

The practical question is therefore: which form of competition directs human effort toward creation rather than waste? A good rule should make people improve products, skills, service, and efficiency. A bad rule makes people spend energy on flattery, waiting, hiding, or fighting.

Scarcity is not a moral failure. It is the condition that makes choice necessary. Pretending scarcity does not exist only hides the real competition from view.

A mature society does not ask whether there should be competition. It asks which rules make competition more open, productive, and less destructive.
