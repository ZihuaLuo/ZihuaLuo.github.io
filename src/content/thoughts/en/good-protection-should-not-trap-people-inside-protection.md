> Effective protection does not arrange everything for people; it preserves choice and mobility.

Lecture 050 discusses affordable housing and the unintended costs of protection. A policy may aim to help people by giving them cheaper housing, but if the design is rigid, it can trap people inside the protection.

When a subsidized home is tied to strict conditions, people may become afraid to move, change jobs, increase income, or relocate for better opportunities. The protection that was meant to reduce pressure can become a new constraint.

This happens because benefits change incentives. If leaving a protected unit means losing a valuable subsidy, a person may stay even when another city, job, or arrangement would be better. The policy protects current housing but reduces future mobility.

Good protection should help people cross difficult periods, not freeze them in place. It should reduce extreme hardship while preserving the ability to choose, work, move, and improve.

This requires design discipline. Support should be transparent, targeted, and flexible enough that people do not have to choose between security and progress. Otherwise, assistance may become a cage with a kind name.

The same principle applies beyond housing. Any support system should ask whether it strengthens people's capacity to act or makes them more dependent on the system's permission.

Good protection does not decide a person's life for them. It gives them enough stability to keep choosing.
