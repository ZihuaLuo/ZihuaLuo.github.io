> As long as resources are limited, selection exists; the real question is not whether distinction can disappear, but who has the right to choose and who bears the consequences.

In Lecture 010, Xue Zhaofeng connects scarcity, choice, distinction, and discrimination. The angle is uncomfortable because discrimination usually means unfairness and harm. But from an economic view, limited resources require choice; choice requires standards; standards create different treatment.

Choosing a movie tonight means not reading at home. Wood used to make pencils cannot simultaneously build a house. A university that admits some students must reject others. We may call this screening, preference, or standard rather than discrimination, but the structure is the same.

Many fairness discussions become confused because people want to eliminate discrimination as if standards can disappear. When resources are limited, standards do not disappear; they become hidden. Not writing a standard down does not mean it has no effect. A seemingly neutral system may simply move selection into a less transparent place.

The Bakke case against the University of California shows the difficulty. The school reserved some seats to support minority and disadvantaged groups. Bakke believed he was also disadvantaged in age and background but did not receive that consideration. The case reached the U.S. Supreme Court.

The complexity is that helping one group necessarily reduces opportunities for another when seats are limited. Supporters emphasize compensation for historical disadvantage; opponents emphasize unified standards. Both sides contain value judgments and both involve different treatment.

The real question is therefore not whether discrimination exists, but who has the right to set standards. A school may value scores, athletics, arts, family background, diversity, or social responsibility. Different standards create different schools and consequences. If a school chooses a standard, it should state it clearly and bear the reputational and quality consequences.

The same is true in personal life. Choosing friends, careers, schools, cities, and even how to spend each day means treating possibilities differently. Mature people do not pretend they have no preferences. They understand their standards and accept the consequences.

Economics does not defend prejudice. It warns against using vague slogans to hide the reality of choice. What should be opposed is not every distinction, but distinction that is unreasonable, irresponsible, opaque, or disconnected from consequences.
