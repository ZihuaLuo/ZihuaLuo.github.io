> Property rights protect a person's boundary from invasion, but they do not guarantee that assets will always remain valuable.

Lecture 056 contains a judgment that is easy to miss but extremely important: property rights protect the physical attributes of an asset, not its economic value. In other words, property institutions protect the rule that others cannot destroy your things. They do not protect the rule that your things must keep making money forever.

This distinction is crucial. Suppose someone runs a teahouse. Property rights mean others cannot smash the tables, block the entrance, steal equipment, or occupy the store. Those are invasions of physical attributes. But if someone opens a coffee shop across the street with better design, lower prices, and better service, attracting customers away and lowering the teahouse's revenue, that is not a property rights violation. The teahouse's economic value fell, but its boundary was not broken.

Many real disputes become confused because people mix up damaged interests with violated rights. An industry hit by new technology, a job replaced by new tools, a license devalued by rule changes, or a store hurt by a new competitor may all involve real pain. But pain does not automatically mean injustice, and loss does not necessarily mean infringement. Market competition constantly revalues resources, making some formerly expensive things cheap and some formerly stable incomes unstable.

If property rights protected not only boundaries but economic value, social progress would be locked by vested interests. Carriage drivers could block cars because cars hurt their business. Telegraph companies could block telephones because telephones lowered the value of telegraphs. Traditional taxis could block ride-hailing because it hurt license values. Physical stores could block e-commerce because it changed customer flows. Traditional media could block internet platforms because they changed advertising revenue. Every new technology could be stopped by old interests in the name of protecting rights.

What truly needs protection is that a person cannot be forcibly deprived of what they legally own, not that their market advantage can never be challenged. A teahouse owner may protect the shop, but may not stop others from opening coffee shops. A taxi driver may protect the vehicle and lawful income from illegal seizure, but may not require consumers always to choose traditional taxis. A firm may protect trade secrets and brands, but may not demand that the market forever reward it under old rules.

This is not indifference toward those who fail. It is sobriety about how society works. Every technological change creates winners and losers. When new efficiency appears, old resource combinations will be repriced. The question is not whether society should care about people being disrupted, but how. Training, transition support, and reemployment face the future. Using administrative or legal power to stop new technology, new models, and new competition sacrifices many people's choices to preserve an old arrangement.

There is a subtle human tendency here. As consumers, people welcome competition. They want cheaper rides, easier shopping, more transparent information, and better service. But when their own industry, asset, or position is challenged by competition, they often hope the market slows down and the rules block change for them. Everyone enjoys the efficiency created when others are pushed by competition, but not everyone accepts that they may also be repriced.

Economics is calm because it does not casually call loss victimization. Victimization means someone crossed a boundary and invaded you. Loss may simply mean the market has newly evaluated your resources. A quiet store is not necessarily treated unfairly by society. A declining occupation is not necessarily proof of institutional injustice. A depreciating asset does not necessarily need the law to restore its old price. Often this is the process by which resources move from lower-value uses to higher-value uses.

This makes security more complicated. People hope property rights will make houses always appreciate, industries always stable, skills always scarce, and careers never replaceable. Property rights cannot promise that. What they can provide is boundary security: others cannot steal your things, smash your store, violate your contract, or forcibly occupy your resources. Whether the market still needs you, whether consumers still choose you, and whether technology replaces you are not promises property rights can make.

A mature market society must do two things at once: firmly protect property boundaries and allow economic value to fluctuate freely. The first gives people basic security. The second gives society continuing vitality. Without property protection, markets decay into predation by the strong. Without value fluctuation, markets harden into a protection net for old interests. A good system does not shield every existing interest from shock. It ensures that shocks occur within clear, stable, and predictable rules.

Property rights are not a permanent profit guarantee. They are a boundary line jointly recognized by society. Others cannot cross the boundary to harm you, but they can do better than you outside it. A society that understands this will neither tolerate infringement nor kill progress in the name of protection.

Property rights protect not the value of the past, but the boundary that lets future competition remain fair.
