> Real effort is not filling time, but using limited energy on what truly matters.

In Letter 124, "Breaking Out Of The Cycle Of Becoming Poorer The Busier You Are," from Wu Jun's *Silicon Valley Letters 2: The Google Methodology*, the issue most worth warning against is false diligence. Many people look extremely hardworking. They arrive early, leave late, and move from one task to another, yet they never truly improve. The problem is not necessarily insufficient diligence. It is diligence without direction. Heaven does not mistreat those who truly work hard, but it also does not pity those who mistake low-value repetition for effort.

The letter mentions a female colleague who always said yes when others asked her for help. As a result, her entire day was filled with miscellaneous tasks, and the work she truly had to finish could only be rushed through overtime at the end. Over time, she became a "model worker" in the office, but her performance was not good. This example shows that if a person cannot distinguish what should and should not be done, they will be led around by other people's needs. Being useful does not mean being a boundaryless nice person. Much busyness is not responsibility; it is a lack of ability to choose.

The second form of inefficiency is believing too much in multitasking. Multitasking sounds like strong ability, but in many cases it only cuts attention into fragments. The human brain can handle limited information at one time. Doing one thing while worrying about another may look like advancing many tasks at once, but in reality every task remains in a low-quality state. Heinrich's law says that behind a serious accident are many minor incidents and hidden risks. This also applies to personal life: repeated small mistakes, small delays, and small disorder eventually accumulate into real loss of control.

The third difficulty is a mindset of aging before growing old. Many young people are not physically weak; their cognition has aged too early. When they encounter content different from their past views, their first reaction is not to understand, but to refute. When facing a new method, they do not try it; they use old experience to prove it does not work. The most dangerous thing is not not knowing. It is feeling too early that one already knows. If a person always uses old logic to explain new problems, it becomes hard to leave the old situation.

The fourth problem is always wanting to find shortcuts other people cannot find. Many people want an easy major with high income and little competition, want to learn English through a quick course, or want results that require long-term accumulation without doing the work. But today's society is already efficient enough that truly low-cost, high-return opportunities are very rare. Language learning is a typical example: switching among American TV shows, the BBC, and Voice of America may look like spending a lot of time, but without stable input and long-term imitation, it only stays on the surface. So-called shortcuts are often another way of taking the long road.

The root of "the poorer you are, the busier you are; the busier you are, the poorer you become" is not busyness itself, but busyness that creates no accumulation. Doing what should not be done, fragmenting attention, rejecting new ideas, and fantasizing about low-cost shortcuts all trap people in a low-return cycle. The more time feels insufficient, the more one should stop and subtract. The more anxious one is, the less one should use more low-value action to cover a directional problem.

Truly effective effort should create accumulation in ability, judgment, and resources, rather than leaving only exhaustion. Before doing something, ask clearly: Is this truly important? Is it part of my core task? Will it create long-term value? Is it only giving me the psychological comfort of feeling busy? If the answer is no, it should be deleted decisively.

To leave this cycle, life does not need a day packed even fuller. It needs unnecessary things removed from the list. Doing less useless work, rejecting false diligence, and calmly improving real ability are what can turn effort from consumption into accumulation.
