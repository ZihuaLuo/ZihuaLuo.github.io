> The hardest part of society is not only scarce resources, but the fact that every person wants different things.

Lecture 037 asks why planned economies struggle with economic calculation. The appeal of planning is easy to understand: if a central mind had enough data, rules, and computing power, perhaps it could arrange the whole economy like a physical system.

But society is not a physics problem. Missiles and planets can be calculated because their goals and relationships are external and measurable. Human beings are different. Each person has preferences, private knowledge, changing circumstances, and subjective valuations that cannot be fully compared from the outside.

The planner faces several impossible tasks. It must know what everyone wants, how strongly they want it, what they are willing to give up, and how those choices will change when conditions change. It must also know whether the rewards and punishments it sets will create real effort, false reporting, or hidden resistance.

Prices solve part of this problem because they compress countless individual judgments into usable signals. A price is not perfect knowledge, but it is a way for decentralized people to express scarcity, preference, and opportunity cost through action.

Without prices, the planner may know quantities but not value. It may count tons of steel, acres of land, and hours of labor, yet still not know which use matters more to people. Producing more of the wrong thing can look like success on a spreadsheet while wasting real resources.

This also explains why one person's intelligence cannot replace social coordination. Even a very smart person cannot know enough about millions of changing situations. The problem is not IQ; it is information that exists only in dispersed, local, and often unspoken form.

Real economic order is not calculated by one large brain. It emerges from many people comparing, choosing, pricing, and adjusting under scarcity.
