> Many customs that look unreasonable may exist because they save transaction costs.

In the seventh-week Q&A, Xue Zhaofeng continues discussing Coase, transaction costs, and institutions. The real world is not a zero-transaction-cost world. As long as people must negotiate, allocate resources, and coordinate action, information asymmetry, communication cost, enforcement cost, and trust cost exist.

The example of Chinese-style street crossing is interesting. From a formal rule perspective, pedestrians crossing when enough people gather looks undisciplined. From a resource-use perspective, it also reflects on-site coordination. When many pedestrians are waiting, the collective time cost of waiting may exceed the cost of cars stopping briefly.

This does not mean the behavior is always right. It means that when formal rules do not flexibly reflect real scenes, people may create customs and tacit coordination to reduce cost. Economics can make us reexamine traditions before dismissing them.

Many customs are not meaningless just because they look old. Queuing, marriage, gift money, titles, neighborhood rules, and industry conventions may all be low-cost arrangements formed through repeated interaction. They may need revision, but they often once solved real coordination problems.

Bride price is one example. It can be excessive or distorted, but historically it also served institutional functions: compensating the bride's family for lost labor and signaling the groom family's resources, sincerity, and ability to mobilize support. In settings with weak welfare and incomplete information, it was not only money but also a signal.

Cemetery relocation shows that property and development have no permanent simple answer. An old cemetery may have existed long before an airport expansion, so the church has reasons to object. But airport expansion may create large public benefits. Legal procedures, compensation, family tracing, and litigation are all costs.

Noise compensation near airports also shows who is truly harmed. If residents lived there before the airport, later noise creates new harm. If the airport existed first and later residents paid lower prices to live nearby, the noise may already have been reflected in housing prices.

Mature rules neither worship tradition blindly nor overturn it lightly. They ask why a rule existed, who bears the cost of changing it, whether expectations have been protected, and whether losses were already priced. Economic thinking moves us from simple good-or-bad judgment to detailed trade-offs.
