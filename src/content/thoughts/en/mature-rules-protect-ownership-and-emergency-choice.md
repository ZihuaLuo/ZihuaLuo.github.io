> The liability rule does not weaken property rights. It finds a second-best protection when transaction costs make negotiation impossible.

Lecture 060 discusses the second method of protecting property: the liability rule. The previous lecture's property rule emphasized that without my consent, you cannot take my thing. This lecture adds that in the real world, not every situation gives people a chance to negotiate in advance. When time, information, and risk cannot be coordinated, society cannot ask only who owns something. It must also ask which action minimizes loss.

This is why the liability rule is more complex than the property rule. The property rule protects voluntary exchange. The liability rule handles situations where voluntary exchange cannot happen in time.

The dock emergency case is a classic example. A person on a lake encounters a storm and ties his boat to another person's dock in order to survive. The dock owner of course owns the dock. Under the property rule, no one may use it without consent. But the storm has arrived. The boat owner has no time to sit down and negotiate a price. If society strictly insists that the dock is sacred and untouchable, the boat may be destroyed and people may be hurt. Temporary use of the dock causes smaller harm. Choosing the lesser loss, society recognizes the boat owner's right of necessity.

This does not deny property. It reinterprets property under special conditions. Property does not exist to freeze resources in the hands of nominal owners. It exists to respect ownership while avoiding larger losses when possible. The dock remains the owner's dock, but in an emergency the boat owner may use it first and compensate later. That is the core of the liability rule: when prior transaction cannot be completed, action may occur first and a third party later determines compensation.

This may look like legal technique, but it reflects a realistic view of the world. People often prefer absolute principles: someone else's thing cannot be touched, rules are rules, rights are rights. The difficulty is that many conflicts in life do not leave enough time for perfect negotiation. Fires, car accidents, storms, medical emergencies, software infringement, and contract breaches are not scenes where people sit at a table and slowly set prices. If only the property rule exists, many losses will be enlarged. If property is ignored entirely, ownership becomes unreliable. The liability rule seeks a necessary but imperfect balance between the two.

The contrast between drug patents and software patents shows the same point. If a pharmaceutical company spends years developing a new medicine and another company copies it, courts often protect the original company through the property rule and prohibit further production. The infringement boundary is relatively clear and the cost of stopping it is controllable. But in a large software system with tens of millions of lines of code, a small portion may allegedly infringe a patent. Courts often will not require the entire company to stop selling, recall the product, or rewrite the system, because the social cost may far exceed the infringement itself. Compensation is more reasonable.

This does not mean software companies have greater rights or patent owners do not matter. It means different situations require different methods of protecting property. When the property rule can be used, the rights holder should decide as much as possible. When it cannot, the liability rule provides a remedy. A mature institution does not use the same rule forever. It knows when to insist and when to substitute.

This also explains why breach compensation often does not pay for an imagined future, but tries to return people to the state before the contract. A stove company delivered stoves to a railway company for transport to an exposition, but the railway delayed and the stoves arrived too late. The plaintiff argued that it missed a huge order. But how large that order would have been was unknowable. The law could not place the plaintiff into the future that might have occurred. It could only return the plaintiff as much as possible to the state before the contract: transport costs, lodging, and other actual expenses.

This may sound imperfect and even disappointing, but institutions often can provide provable fairness rather than imagined completeness. Real losses may be large but impossible to prove precisely. Expectations may be genuine but impossible for law to fully compensate. The liability rule admits this imperfection, and because it admits imperfection, it is closer to reality.

For personal life, the lesson is not only how the law judges, but how to think during conflict. Many disputes are not simply about who is right or wrong. They are about which handling method causes less loss. Principles matter, but if a principle ignores transaction costs completely, it can become rigid. Sympathy for the weak matters, but if sympathy ignores compensation boundaries, it can create new unfairness.

The property rule teaches that we cannot casually take another person's thing. The liability rule teaches that when negotiation is impossible and action is necessary, we still bear responsibility afterward. The first protects order. The second handles accidents. A mature society cannot lack the property rule, or everyone could take first and explain later. It cannot lack the liability rule either, or every emergency would be trapped by absolute ownership.

The key is not to push one principle to the extreme, but to understand the purpose behind rules in different contexts. Property protection is not about preserving formal rigidity. It is about letting resources be used as reasonably as possible during conflict. The value of the liability rule is that it admits the world does not always give time for negotiation, while still refusing to let emergency become an excuse for avoiding cost.

The liability rule is therefore not a betrayal of ownership. It is an admission of real-world complexity. It tells us that civilization includes not only boundaries that say do not touch, but also arrangements that say how to take responsibility after touching. A society is mature not because conflict never occurs, but because after conflict occurs it can still find a way to reduce loss, clarify responsibility, and keep order working.
