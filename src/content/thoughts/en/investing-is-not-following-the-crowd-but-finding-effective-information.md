> Real investment wisdom is maintaining independent judgment inside market noise.

After reading Letter 055, "Peter Lynch's Secret Weapon for Success," from Wu Jun's *Silicon Valley Letters 2: The Google Methodology*, my biggest reflection was this: the hardest part of investing is not finding a company that looks good, but still being able to judge a company's real value independently when everyone else is pulled around by market emotion. Peter Lynch's success was not only because he discovered the PEG ratio. It was also because he understood how to filter effective information, insist on cutting losses, and avoid blind conformity.

The letter mentions that during the 13 years Lynch managed the Magellan Fund, he achieved a 29.2 percent compound annual return, growing the fund from 20 million dollars to 14 billion dollars. This result is astonishing because it was not short-term luck, but the result of long-term compounding. Many investors can earn a lot in one year, but very few can sustain high returns for many years. What made Lynch truly powerful was that he turned investing into a method that could be executed over the long run, rather than relying on one accidental judgment.

The core content of the letter is Lynch's PEG ratio, the relationship between price-to-earnings ratio and growth rate. Before Lynch, many people used PE to judge whether a stock was expensive. But PE itself has limitations. A company with a low PE is not necessarily cheap, because it may have no future growth. A company with a high PE is not necessarily expensive, because its future growth may be very fast. Lynch's contribution was to connect valuation with growth and use PEG to judge whether the price of a growth company was reasonable. This indicator reminds me that investing cannot look only at static price. It must ask whether future growth can support that price.

But the letter also reminds us that any information known to the public quickly loses its advantage in investing. PEG was initially Lynch's secret weapon, but once every fund began using it, it could no longer reliably produce excess return. This is a deep lesson. The market is an arena of information competition. If a method is already known by everyone, it is hard for it to remain an advantage. Truly valuable information is often not the information that is publicly noisy, but the information others have not yet valued, understood, or priced in.

This also explains why Lynch disliked blind herding. He believed the market is not as completely efficient as economists imagine, because most people are not long-term sober investors. They are easily influenced by short-term emotion. When the stock market rises, people become optimistic and feel opportunities are endless. When the market falls, they become fearful and rush to escape. Lynch used the "cocktail party theory" to describe this phenomenon: when no one wants to talk about stocks, opportunity may be appearing; when everyone surrounds investors asking for stock tips, the market may already be overheated. This theory is not a mechanical rule. It reminds investors that crowd emotion is often not a basis for judging value, and may instead be a risk signal.

Another important Lynch principle is cutting losses decisively. After buying the wrong stock, many investors always think, "If I wait a little longer, maybe it will rise back." The result is deeper and deeper losses. Lynch emphasized that if the judgment is wrong, one should admit the mistake in time and move resources to more effective places. This applies not only to investing, but also to many life choices. The hardest thing for people is not starting, but admitting that they chose the wrong direction. Many times we fail not because there was no chance to stop, but because we refuse to accept loss and then keep investing more time and resources into the wrong thing.

The letter also contains an important idea: the most resources should be used in the most effective places. This is an idea from information theory, and it also applies to investing. Lynch spent time studying which companies truly had long-term value and which were only temporary flashes. He did not divide attention equally among all stocks, but concentrated resources on the places most worth studying and most likely to produce return. Investing is not diligently looking at everything. It is judging what deserves deeper attention. When resources are limited, choice itself is ability.

This is very helpful for my understanding of finance. Many people think investing is predicting rises and falls. In reality, investing is more like information filtering and decision discipline. One must know which information is valuable and which is noise; which companies have long-term growth and which are only temporarily popular; when to persist and when to cut losses. Without an independent judgment system, an investor is easily carried away by market emotion, expert opinions, and public discussion.

The core reflection this letter left me with is that good investing is not following the crowd, but maintaining independent judgment inside market noise. Indicators like PEG matter, but understanding the logic behind them matters more. Cutting losses matters, but the courage to admit mistakes matters more. Finding opportunities matters, but putting limited resources into truly effective places matters more. In the end, investing does not test how much information a person knows. It tests whether they can judge which information is truly valuable.
