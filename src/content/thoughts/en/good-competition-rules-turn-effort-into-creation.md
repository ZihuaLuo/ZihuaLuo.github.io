> Competition cannot be eliminated; what matters is whether a rule directs effort toward creating wealth or consuming it.

Lecture 042 compares different competition rules. The key is not whether a rule feels fair in the abstract, because every rule favors some traits over others. The key is where the rule sends people's effort.

Some rules turn effort into creation. If firms compete by improving quality, lowering cost, and serving users better, society gains more goods and better services. If students compete by learning more effectively, knowledge and ability grow.

Other rules turn effort into waste. If people compete by waiting in long lines, cultivating special connections, bribing gatekeepers, or gaming bureaucracy, the effort may be intense, but it does not create much new value.

This is why competition itself is not the enemy. The same human desire to win can produce innovation or corruption depending on the rules. A society should care less about whether people are competing and more about what competition asks them to do.

Good rules are transparent enough that people know how to improve their chances, open enough that new participants can enter, and productive enough that individual effort also increases total value. Bad rules make success depend on hidden access, arbitrary favor, or pure endurance.

For personal life, the same logic applies. Choose arenas where effort accumulates into real capability. Avoid games where winning requires spending more energy on signaling, pleasing, or manipulating than on creating value.

Good competition rules do not make everyone equal. They make effort more likely to become creation rather than consumption.
