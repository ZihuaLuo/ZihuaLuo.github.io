> Price is not a number set by one person, but the result that grows out of countless concrete transactions.

Lecture 039 emphasizes that price is not an abstract average imposed from outside. It forms through concrete transactions between buyers and sellers under specific conditions.

People often ask what a fair price should be as if there were one correct number independent of the transaction. But real prices are shaped by urgency, alternatives, information, timing, location, trust, and expectations. Different buyers and sellers can reasonably arrive at different prices because their situations differ.

This does not mean any price is automatically good. It means price must be understood as a process. A transaction price carries information about the two sides' alternatives and judgments at that moment. Many such transactions together form the broader market price.

If we replace this process with an abstract average, we may lose the information that makes allocation possible. A single controlled price can look fair on paper but fail to reflect urgency, scarcity, quality differences, and changing demand.

The question of dividing the pie also affects how large the pie becomes. If a pricing rule discourages production, investment, and exchange, the apparent fairness of the rule may shrink total value. If a rule allows people to respond to real signals, it can encourage more creation.

For everyday judgment, this means we should be cautious about simple statements such as "this should only cost X." The better question is: under what conditions did this price form, who has alternatives, and what behavior will this pricing rule encourage next?

Real price order is not an arithmetic answer. It is a social process grown from countless specific choices.
