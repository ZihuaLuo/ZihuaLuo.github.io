> Effort without purpose can easily leave only form behind.

After reading Letter 037, "Do We Really Understand Why We Go To College?" from Wu Jun's *Silicon Valley Letters 2: The Google Methodology*, my biggest reflection was this: many people treat going to college as something obvious, but rarely truly ask why they want to go. We often assume that going to college leads to a good job, and that attending a famous school leads to a good future. But this letter reminds me that if the purpose is unclear, even the best form can become an empty shell.

In the letter, Wu Jun first tells two ironic stories. One is that Britain long kept three sentry positions at Dover, only to later discover that those guards had originally been placed there to defend against Napoleon's invasion. The threat of war had long disappeared, but the positions were mechanically preserved for more than a hundred years. The other story is from the time of Wu Zetian: two palace maids stood guard every day at a corner of the Luoyang palace, and only later was it discovered that the original reason was simply that the paint had not dried and someone needed to remind officials not to brush against the wall. These stories look absurd, but they reveal a common problem: many things begin with a reasonable purpose, but over time the purpose is forgotten and only the form remains.

This makes me think of college. For many students and parents, going to college seems to have become a fixed path: other people's children go to college, so I should too; everyone pursues famous schools, so I should too; society says degrees matter, so I cannot fall behind. But the problem is that college itself is not the purpose. It is a method for achieving some goal. If a person does not know what they want to gain from college and only follows because everyone else does, college can easily become a form. They may spend several years studying without truly building ability or finding direction.

The letter mentions that in some countries, people understand college more clearly. If someone wants to become a doctor, lawyer, or engineer, college is of course a necessary path. But if someone wants to learn cooking, gardening, winemaking, or restaurant management, a vocational school may be more suitable than a comprehensive university. This comparison made me realize that the value of education is not how advanced it appears, but whether it fits a person's goal. Not everyone must walk the same road, and not every career requires the same kind of education. A truly rational choice is not blindly pursuing a higher platform, but first understanding where one wants to go.

This letter is especially useful for students today. Many people begin studying hard in middle school with the sole goal of "getting into a good university." But after they actually enter university, they may not know why they are studying or how their major relates to the future. College then becomes a new stage of confusion: grades need to be earned, internships need to be found, activities need to be joined, but there is no clear line connecting all these things. If the purpose is unclear, effort easily becomes busyness. If the direction is unclear, a prestigious school cannot automatically solve life problems.

Of course, I am not denying the importance of college. College can still provide knowledge, perspective, networks, training, and opportunities. The problem is that we cannot mythologize college, as if receiving a diploma automatically improves life. The true value of college is not to arrange the future for someone, but to help a person develop judgment, learning ability, and self-positioning. If someone enters college with a clear goal and knows what abilities they want to train, what directions they want to explore, and what resources they want to connect with, college can become an excellent platform. But if they only drift with the crowd, even the best platform can be wasted.

The core reflection this letter left me with is that before making any important choice, we should first ask clearly what the purpose is. Going to college is not about proving that I have kept up with the majority, nor about satisfying external expectations. It is about helping myself move toward a clearer life direction. The real question is not "should I go to college?" It is "why am I going to college, and what kind of person do I hope to become through college?" If this question has no answer, effort easily loses meaning. If this question is clear enough, college can truly become the starting point of growth.
