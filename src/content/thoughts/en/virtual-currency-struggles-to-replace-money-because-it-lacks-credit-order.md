> Money can be transformed by technology, but it cannot detach from stability, circulation, and regulation.

After reading Letter 084, "Can Virtual Currency Replace Existing Currency?" from Wu Jun's *Silicon Valley Letters 2: The Google Methodology*, my biggest reflection was this: whether virtual currency can replace existing currency looks like a technical question on the surface, but it is actually a question of credit, efficiency, and governance. When many people talk about Bitcoin, blockchain, and cryptocurrency, they are easily attracted by ideas such as "decentralization," "immutability," and "freedom from government control," as if sufficiently advanced technology could naturally replace traditional money. But this letter reminds me that money becomes money not merely because it can be recorded, but because it can stably perform exchange, accounting, circulation, and macroeconomic regulation.

The previous letter explained that the essence of money is being a medium of exchange and a tool of accounting. Following that logic, virtual currency does have some monetary functions. It can be used to record value, and it can complete transfers and payments within specific groups of people. From a technical perspective, cryptocurrency is even more transparent than traditional ledgers in some ways, because transaction records can be publicly verified and are not easily altered at will. This is exactly why many people believe it represents the future, or even believe that state-issued money will eventually be replaced by it.

But Wu Jun points out that the real question is this: for something to become a common currency for society, we cannot only ask whether it can "keep accounts." We must also ask whether it can serve the entire economic system with sufficiently low cost, sufficiently high speed, and sufficiently stable value. For example, as a transaction medium, Bitcoin's cost and time are difficult to compare with cash, bank cards, and mobile payment. When we buy a cup of coffee, payment usually takes only a few seconds. A Bitcoin transaction may take longer and consume more computing power and energy. If the payment tool itself is more expensive than the object being traded, it is very hard for it to become true everyday circulating money.

More importantly, money cannot fluctuate violently. As a unit of account, money must be relatively stable. If a merchant receives payment in one currency today and that currency suddenly depreciates by 10 percent tomorrow, merchants, workers, exporters, and ordinary consumers all face huge uncertainty. Bitcoin struggles to become a settlement currency not because it has no price, but because its price is too unstable. If a country's currency fluctuated 10 percent in one day, the entire economy would fall into confusion, not to mention international trade, wage payments, and long-term contracts. Therefore, the biggest problem with virtual currency is not whether it has value, but whether it can serve as a stable measure of value.

This also made me re-understand the role of central banks. Many people criticize central banks for being able to issue more money, believing this causes inflation and currency depreciation. But from another perspective, the meaning of a central bank is precisely that, during economic crises, deflation, unemployment, or financial panic, it can adjust the economy through monetary policy. Money is not only a transaction tool. It is also an important instrument for a country to govern its economy. After the 2008 financial crisis, the United States and other countries were able to ease the crisis through interest rate cuts, quantitative easing, and other measures precisely because they had monetary policy tools. If the whole world used only one cryptocurrency that could not be adjusted, it would be difficult to provide systemic rescue once the economy ran into trouble.

Of course, this does not mean virtual currency has no meaning. On the contrary, it raises a very important question: is the traditional financial system efficient, transparent, and fair enough? Blockchain technology receives attention because it may improve efficiency in clearing, cross-border payments, contract execution, digital identity, rights allocation, and other areas. In other words, virtual currency may not replace fiat currency, but the technology behind it may change certain links in the financial system. What is truly valuable is not "issuing a coin," but using technology to solve old problems of high transaction costs, difficult credit allocation, and opaque information.

This also explains why Wu Jun remains cautious about ICOs. Many so-called cryptocurrency projects do not issue coins because a currency is necessary to solve a problem. They borrow the form of a "coin" to raise money. If a project can be completed without issuing an ICO, then issuing an ICO is likely only a way to bypass regulation, raise money quickly, or create speculative heat. This kind of virtual currency does not create new production relationships or improve real efficiency. It merely packages a financial story. Financial innovation can easily become a bubble when it detaches from real demand.

From the perspective of financial thinking, this letter gave me this insight: technology can reshape financial tools, but it cannot cancel financial laws. Whether it is paper currency, a bank account, an Alipay balance, or Bitcoin, all of them must answer the same question in essence: why do people trust it? If a currency has no stable value, insufficient liquidity, no governance mechanism, and no broad credit foundation, then no matter how advanced it is, it is hard for it to become real money. The most central thing in the financial world has always been trust, not form.

Therefore, I believe virtual currency will find it very difficult to replace existing money in the short term. It may become an asset, a technical experiment, or even a payment tool in certain scenarios. But to become the mainstream money used jointly by nations and the global economy, it still lacks too many conditions. Money is not a pure technical product. It is a collection of social institutions, economic order, and public credit. The future of finance will indeed become more digital, but digitalization does not equal decentralization, and it certainly does not mean having no regulation.

This letter helped me understand that to judge a financial innovation, we cannot only ask whether it sounds advanced. We must ask whether it truly solves problems of cost, efficiency, risk, and trust. The part of virtual currency that is truly worth attention is not necessarily whether it can replace the U.S. dollar, the renminbi, or other fiat currencies. It is whether it can push the financial system to become more transparent, more efficient, and more fair. Technology can challenge an old order, but if it cannot establish a new credit order, it can only remain in speculation and imagination.
