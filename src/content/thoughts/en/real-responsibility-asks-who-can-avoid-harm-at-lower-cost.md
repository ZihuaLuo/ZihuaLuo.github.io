> The key to social conflict is not only identifying right and wrong, but finding the lowest-cost solution.

In Lectures 021 and 022, Xue Zhaofeng discusses Coase's problem of social cost. The cases look at first like one party harming another: cattle eat wheat, a tall hotel blocks sunlight from a pool, train sparks burn flax, a candy maker's noise disturbs a doctor, and a chicken farm's odor bothers residents.

Coase's strength is turning the question around. Many harms are not one-sided. Two sides are competing for the same scarce resource. Cattle and wheat compete for land; hotels compete for sunlight; the doctor and candy maker compete for quiet; the chicken farm and residents compete for air. The question becomes who should use the resource and which arrangement minimizes total loss.

The slogan that exercising one's rights must not harm others sounds fair, but real cases make it ambiguous. A pool hotel can say a nearby building harms its business; the other hotel can say being prevented from building harms its land-use right. Both sides can use the same sentence.

The train spark example shows Coase's method. If train sparks burn flax stacked beside the rail, intuition says the railway should pay. But many parties can prevent the damage. The railway can redesign trains, build walls, or reroute; the farmer can place flax farther away. The key question is who can avoid the harm at lower cost.

If redesigning trains is extremely expensive while moving flax a few meters is cheap, it may be socially more efficient for the farmer to bear more avoidance responsibility. Responsibility should not be assigned only by asking where the spark came from, but by asking how damage can be reduced at the lowest total cost.

The chicken farm case is similar. If the farm existed first and the city later expanded toward it, simply calling the farm the polluter misses the point. The farm had an operating right, but city development may also create value. If residents or the city want to remove the farm's pollution right, they should bear the relocation cost.

This is not coldness. It is clarity. Social problems cannot be solved only by asking who looks weaker or who looks like the harmer. Good rules assign responsibility to the party that can reduce harm at lower cost and allow resources to move toward higher-value use.

In personal life, many conflicts also come from competing uses of the same space, time, or attention. The mature question is not only who made me uncomfortable, but who can adjust at lower cost and what arrangement minimizes loss for both sides.
