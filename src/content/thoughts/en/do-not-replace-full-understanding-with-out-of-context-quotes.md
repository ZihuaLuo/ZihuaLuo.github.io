> Many sayings mislead people not because they are necessarily wrong, but because people keep only the half that serves them.

In Letter 114, "On Context And Logic Through Two Common Sayings," from Wu Jun's *Silicon Valley Letters 2: The Google Methodology*, what is most worth discussing is not the two sayings themselves, but how people use them. Many popular phrases, proverbs, and so-called famous quotes originally have specific contexts and boundaries. But after they spread for a long time, their premises are often cut away, leaving only the part most easily used. Then a sentence no longer helps people understand the world. It becomes a tool for self-justification.

"There are no eternal friends or eternal enemies, only eternal interests" is such an example. Many people understand it as naked utilitarianism, as if relationships between people or countries contain only interests and no principles. But Palmerston's original statement was not encouraging individuals to betray morality for profit. In the parliamentary context, he emphasized that national leaders have a responsibility to protect national interests. Once the context of national responsibility is removed and only "only interests" remains, a neutral political judgment is transformed into an excuse for cold calculation.

This kind of quoting out of context spreads easily because it caters to human laziness. To understand a sentence fully, one needs to check the source, look at the background, and distinguish the object. To misuse a sentence, one only needs it to sound reasonable. Especially when a sentence can excuse one's own choice, people are more willing to believe the simplified version. Complex historical figures are compressed into one label, and specific institutional responsibilities are turned into universal rules of human nature.

"The customer is God" is the same. Businesses should respect customers and ensure service quality, but that does not mean customers stand above rules. A flight delay may come from weather or air traffic control, and flight attendants or counter staff cannot change objective conditions. If passengers use the phrase "the customer is God" to vent emotions at staff, they solve nothing and damage public order. A truly civilized commercial society does not turn customers into gods. It lets businesses and customers each take responsibility within rules.

The example of the elderly woman jaywalking in Vancouver makes the importance of rules even clearer. She thought that sitting on the ground crying and shouting about being bullied as an elderly person would transfer responsibility away from her. But the dashcam record, police judgment, and medical bills all pointed to the same fact: whoever violates the rule bears the consequence. Many times, real insight is not only knowing what rights one has, but also knowing what boundaries one must obey.

This letter is really about understanding and rule consciousness. If a person habitually extracts only the half-sentence that benefits them, they will find it hard to truly understand others, society, and the world. If a person habitually replaces logic with slogans, they can package their lack of rules as righteousness. Insight is not knowing more famous sayings. It is knowing under what conditions a sentence holds, and where it cannot be used casually.

Therefore, when facing any statement that sounds reasonable, we cannot only ask whether it sounds agreeable. We must ask about its source, context, and range of application. Once language leaves context, it can change from wisdom into misdirection. Once reasoning serves only one's own interests, it can change from principle into excuse. Mature judgment is not memorizing more sayings, but having the patience and ability to understand a sentence completely.
