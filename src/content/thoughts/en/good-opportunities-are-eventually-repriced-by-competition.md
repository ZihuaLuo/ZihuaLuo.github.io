> There are no permanently easy benefits; competition turns extra returns into new costs.

In the fifth-week Q&A, one central idea stands out: many opportunities that look stable and easy stop being easy once more people see them. Competition rushes in and turns extra returns into entry barriers, effort costs, and higher resource prices.

This explains why being an emperor may not be a great job. The emperor appears to hold the highest power, but maintaining power has enormous cost: preventing coups, managing threats, preserving authority, and defending legitimacy. Many attractive positions are hard not because they are hard to enter, but because they are hard to keep.

Civil service jobs, elite schools, and hot industries work the same way. Once a role is seen as stable, respectable, and well rewarded, competition increases. Preparation time, credentials, exam pressure, and opportunity cost rise until much of the extra benefit is converted into cost.

This also shows why there is no fixed reasonable profit rate. Some people say an iPhone or luxury bag should not be expensive because its production cost is not that high. But price is not a reward for labor embedded in the object. It is the result of demand, scarcity, and substitution.

A celebrity's clothing, a historic video, or a concert ticket may contain little additional labor, yet sell for a high price. The reason is strong demand and limited substitutes. If we judge value only by how hard something was to produce, we misunderstand markets.

Rent can exist when payment is weakly tied to service quality. If income does not change whether a job is done well or badly, people may slack off. But over time, competition often converts even apparent rents into new costs. Reputation, repeat purchases, and future opportunities all depend on continued effort.

For personal development, the lesson is clear. Many people hope to find an easy, stable, high-return path, but good paths rarely remain costless if they can be copied. If an opportunity is truly good and easy, others will enter; if others enter, returns are competed away.

What is worth pursuing is not a static advantage that looks safe forever, but the ability to remain needed. Static benefits are repriced by competition. People who keep creating value are harder for new costs to swallow.
