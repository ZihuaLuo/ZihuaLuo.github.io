> Listening to advice is not about who speaks loudest, but who can truly reduce uncertainty.

After reading Letter 193 from Wu Jun's *Silicon Valley Letters 2*, I gained a clearer standard for judging advice. Good advice does not simply add one more opinion. It narrows the range of wrong choices. A person who gives useful advice helps us remove paths that look attractive but are unlikely to work under the current conditions.

The Monty Hall problem is a good example. Many people's intuition gives the wrong answer, but once the information structure becomes clear, the right choice changes. Advice works in a similar way. A useful advisor does not decide for us by force. They help us see information we missed, update probabilities, and understand which options should no longer be treated as equal.

The letter also mentions Frederick Jelinek and IBM. Real experience matters because successful decisions usually depend on conditions. Someone who only sees the final result may imitate the surface action without understanding why it worked. Advice from people without relevant experience can become noise, even when it is well-intentioned. It may increase confidence without reducing uncertainty.

This does not mean only famous experts are worth listening to. It means we should ask what kind of uncertainty this person can reduce. Have they faced a similar problem? Do they understand the constraints? Can they explain the conditions under which their advice applies? Can they point out what would make their recommendation fail? These questions help separate knowledge from opinion.

Good advice is also not always comfortable. Sometimes it removes a choice we hoped would work. Sometimes it reveals that a decision is more constrained than we imagined. But that is exactly its value. A narrower set of realistic options is better than a wide set of illusions.

The lesson for me is to listen more carefully, but also more selectively. I should not be moved by volume, status, or certainty alone. I should seek advice from people who can reduce ambiguity, compare it across different experienced perspectives, and update my decision when new information appears. The purpose of advice is not to make choices feel easier, but to make them more accurate.
