> Many opportunities are missed not because they do not exist, but because no one says the real thought out loud.

After reading Letter 187 from Wu Jun's *Silicon Valley Letters 2*, I became more aware of the difference between private understanding and public consensus. Many things are privately understood by many people, but because nobody says them clearly, they never become shared knowledge. As a result, people continue to act as if they do not know what they actually know.

The story of "The Emperor's New Clothes" is a classic example. Almost everyone may have seen the truth, but the truth had no social force until someone said it out loud. Once it was spoken publicly, private perception became common knowledge. This shift matters because action often depends not only on what I know, but on what I know that others also know.

Financial markets provide many similar examples. Before the subprime mortgage crisis, many people had doubts. In speculative markets such as cryptocurrency or Ponzi-like schemes, many participants may sense that something is wrong. But if the shared language never forms, individuals hesitate to act. Silence allows fragile systems to continue because each person assumes others may still believe.

This is not only about markets. In relationships, workplaces, and negotiations, people often avoid saying what matters. A person may be dissatisfied with salary, boundaries, expectations, or cooperation, but chooses indirect hints instead of direct communication. The result is that both sides remain trapped in private guesses. What could have become a solvable issue becomes resentment or missed opportunity.

Of course, saying things out loud does not mean speaking without judgment. A lawyer must know professional boundaries. A manager must choose timing and method. A person should not turn every private feeling into public accusation. The point is different: when something affects cooperation, trust, and future action, it must eventually become discussable.

The lesson I take from this letter is that clear expression creates reality. Needs, boundaries, risks, and assumptions do not become useful merely because they exist in one's mind. They become useful when they are stated in a way that others can understand and respond to. Many opportunities are not missed because people lack information, but because they fail to turn information into consensus.
