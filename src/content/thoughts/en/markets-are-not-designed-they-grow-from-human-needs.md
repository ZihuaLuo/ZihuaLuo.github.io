> Markets are not institutions designed by force, but orders that grow from people's behavior under real constraints.

After studying Xue Zhaofeng's first economics lecture on the economic organization of prisoner-of-war camps, I realized that a market is not a decoration that appears only in prosperous societies. It grows wherever resources are scarce, preferences differ, and people have room to exchange.

A prisoner-of-war camp is not a normal commercial society. There are no companies, no shopping streets, and no formal free-market institution. Supplies mainly arrive through packages. Yet even there, exchange appears because people value the same goods differently.

The story of the chaplain trading cheese and cigarettes shows why exchange creates value. He did not produce new physical goods. He discovered differences in preference and moved goods toward people who valued them more. After several trades, more people were better off.

This is why trade is often underestimated. People assume only production creates wealth, while trade merely moves things around. But the same object has different value in different hands. Exchange raises value by improving allocation.

Prices then emerge from repeated exchange. Bread becomes more expensive before the next supply delivery because demand rises. Cigarettes can become money because they are widely accepted and easy to carry. Even expectations about future supply can change prices.

The camp also had criticism of trade, middlemen, and price fluctuations. These criticisms resemble real life. People notice that middlemen earn a spread, but often ignore the information, matching, and risk-bearing they provide.

The lesson is that markets are not slogans. They are mechanisms that help people coordinate under scarcity. Understanding markets is not about becoming colder. It is about seeing how human needs, differences, and cooperation create order.
