> Value is not an attribute objects carry by themselves, but what people are willing to give up for them.

In Lecture 029, Xue Zhaofeng enters the law of demand through subjective value theory. Before understanding demand, we must understand personal valuation. The value of an object to a person is not determined by how much labor or material it contains, but by what that person is willing to give up for it.

This pulls value away from objects and back to concrete people. Diamonds, photographs, signatures, antiques, and sports cars are physical objects. What makes them expensive is that someone is willing to pay for them. Without preferences, memories, desires, and choices, objects remain objects.

An old family photo may be worthless to a stranger but priceless to the family in it because it carries memory, relationship, and time. A celebrity photo may take little more labor to produce than an ordinary one, yet sell for much more because people pay for fame, scarcity, and symbolism.

Subjective value theory explains more than objective value theories. A thing is not expensive simply because it contains more labor or technique. Teresa Teng's singing created enormous value not because her physical vocal cords were fundamentally more costly, but because listeners wanted to hear her and were willing to pay for her work.

This matters for careers and products. A person cannot say I worked hard, therefore I must be valuable. A product cannot say we invested heavily, therefore it should sell. Markets ultimately look at whether users are willing to pay for the result. Effort matters only when it becomes something others need.

Many projects fail because creators fall into the illusion of effort. Having spent time, they feel the output should be recognized. Having suffered, they feel the market owes them reward. But the world does not price suffering. Work nobody needs remains self-movement no matter how polished it is.

Personal valuation is subjective, but it is not invisible. Words are weaker than actions. If someone values a book, see whether they buy it and read it. If someone values a relationship, see whether they invest time and energy. Choice reveals preference because choice carries cost.

The practical reminder is direct: do not ask only how much I have paid. Ask why others would need me. Value is proven in exchange and choice, not declared by the producer.
