> A Spring Festival train ticket tests whether we can admit that when price cannot adjust scarcity, scarcity adjusts people in other ways.

Lecture 045 discusses Spring Festival train tickets, a topic that easily triggers emotion because it is tied to going home. But the economic problem remains: when demand greatly exceeds supply, someone must decide who gets the ticket.

If ticket prices are held low, more people want tickets than there are seats. The shortage does not disappear. It turns into other costs: staying up to fight for tickets, using connections, paying scalpers, wasting time, changing travel plans, or accepting lower-quality alternatives.

A low price can look fair because the number printed on the ticket is friendly. But the full cost may be higher once time, anxiety, uncertainty, and unequal access are counted. People with better information, faster tools, more flexible schedules, or stronger connections may gain an advantage.

This does not mean raising prices is emotionally easy. The point is that suppressing price does not eliminate scarcity. It only changes the form of payment from money to something else. Sometimes that something else is less fair and more wasteful.

The hard question is how to help people who truly need support without destroying the signal that scarcity exists. Direct subsidies, transparent assistance, or increased supply may be better than pretending a scarce good is not scarce.

For personal thinking, this is a reminder to count all costs, not only visible prices. A cheap ticket that requires ten hours of stress and uncertain success may not be truly cheap. A policy that lowers the displayed price may raise the real cost elsewhere.

Suppressed prices often return as more expensive hidden costs. Scarcity always finds a way to be paid.
