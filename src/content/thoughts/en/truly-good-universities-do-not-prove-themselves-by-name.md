> A university's value is not its ranking, but whether it shapes a person's future ability, vision, and spirit.

After reading Letter 158 from Wu Jun's *Silicon Valley Letters 2*, I felt that many people's understanding of a good university remains too attached to labels. Ivy League, 985, HYPMS, world ranking, admission difficulty: these labels are not meaningless, but they can easily replace real judgment. A truly good university should not need to prove itself only through its name.

Rankings are useful references, but they are limited. They often measure research output, reputation, funding, selectivity, or alumni signals. These indicators can reflect part of a university's strength, but they do not fully answer the question that matters most to an individual student: will this place help me become a better thinker, a stronger learner, and a person with broader future possibilities?

Wu Jun's letter reminds me that a good university is not just a place that issues diplomas. It should be a center where future talent gathers, research is created, new ideas are tested, and young people find a temporary home for growth. In this sense, the university's environment matters as much as its classroom. The people one meets, the standards one absorbs, and the questions one learns to ask may shape a person more deeply than any single course.

This perspective also changes how we should choose schools. It is not enough to ask which name sounds better. We should ask whether the school has the academic atmosphere, peer group, resources, city context, and value system that fit one's direction. A famous school that does not fit may become a decorative label. A less famous but more suitable school may become a real platform.

The deeper point is that education should not be reduced to brand consumption. If a student enters a university only to collect a name, they may miss the part of education that actually changes people. The best universities give students not only knowledge, but also courage to explore, standards for judgment, and the habit of pursuing meaningful problems.

So when thinking about universities, I should care less about whether the name can impress others and more about whether the environment can shape me. A truly good university proves itself through the people it cultivates, the ideas it protects, and the future it opens.
