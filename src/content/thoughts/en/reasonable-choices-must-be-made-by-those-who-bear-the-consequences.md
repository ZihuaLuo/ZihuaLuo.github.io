> Different treatment is unavoidable; what matters is whether the standard is reasonable and who bears the consequences.

In Lecture 012, Xue Zhaofeng continues the discussion of discrimination. As long as resources are limited, people must choose. Choice requires standards, and standards produce different treatment. The real issue is who sets the standard and who bears its consequences.

The first example is the commercial network of Southeast Asian Chinese families. In the rubber business, some families judged credit by closeness: direct relatives, distant relatives, same surname, same village, same dialect, other Chinese, and then non-Chinese. On the surface this looks narrow, but in an environment with language barriers, weak legal protection, and high judicial costs, kinship and reputation became substitute credit systems.

This kind of distinction is not always prejudice; it can be risk management. When formal institutions cannot protect transactions well, people naturally prefer partners with stronger reputational constraints. The closer the relationship, the higher the cost of default; the farther the relationship, the less information and the greater the risk.

This shows that many social phenomena cannot be judged only by saying fair or unfair. The more reliable formal rules are, the more people can trade with strangers. The less reliable formal rules are, the more people retreat into acquaintance networks. A familiar society may not be warmer; it may simply lack trust mechanisms among strangers.

The second example is mortgage standards before the U.S. subprime crisis. Banks originally distinguished borrowers by income, credit, and repayment ability. Under pressure to help disadvantaged groups, standards were relaxed and part of the risk was shifted to institutions with government backing. Banks found that loose screening did not necessarily leave them with the final loss, so they expanded risky loans.

The warning is not that weak groups should never be helped. It is that risk judgment should not be removed from those who bear the consequences. If banks bear default losses, they judge repayment ability carefully. If risk is transferred, the incentive for caution declines. A policy may hope to expand homeownership, yet harm the very people least able to bear loss.

When outside forces change selection standards while escaping consequences, new problems emerge. School admission, hiring, lending, and charity all allocate scarce resources. If the standard setter does not bear consequences, surface fairness may replace real responsibility.

In personal life, standards are also unavoidable. Choosing reliable project partners, judging whether to lend money, and checking past records are not coldness; they are responsibility for consequences. Mature fairness does not treat everyone identically. It uses reasonable standards under clear responsibility.
