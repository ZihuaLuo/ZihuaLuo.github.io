> The real career ceiling is not a lack of effort, but an outdated way of thinking.

After reading the first letter in Wu Jun's *Silicon Valley Letters 2: The Google Methodology*, my strongest takeaway was this: what people often call a career ceiling is not only caused by insufficient ability or insufficient effort. It is often caused by using an old way of thinking to face problems that belong to a new era.

In the past, we often believed that as long as someone was hardworking enough and experienced enough, they would keep moving upward in their career. But this letter raises a sharper question: if a person's way of thinking has already fallen behind, then more effort may simply mean repeating the same work in the same place.

This is especially true in the information age, where the logic of how society operates has changed. Data, systems, algorithms, and scalable thinking are reshaping every industry. If someone still focuses only on the small tasks immediately in front of them, without understanding trends, structures, and complex systems, they can easily run into what feels like a career ceiling.

## What Computational Thinking Reminds Us

The concept of "computational thinking" in the letter was especially helpful to me, because it is not simply about how fast computers are. It is a reminder that computers represent a cognitive mode very different from human intuition.

Human beings are not naturally sensitive to large numbers or complex change. The letter gives a vivid example from George Gamow's *One Two Three... Infinity*: two tribal chiefs compete to see who can name the larger number. One chief says "three." The other thinks for a long time and finally says, "You win."

From today's perspective, this example seems almost funny, because three is such a small number. But for those chiefs, their living environment meant they rarely needed to process quantities larger than three. They were not unintelligent; their life experience had limited their understanding of scale.

That kind of limitation has not completely disappeared. People today can say "one million" or "one hundred million," but truly understanding the scale behind those numbers is still difficult. The letter mentions that people who make presentations often prefer charts instead of long strings of numbers precisely because humans are not very sensitive to raw numbers. We often need visualization to understand differences more clearly.

Another example is Wang Jianlin's famous line: "First set a small goal, such as earning one hundred million." Many people found it absurd because the scale of ordinary life cannot easily contain the idea of one hundred million. For most people, that number feels almost like infinity, rather than a target that can be calculated, broken down, and planned.

## From Intuition To Decomposition

The emergence of computers changed this cognitive limitation.

When the first electronic computer was born in 1946, it could perform only 5,000 calculations per second. By today's standards, that is far weaker than a phone. But it already showed a completely new way of thinking: when facing complex problems that humans cannot process quickly, a computer can use calculation, decomposition, and simulation to find answers.

The letter mentions that scientists used computers to calculate the trajectory of long-range artillery shells. Before the shell had even landed, the computer had already calculated its path. This shows that a computer does not judge by feeling. It transforms a complex problem into something analyzable and predictable through large-scale computation.

Therefore, the core of computational thinking is not "knowing how to use a computer." It is learning to understand the world through decomposition, quantification, modeling, and systems analysis. Human beings are easily limited by intuition and experience. We often treat problems we cannot see clearly as "too complex" or "impossible to solve." Computational thinking asks the opposite questions: can this problem be broken into smaller parts? Can it be described with data? Can we find a pattern? Can we build a model to predict what may happen?

That is where its real importance lies. It reminds us that modern society does not only require more effort. It requires a more advanced way of thinking.

## Not Becoming A Machine, But Updating Cognition

At the same time, I do not think computational thinking means that people should become machines.

What matters is not blindly worshiping technology, but understanding the way of thinking behind it. If a person only learns how to use a few pieces of software or a few tools, but never develops structured thinking, they may still be left behind by the era. By contrast, if a person can use technology to reinterpret industries, organizations, and personal development, they may be able to break through their original limits.

The reflection this letter left me with is this: career development should not only ask, "Am I working hard enough?" It should also ask, "Is my way of thinking suitable for this era?"

Effort determines how fast a person can move. Cognition determines how far a person can go. In the future, the gap may not be created by who works the hardest, but by who can understand the changes of the era earlier and use a new way of thinking to redefine their own growth.
