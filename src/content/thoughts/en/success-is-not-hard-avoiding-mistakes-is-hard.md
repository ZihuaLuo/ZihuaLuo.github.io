> Life is not about winning every moment, but about not exiting early because of short-sightedness, anxiety, and repeated mistakes.

After reading Wu Jun's follow-up letter on why success is not difficult, my strongest impression was that many people understand success in the wrong direction. They imagine success as a spectacular leap, a rare opportunity, or a brilliant decision. But Wu Jun's point is more restrained and more useful: in a long life, the hardest thing is often not creating a miracle, but avoiding repeated mistakes that remove a person from the game too early.

Life is closer to a marathon than a sprint. In a sprint, one explosive moment can decide the result. In a marathon, stable pace, low error rate, and the ability to keep going are more important. Many people do not fall behind because they are not talented enough. They fall behind because they are too anxious, too short-sighted, or too eager to prove themselves. They make choices that feel clever in the moment but narrow their future options.

The letter uses housing anxiety in cities such as Beijing and Shanghai as an example. Some people measure the future only by today's housing price and conclude that they have no chance. But this is a static view. A young person's most important asset is not current cash, but future contribution. If someone can become a valuable creator in the top quarter of their field, their income and choices will not be frozen at today's level. The real question is not whether today's housing price is frightening, but whether one's future value is growing.

This does not mean optimism solves everything. It means pessimism based on a static snapshot is often misleading. If a person hoards resources, fears every loss, avoids every difficult problem, and only thinks about immediate security, they may protect the present while sacrificing the future. Many mistakes in life come from overreacting to temporary pressure and making decisions that permanently reduce long-term possibility.

Success, in this sense, is not mysterious. Choose a direction with real value, keep improving, avoid fatal mistakes, and do not destroy the compounding effect of time. The gap between people is often widened not by one perfect decision, but by years of different error rates. Some people keep their energy available for important problems. Others spend it repairing the damage caused by impulse, vanity, fear, and short-term gains.

The lesson I take from this letter is that the pursuit of success should begin with respect for risk and time. Do not always ask how to win faster. Ask first what mistakes must not be made. Do not let anxiety determine strategy. Do not let temporary pressure define the whole future. If a person can keep learning, keep contributing, and keep themselves in the long game, success becomes much less mystical.
