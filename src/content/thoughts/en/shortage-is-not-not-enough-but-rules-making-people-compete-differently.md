> Scarcity is normal, but shortage and surplus often come from distorted prices that force people into other forms of competition.

Lecture 043 distinguishes scarcity, shortage, and surplus. Scarcity is the normal condition of human life: good things are limited and people must choose. Shortage and surplus are different. They often appear when prices are pushed below or above the level at which people are willing to trade.

When a price is held too low, demand rises and supply falls. The result is shortage. But the deeper issue is not merely that there are not enough goods. It is that people must now compete through other methods: waiting, relationships, privilege, favoritism, bundled purchases, or black markets.

When a price is held too high, supply may exceed demand and surplus appears. Producers may keep making what people do not want at that price, while resources are stuck in the wrong place.

Price is a rationing mechanism. If price is not allowed to ration, something else will ration. The new mechanism may look more humane at first, but it can become less fair because it is often hidden and harder to challenge.

This is why shortage should not be explained only by greed or insufficient supply. Sometimes supply is discouraged because the controlled price makes production unattractive. Sometimes demand is artificially expanded because the price no longer tells people the real cost.

For policy and personal judgment, the question is not only whether something feels too expensive. It is what happens if the price is prevented from reflecting scarcity. Who will get the good then? Who will wait, who will use connections, and who will quietly be excluded?

Shortage is not just "not enough." It is often a sign that rules have forced competition to move away from price into less visible channels.
