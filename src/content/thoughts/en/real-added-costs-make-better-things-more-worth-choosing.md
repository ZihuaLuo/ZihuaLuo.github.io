> The higher the added cost, the more worthwhile it becomes to transport, publish, or preserve higher-value things.

Lecture 037 discusses the third law of demand: when an added cost is attached to a good, the better version of that good becomes relatively more attractive. Shipping, travel, time, regulation, and entry barriers all change what is worth choosing.

If two bottles of wine differ greatly in quality but both require the same high shipping cost, the shipping cost takes up a smaller share of the high-quality bottle's total value. This makes the better bottle more likely to be shipped. The same logic applies to books, performances, education, food, and many other goods.

This explains why distant markets often receive better goods. If transport is expensive, low-value goods may not be worth moving, while high-value goods still are. If publication is costly, weak writing is less likely to survive the process, while stronger work can justify the expense.

Added cost acts like a filter. It does not merely make everything more expensive; it changes the composition of what passes through. When a hurdle rises, mediocre things are squeezed out first, because they cannot justify the extra burden.

This also applies to personal choices. If attending a meeting takes two hours of travel, the meeting must be more valuable. If a relationship requires high emotional cost, it must create more real value. If a project requires years of effort, it cannot survive on shallow interest alone.

A useful question is: after adding all hidden costs, is this still worth choosing? Many things look attractive only before the cost of time, attention, risk, and coordination is counted.

Real added costs make quality more important. When the road is long, only things with enough value deserve to be carried to the end.
