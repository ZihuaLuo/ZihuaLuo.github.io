> Whether an organization can grow does not depend on whether the leader is always strongest, but whether they can bring in stronger people.

After reading Letter 203 from Wu Jun's *Silicon Valley Letters 2*, I was reminded of Ogilvy's law about hiring people larger than oneself. The image of nested dolls is very direct: if every manager hires people smaller than themselves, the organization becomes smaller and smaller. If every manager dares to hire people stronger than themselves, the organization becomes larger and stronger.

This is easy to agree with in principle, but difficult to practice. Many managers want talented people, yet feel threatened when someone is too capable. They fear losing authority, being compared, or becoming less important. But this insecurity limits the organization. A leader who must always be the smartest person in the room can only build a room smaller than themselves.

The letter mentions examples from Google and Silicon Valley, including the recommendation of strong people such as Kai-Fu Lee. Great organizations often develop mechanisms to attract, evaluate, and retain people who raise the average standard. This is not only a hiring technique. It is a cultural filter. If strong people feel there is no room to grow, they leave. If weak managers block strong people, the organization gradually loses future capacity.

This principle also applies to personal growth. The people around us quietly determine our standards. If I only stay among people who make me feel superior, I may protect my confidence but lose the chance to grow. Entering an environment with stronger people can be uncomfortable, but it expands the reference system. It forces me to see what better thinking, better execution, and better discipline look like.

The real test of a manager is not whether they can control people weaker than themselves. It is whether they can create a system where stronger people are willing to join, cooperate, and contribute. This requires broad-mindedness, clear incentives, trust, and the ability to judge talent without jealousy.

The lesson for me is that insecurity and vision lead to different choices. Insecurity asks, "will this person threaten me?" Vision asks, "will this person help the organization become better?" A true manager should not be afraid of people stronger than themselves. They should be afraid of building a team where nobody is.
