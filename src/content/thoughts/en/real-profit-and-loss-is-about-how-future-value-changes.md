> Profit and loss truly change the future cost of using a resource.

In Lecture 017, Xue Zhaofeng offers a different way to understand profit and loss. Profit and loss are not only records of how much was earned or lost in the past. They show whether people's judgment of a resource's future value has changed. Once a resource is revalued, the future cost of using it changes too.

The bowl example is clear. Someone buys an ordinary bowl for ten yuan and later discovers it was used by the Qianlong Emperor and is worth one hundred thousand yuan. Profit happens at the moment its true value is discovered. From that point on, the bowl is no longer merely a dinner bowl; it is a collectible, tradable, displayable resource.

If the owner keeps using it for meals, the cost is no longer ten yuan but one hundred thousand yuan, because the alternative forgone is selling it or putting it into a museum. Profit raises the future cost of using a resource because the resource has been shown to have higher value.

Loss works the same way in reverse. If someone buys what they believe is an antique bowl for one hundred thousand yuan and later learns it is worth ten yuan, the loss occurs when the truth is discovered. From then on, using it for meals costs ten yuan, not one hundred thousand. The money already lost has sunk.

This matters because people are easily trapped by past prices. If something was bought expensively, they feel they must treat it as precious. If it was bought cheaply, they feel free to waste it. But past purchase price does not necessarily determine present cost. Present cost depends on what value the resource can still create now.

The same logic applies to personal assets. Skills, experiences, relationships, and assets are constantly revalued by changing environments. A once ordinary skill may become scarce; an once admired experience may lose value as an industry changes. The important question is not what I once invested, but what this resource is worth now and what it can still create.

The hard part of profit and loss is attribution. A coffee shop may earn money because of location, beans, service, brand, or timing. If the cause is misread, the wrong people are rewarded and the wrong resources receive investment. A loss also needs diagnosis. Mature review asks which resource rose in value, which was overvalued, and what new information should change future action.
