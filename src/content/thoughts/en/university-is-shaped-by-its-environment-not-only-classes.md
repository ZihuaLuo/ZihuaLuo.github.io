> Good university education is not only about teaching knowledge and granting diplomas; it helps young people become more complete through shared life, conversation, and exploration.

After reading Letter 093, "Oxford And Cambridge's Distinctive Education And Admissions System," from Wu Jun's *Silicon Valley Letters 2: The Google Methodology*, my biggest reflection was this: the meaning of university goes far beyond classes, exams, credits, and diplomas. A truly good university education places young people with curiosity, potential, and different backgrounds together, lets them influence one another through study, life, conversation, and cooperation, and eventually shapes them into people who are more mature, more capable of judgment, and more prepared to carry social responsibility.

In the letter, Wu Jun cites John Henry Newman's view in *The Idea Of A University*. Newman argued that if he had to choose between two kinds of universities, one managed by teachers where students graduate after earning enough credits, and another without professors or exams but where young people live together and learn from one another for three or four years, he would choose the latter without hesitation. That view is striking because it names the most essential value of university: education is not simply pouring knowledge into students, but helping them gain thought, perspective, judgment, and character through shared life.

This also helped me understand the college system at Oxford and Cambridge in a new way. The point of the college system is not only residential management, and it is not merely an old ritual. It creates a community where students, and students and professors, can communicate over a long period of time. In that community, students learn not only course content, but also how to get along with others, express views, judge right and wrong, and take responsibility. In other words, a college is not only a place to live; it is an environment for growth.

The letter notes that Newman opposed an overly utilitarian and narrowly technical view of higher education. He believed universities should transmit broader ways of understanding, rather than only train narrow skills. This made me think of how people often judge universities today by asking direct questions: Is this major good for finding a job? Is this school highly ranked? Will this diploma bring higher income? These questions are realistic and cannot be ignored, but if university is reduced to only these goals, it becomes career training rather than education in the fuller sense.

The tutorial system at Oxford and Cambridge also moved me. Wu Jun says that Cambridge tutors do not merely help students sign course forms. They guide students to read, do experiments, write reports, and grow according to each student's characteristics. That kind of tutor relationship is very different from ordinary teaching administration. It is closer to long-term accompaniment and formation: the tutor cares not only about what students study, but also whether they can think, research, and become people with independent judgment.

Newton's example is representative. At Cambridge, his tutor was the Greek professor Benjamin Pulleyn, but he spent much of his time studying with the mathematics professor Isaac Barrow. This shows that a truly good education system does not lock students into a narrow track. It gives students freedom and helps them find the direction that genuinely fits them. What is most precious about university is exactly this combination of institutional support and enough room for exploration.

The letter also mentions that Cambridge has only 24 weeks of concentrated classes each year, while the rest of the time requires students to do experiments, research, major assignments, and exam preparation under tutorial guidance. This made me realize that university learning is not "however much teachers teach, students learn." It is "the school provides an environment, and students decide how much they actively use it." If students understand university only as classes and exams, they may miss many of the most important learning opportunities. Abilities such as research, expression, cooperation, judgment, and self-management are not directly taught in class; they slowly form through long-term practice.

This also made me reflect on my own university experience and future planning. In the past, I sometimes paid more attention to school reputation, program ranking, course design, and employment outcomes. But this letter reminds me that when choosing a school or program, the more important question is whether it can provide an environment that truly helps people grow. Are there excellent classmates who can stimulate one another? Are there teachers willing to offer long-term guidance? Are there opportunities to do research, discuss problems, and join real projects? Is there an atmosphere that encourages people to study not only for grades, but in order to become better people?

So the core reflection this letter gave me is that what truly matters in university is not only what courses it teaches, but what kind of environment it creates. Classroom knowledge matters, but the deeper education is learning to live with excellent people, to speak and listen, to judge independently, and to take responsibility. A diploma can only prove that someone went through a period of study. A real university education should change how a person sees the world, does things, and relates to others.

In the future, whether I am looking back at my undergraduate experience or planning graduate applications, I hope I will not look only at rankings and outcomes. I also want to look at the educational philosophy behind a school. A good university is not a diploma machine. It is a community that shapes people. The education worth pursuing is not one that only teaches a technique, but one that makes people more complete in knowledge, perspective, character, and responsibility.
