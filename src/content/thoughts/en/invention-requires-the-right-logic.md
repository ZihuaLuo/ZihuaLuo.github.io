> Real innovation is not copying the surface, but understanding the principle behind it.

After reading Letter 029, "The Logic Behind Invention: Flight," from Wu Jun's *Silicon Valley Letters 2: The Google Methodology*, my biggest reflection was this: many important human advances did not happen because people found the correct answer from the beginning. They happened because people kept trying, correcting, and re-understanding until they slowly approached the real principle of the thing. The invention of flight is a good example. Humans admired birds very early and hoped to fly into the sky like birds. But the earliest efforts mostly stayed at imitating bird wings, assuming that as long as a similar wing device was made, flight could be achieved.

The letter mentions that in early research on flight, humans took a long detour. Leonardo da Vinci studied bird flight, and later the Italian scientist Borelli analyzed the relationship among birds' muscles, bones, and flight from the perspective of biomechanics. He eventually proved that humans could not fly by imitating birds flapping their wings. This conclusion looks like a denial, but it was very important, because it told people that a wrong direction cannot truly succeed no matter how much effort is spent. Many times, proving that a path does not work is itself progress.

But the human problem is that even after theory has shown certain methods to be impossible, people may still keep trying. This makes me think of a point in the letter: many actions that violate theory come from people's excessive trust in intuition. This is true in flight and in many other things. We often think, "it looks like it should work," "others have done it this way," or "with more effort it will succeed," without truly analyzing the conditions and principles behind it. The result may be generation after generation repeating the same failure.

The person who truly pushed flight forward was Sir George Cayley. What made him valuable was not only that he dared to try, but that after realizing a mistake, he could admit it and search again for the correct direction. He did not continue insisting on imitating birds flapping their wings. Instead, he proposed that a bird's wings provide not only power but also lift, and that human aircraft should separate lift and propulsion. This understanding changed the direction of flight research and laid the foundation for later aircraft invention.

This letter made me realize that imitation is not shameful. What is truly frightening is imitating only the surface without understanding the principle. Early humans imitating birds was the beginning of learning. But if imitation stays forever at the surface, it can never become real invention. This is true not only for technology, but also for personal learning, work, and growth. When we see others succeed, we cannot only copy their actions, methods, and results. We should ask: why did they do it this way? Under what conditions does this method apply? What is the logic behind it? If the environment changes, does the method still hold?

The core reflection this letter left me with is that real innovation is not blind imagination or blind effort. It is understanding the logic behind things. Mistakes are not frightening; what is frightening is insisting on mistakes to protect one's pride. Imitation is not frightening; what is frightening is learning only the form and not the principle. If a person can admit problems in failure, understand mechanisms through imitation, and continuously correct direction between theory and practice, then they may truly find their own path.
