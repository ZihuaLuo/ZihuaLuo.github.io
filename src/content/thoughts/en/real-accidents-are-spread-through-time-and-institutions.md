> People are not passive receivers of shocks; given time, they find substitutes, transfer risk, and rearrange costs.

The Week 9 Q&A develops a deeper point behind demand: shocks rarely stay where they first land. Taxes, disasters, sanctions, risks, and price changes move through choices, contracts, insurance, markets, and institutions.

At first glance, an accident appears to hurt one clear party. A storm damages a house, a policy raises a cost, a supplier fails, or a tax is imposed. But once people have time to respond, the cost begins to spread. Prices change, contracts are renegotiated, insurance pays, suppliers adjust, and behavior shifts.

This is not because people are indifferent. It is because people are adaptive. They look for substitutes, reduce exposure, transfer risk, and change arrangements. A shock becomes a process rather than a single event.

Insurance is the most visible example. A rare loss that would destroy one person can be spread across many people through premiums. Contracts can also allocate risk in advance. Markets then continue the process by changing prices and incentives.

The same logic applies to social life. A cost imposed on one group may gradually be passed to consumers, workers, landlords, suppliers, or future users depending on who has flexibility and who has alternatives. To understand consequences, we must follow the adjustment path rather than stop at the first impact.

This is why serious policy judgment cannot only ask who is named in the rule. It must ask how people will respond over time, which contracts will change, and where the cost will eventually settle.

Real accidents are not only events. They are tests of time, institutions, and adaptability. A mature system is not one where shocks never happen, but one where shocks can be absorbed without destroying the people first hit by them.
