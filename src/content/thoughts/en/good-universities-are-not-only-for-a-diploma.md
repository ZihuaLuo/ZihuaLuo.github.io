> What truly changes a person is not the degree itself, but perspective, environment, and training.

After reading Letter 039, "Why Go To College, And Why Go To A Good College?" from Wu Jun's *Silicon Valley Letters 2: The Google Methodology*, my biggest reflection was this: the meaning of college is not only to obtain a diploma, nor only to study a specific major. It is to give a person the chance to enter a larger world, receive better training, and reshape their perspective among more excellent people.

When many people talk about college, the first thing they think of is employment. It is as if going to college is for finding a job, and going to a good college is for finding a better job. This is certainly realistic, but if college is understood only from this angle, it becomes too narrow. Wu Jun mentions that going to college not only increases the chance of becoming a future social elite; even if one does not become an elite, it can greatly improve the chance of living a happy life. This made me realize that what college provides is not merely an entry point into a career, but a possibility of upward growth.

The letter tells the story of a small retail stock trader. He worked hard for many years, slowly accumulating wealth by buying high and selling a little higher. But Wu Jun points out that his problem was not lack of effort, but "low perspective and shallow knowledge." He missed the rapid development of China's internet and real estate sectors and remained trapped in the cycle of short-term trading and small profits. This example is powerful because it shows that diligence does not necessarily bring major progress. If a person's vision is limited, they may keep working hard inside a very small range while failing to see larger opportunities. Effort determines whether a person is acting; perspective determines whether they can see a larger direction.

This also explains why college matters, especially a good college. A good college is not only better facilities. It places a person in a higher-quality environment. Teachers, classmates, alumni, and academic atmosphere all influence one's horizon. Wu Jun says that if someone wants to achieve a first-rate education at a second-rate university, the key is to actively enter circles of first-rate talent. A person's perspective and knowledge come largely from the people and environments they are exposed to over time. Being with more excellent people makes it easier to see one's own shortcomings and understand what is truly worth pursuing.

This letter also helped me rethink the meaning of a major. Many college students worry about whether their major is useful, and some start drifting because they dislike their major. But Wu Jun reminds us that one important purpose of studying a major in college is not necessarily to work in that field after graduation, but to prove that one has learning ability and can learn something well. When top companies recruit, they may not care most about the exact major a candidate studied. They care more about whether the person has learning ability, problem-solving ability, and experience in a competitive environment. In other words, college does not really train only one course. It trains a person's ability to learn new things, adapt to new environments, and keep growing.

I think the deeper point in this letter is that we should not treat college as a machine that automatically changes fate. A good college provides a platform, but the platform will not complete a person's growth for them. If someone merely enters a university without actively studying, communicating, or training themselves, then even at a good university they may only receive an attractive label. By contrast, if someone has a clear purpose and knows they want to improve perspective, train ability, and meet excellent people, then college can become a real field of growth.

This also makes me think of myself. Whether in undergraduate study, graduate applications, or future career planning, I should not ask only whether a school's ranking is high or whether a program can lead to a good job. I should also ask: can it let me meet better people? Can it train my abilities? Can it open a larger horizon? Can it help me change from someone who only accepts arrangements into someone who can actively choose direction, take responsibility, and create value? If I only pursue the name of a school without using the resources inside it, I may still remain at the level of form.

The core reflection this letter left me with is that the meaning of attending a good university is not only employment, nor proving that I am better than others. It is standing at a higher starting point, receiving better training, and seeing a larger world. A diploma is only the result. What truly matters is whether the college stage changes a person's perspective, ability, and self-expectation. Entering a good university does not automatically give someone a future. But if they can use that environment well, the possibilities of the future can open much wider.
