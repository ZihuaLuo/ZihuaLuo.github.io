> Money is money not because the object itself is precious, but because people are willing to recognize its value together.

After reading Letter 081, "The Origin And Characteristics Of Money," from Wu Jun's *Silicon Valley Letters 2: The Google Methodology*, my biggest reflection was this: money is not something that exists naturally, and an object does not automatically become money simply because it is precious enough. The true essence of money is that it is a commonly accepted medium of exchange. As long as something can be recognized by people, circulate conveniently, and be used to settle transactions, it has the possibility of becoming money.

In this letter, Wu Jun first reminds us that the study of money cannot begin only with modern paper currency, gold, or Bitcoin. It must be understood within a much longer history. Long before formal money appeared in human society, barter already existed. What people first exchanged may have been grain, livestock, tools, cloth, or other necessities. But barter has a major problem: if you want what I have, I may not want what you have. Whether a transaction can happen depends on whether both sides' needs happen to match, and this greatly limits the efficiency of exchange.

So the first problem money solved was the problem of a "medium" in transactions. It allowed exchange to no longer depend on the perfect overlap of both sides' needs. For example, if I sell grain, I do not have to immediately exchange it for cloth or tools. I can first exchange it for a medium everyone recognizes, and then use that medium later to buy what I truly need. In this way, money separates a complex exchange process into parts and makes transactions more flexible and efficient.

The letter mentions Yap Island's stone money, which I found very enlightening. Local people used huge stones as money, and some of them required several people to move. At first glance, this kind of "money" seems strange, because it is not as convenient to carry as modern paper currency. But what truly mattered was that the stones did not necessarily need to be moved around. As long as everyone recognized that ownership had transferred, the transaction was complete. This shows that the key to money is not always "holding it in your hand," but "having the account recognized." From this perspective, the numbers in today's bank accounts, Alipay balances, or WeChat Pay balances are also, in essence, accounting systems.

This also made me re-understand modern finance. Many people think money must be something visible and touchable, such as paper currency, gold, or silver. But in reality, a large amount of wealth in modern society has long stopped being physical. It is numbers recorded inside financial systems. As long as that system is credible, numbers can represent wealth. If the system loses credibility, even beautiful paper money may lose its value. Therefore, behind money there is not metal, but institutions, credit, and shared cognition.

Of course, not everything can become money. The letter also mentions that early commodities were able to function as money because they had intrinsic value, were widely recognized, and had a certain degree of liquidity. Livestock, grain, tobacco, salt, and other goods once played money-like roles in different regions. They were not necessarily easy to carry, but they met real needs in local societies and were easy for people to accept. By contrast, if no one wants something or its value is difficult to judge, it is hard for it to perform the function of money.

This makes me think that the value of money is built on two layers. The first layer is practical value or scarcity. The second layer is social consensus. Early commodity money relied more on the first layer: grain can be eaten, livestock can be raised, and metals can be preserved. Modern money relies more on the second layer: national credit, financial systems, and social trust. Bitcoin and other cryptocurrencies create controversy precisely because they try to replace national credit with technological consensus. Whether that consensus is stable enough still needs to be tested by time.

From the perspective of financial thinking, this letter also reminds me that to understand money, we cannot only look at surface prices and symbols. We must look at the credit structure behind them. Why is it that, although they are all pieces of paper, some countries' currencies are accepted around the world while others keep depreciating? The reason is not the paper itself, but national credit, economic strength, institutional stability, and market confidence. On the surface, money is a payment tool. In essence, it is compressed social trust.

This also explains why the financial world cares so much about "credit." Bonds, loans, banks, funds, insurance, and even the stock market are all built on trust in future payment and future fulfillment. If credit exists, the financial system can expand and transactions can proceed smoothly. If credit collapses, the prices of money and assets can fluctuate violently. Finance is not simply making money produce more money. It uses credit to connect today's resources, future income, and society's trust.

The core reflection this letter left me with is that the essence of money is not metal, paper, or some mysterious technology. It is the shared trust formed by human society to improve the efficiency of exchange. Money can become money because everyone believes it will continue to be accepted by others. The more stable a society's currency is, the more stable the credit system behind it usually is. Once I understand this, I can see that the most central thing in finance has never been money itself, but the trust, order, and consensus behind money.
