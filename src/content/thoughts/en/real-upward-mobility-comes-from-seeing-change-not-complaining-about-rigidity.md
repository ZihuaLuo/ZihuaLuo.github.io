> Opportunities are not evenly distributed, but change constantly revalues people's positions.

Lecture 034 discusses price discrimination and social mobility through a broader point: opportunity is never evenly distributed, but the world is never completely still. A person trapped in the feeling that everything is fixed may miss the places where change is already rewriting the rules.

Many people see unfairness only as a static picture. Some people were born earlier, entered better industries, bought assets sooner, or stood closer to scarce resources. These differences are real. But if we only stare at them, we may overlook another truth: time changes demand, technology, institutions, and relative advantage.

Economic life is full of revaluation. A skill that used to be rare can become common. A profession that used to be stable can lose bargaining power. A small niche can suddenly become important because new demand appears. A person who seems ordinary in one environment may become valuable in another because the evaluation standard changes.

This does not mean effort always wins, or that all inequality is easy to overcome. It means the useful question is not only "who has more now?" but "what is changing, and whose ability will become more valuable under the new conditions?"

Complaining about rigidity can create emotional certainty, but it does not create options. Seeing change is harder because it requires observation, learning, and adjustment. It asks a person to look for new constraints, new demand, new tools, and new ways of combining existing abilities.

For personal development, this matters deeply. If someone defines themselves only by their current disadvantage, they may stop investing before the environment changes. If someone assumes their current advantage will last forever, they may be eliminated by the next round of change.

Real upward mobility does not come from denying structural differences. It comes from recognizing that structures are also moving. The task is to notice where old advantages are weakening, where new demand is appearing, and where one's next capability can meet that change.
