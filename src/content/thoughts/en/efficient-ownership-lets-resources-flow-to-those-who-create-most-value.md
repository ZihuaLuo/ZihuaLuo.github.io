> Who owns a resource should depend not only on where it came from, but also on who can use it best.

In Lecture 023, Xue Zhaofeng extends the Coase theorem. The earlier social-cost discussion focused on conflicts over scarce resources. This lecture goes further: the initial owner of a resource may not be the most important issue. What matters is whether the resource can eventually flow to the person who uses it best.

Taobao data is a good example. A customer's browsing record can be said to belong to the customer because the behavior came from the customer. It can be said to belong to the merchant because the customer browsed the merchant's page. It can also be said to belong to the platform because the data was stored there. Each view has some reason. The key question is who can use the data best.

An individual customer may create little value from one browsing record, and a single merchant sees only a partial picture. A platform or interface designer may use large-scale data to improve recommendations, page layout, product display, and transaction efficiency. The point of property arrangements is not only to decide who produced something first, but to reduce friction in moving resources toward better use.

Kindle highlights show the same logic. A reader may feel the data belongs to them because the book was purchased and the line was highlighted by them. But if Amazon aggregates many readers' highlights, later readers can see which passages mattered most. One reader's data is small; aggregated data can create a knowledge map.

This does not mean privacy can be surrendered without limits. Data and ownership are difficult because they involve both efficiency and boundaries. Platforms may use data well, but data use can violate privacy. Government may use information for security, but power cannot expand without limit.

The diamond example shows how resources move toward those who assign them higher value. Miners discover diamonds, but they do not usually wear diamonds all over themselves. Diamonds flow to consumers who pay more because they give the stones symbolic, emotional, or status value.

For personal development, resources do not stay forever with whoever had them first. Knowledge, opportunities, capital, information, networks, and platforms tend to flow toward people who use them well. Real competition is not only getting resources, but proving one can increase their value.

This is not a theory of taking by force. Coase's insight depends on clear rights and low transaction costs. Without property protection, resource movement becomes seizure; without mechanisms for movement, property becomes inefficient possession.
