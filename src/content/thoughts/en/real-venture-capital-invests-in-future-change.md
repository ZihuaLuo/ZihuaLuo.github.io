> Great investors look not only at money-making opportunities, but at whether the world will be different because of them.

After reading Letter 053, "The Investment Philosophy of John Doerr, the King of Venture Capital (Part I)," from Wu Jun's *Silicon Valley Letters 2: The Google Methodology*, my biggest reflection was this: truly top-level venture capital does not merely judge whether a project can make money. It judges whether the project can change the world. Ordinary investors see companies, valuations, and rates of return. Investors like John Doerr care more about whether a project represents the direction of the future, whether it can create a new industry, and whether society will change because it appears.

In this letter, Wu Jun introduces John Doerr's investment experience. Doerr invested in Compaq, Netscape, Symantec, Sun Microsystems, Amazon, Google, and other companies, which earned him the title "king of venture capital." But these successes were not because he always chose safe projects. On the contrary, the letter says Doerr's investment style was very bold, and even inside Kleiner Perkins his failure rate was high. This is exactly why venture capital cannot be understood through ordinary investment logic. Venture capital does not seek safety in every deal. It is willing to accept failure as long as a small number of truly great projects can produce enormous returns.

The letter mentions that Doerr often asks entrepreneurs several questions. The first is: if I invest the amount you want, what will be different in the world two years from now? This question is powerful because it does not ask, "How much money can you make?" It asks, "Can you create change?" If entrepreneurs merely say they can do better than existing companies, Doerr usually does not approve. Making an existing product cheaper, faster, or more convenient is certainly valuable, but it may not be enough to create a new future. A project truly worth venture capital support should be able to answer: why does the world need you? After you appear, what will be fundamentally different about the industry?

The second question is: why doesn't Microsoft or Google do this? This seems to challenge the entrepreneur, but it is actually examining whether the entrepreneur truly understands the opportunity. Many young entrepreneurs easily assume that large companies move slowly and inefficiently, so they must have a chance. But if a project exists only because large companies do not want to do it, or if it has no barrier even if they do, then a startup will struggle to survive for long. A truly good startup opportunity is often not simply something "big companies did not see." It is a problem that entrepreneurs discover from a new angle, new data, or new technical conditions that made a previously unsolved problem newly solvable.

The letter gives examples of Google's internal face-recognition project and automated question answering for complex problems. The former looked imaginative at first, but did not continue because the technology and application conditions were not yet mature. The latter had a better chance of being realized because Google had massive data and a strong foundation in natural language processing. This example shows that innovation is not simply proposing a new idea. It depends on whether technical conditions, data foundations, market timing, and execution ability are mature enough. Many ideas fail not because the idea is not beautiful, but because the conditions have not yet arrived.

Another important feature of Doerr is that when he invests, he does not only consider making money. He also considers whether the project can have a positive impact on the world. The letter mentions that he once invested in environmentally friendly transportation and new energy projects. Even if those projects did not necessarily succeed in the end, his starting point was not to treat money merely as a tool for making more money. He hoped capital could push the world to become different. This made me feel that venture capital should not be only a capital game. It can also be a force for social change. If money only pursues short-term returns, it may create bubbles. But if money flows toward real technical breakthroughs and social value, it can become fuel for innovation.

Of course, this does not mean a project is worth investing in just because its goal is grand. Venture capital still needs to judge real conditions. However beautiful a project's vision may be, if it lacks technical barriers, the right team, or market demand, it may only be an empty idea. What is worth learning from Doerr's investment philosophy is not blind belief in grand narratives, but the search for executable paths behind those narratives. He looks at whether the world will change, but he also looks at whether the entrepreneurs truly understand the problem and have the ability to make that change happen.

This is very helpful for my understanding of finance. In the past, I may have been more likely to understand investment through return, valuation, and exit proceeds. But this letter reminds me that, especially in venture capital, what really matters is judgment about the future. Finance is not a numbers game far removed from reality. Behind it are technology, industry, entrepreneurship, and social change. A good investor is not only someone who can calculate. A good investor can understand trends, understand people, judge timing, and dare to carry uncertainty for the future.

The core reflection this letter left me with is that truly great venture capital does not look for a short-term money-making opportunity. It looks for a future worth creating. It requires investors to have imagination as well as judgment, to dare to take risk without becoming superstitious about stories. John Doerr became the "king of venture capital" not because he was always right, but because he was willing to direct capital toward possibilities that might change the world. For investment, perhaps the most important question is not "how much money can this project make?" but "if it succeeds, will the world become different because of it?"
