> Discrimination does not disappear on its own; only when prejudice has a cost do people actively correct it.

In Lecture 011, Xue Zhaofeng discusses Gary Becker's economic view of discrimination. People often treat discrimination as one-way harm: the discriminator hurts others and the discriminated person bears the loss. Economics adds that discriminators also pay a price.

Discrimination has two broad sources. One is preference: people may prefer one style, group, or culture. The other is information asymmetry. When information is incomplete, people use group impressions to judge individuals. This shortcut may be unfair, but when information costs are high, people reach for shortcuts.

The problem is that shortcuts can be wrong, and mistakes have costs. An investor who rejects Jack Ma because he does not look like a traditional successful entrepreneur may miss a huge opportunity. A company that rejects a group of employees because of stereotypes may lose excellent talent. Excluding others can also shrink one's own choices.

The amount of discrimination often depends on its cost. In a closed small circle, a person can keep prejudice cheaply. But in a more open and competitive environment, the same prejudice becomes costly. To study, work, and cooperate with people of different backgrounds, continued discrimination can make a person unable to move.

Cities and markets work through this force. Big cities are often more tolerant not necessarily because people there are morally superior, but because cooperation opportunities are richer and competition is stronger. If an employer rejects good people because of appearance, accent, gender, or lifestyle, competitors can hire them.

Markets do not eliminate all prejudice. People have preferences, laziness, and stereotypes. But markets make many unnecessary biases expensive. When a prejudice costs customers, talent, cooperation, and profit, people have reason to revise it.

This matters for personal growth. Bias often appears not as obvious malice, but as premature judgment. Seeing a school, major, accent, or background, we put a label on someone too early. It saves effort in the short term, but it closes doors in the long term.

Reducing discrimination cannot rely only on slogans. A more effective method is to make wrong discriminators bear costs and to reward more open people. Bias not only hurts others; it narrows the person who holds it.
