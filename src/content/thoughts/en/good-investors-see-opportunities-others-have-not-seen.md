> The hardest part of venture capital is not making a bet, but judging people, timing, and the future inside uncertainty.

After reading Letter 054, "The Investment Philosophy of John Doerr, the King of Venture Capital (Part II)," from Wu Jun's *Silicon Valley Letters 2: The Google Methodology*, my biggest reflection was this: truly excellent venture investors do not simply search for projects that look popular. They judge, while others still cannot see clearly, whether an entrepreneur has the ability to build the future into reality. The core of venture capital is not only investing money. It is judging people, timing, risk, and whether something deserves long-term belief.

In this letter, Wu Jun mentions several abilities Doerr values in entrepreneurs. First, entrepreneurs must be able to design, implement, and operate a business. Many people think starting a company means having a good idea or raising money, but that is far from enough. A real entrepreneur must not only propose an idea, but also build the product, organize the team, control costs, and eventually make the company profitable. If an entrepreneur can only tell stories but cannot make things happen, even the best concept remains only a concept.

Second, entrepreneurs must be able to see opportunities, difficulties, and risks that others cannot see. This judgment matters. Many people see opportunities but not difficulties; others see only risk and dare not act at all. Someone with real entrepreneurial potential should have both kinds of vision: on the one hand, the ability to see unmet market demand; on the other hand, the ability to see the obstacles required to realize that opportunity. For example, the early e-commerce company 8848 saw the opportunity of internet shopping, but underestimated the difficulties of online payment, logistics, and user trust at the time. Although it started early, it did not truly break through. This shows that being early is not the same as being right, and seeing an opportunity is not the same as being able to seize it.

Third, entrepreneurs also need the ability to judge resource allocation. A company cannot do everything itself, and not all resources are unlimited. When to cooperate, when to acquire, and when to develop internally all require judgment. Many startups fail not because the direction is completely wrong, but because the way they solve problems is wrong. An excellent entrepreneur must know what is truly missing and where to make up for it.

The sentence in this letter that impressed me most was this: today's founder should be tomorrow's entrepreneur. This is very enlightening. A founder and an entrepreneur are not the same thing. A founder may simply be the person who starts a project, but an entrepreneur must turn a project into an organization that continues to operate. Many people are suited to starting from zero, but not necessarily to scaling a company. Some people have strong technical ability but lack management and business judgment. What venture investors really need to find is not only someone who can build a product today, but someone who can lead an organization to grow in the future.

Doerr's emphasis on the stealth stage is also worth thinking about. Many people assume a project is most worth investing in once the public generally thinks highly of it, but in venture capital the opposite is often true. When everyone says a project is good, the price may already be high and the best investment opportunity may have passed. Truly good venture capital often happens when a project is not yet widely understood, the market has not fully opened, and there are still many opposing voices. This stage carries great risk, but if the judgment is correct, the return can also be the greatest. Venture capital does not need a democratic vote. It needs insight.

The letter also mentions SGI. SGI was once a very powerful graphics workstation and server company, with high gross margins and rapid growth. But when the internet bubble burst and market demand changed, it had become too accustomed to high profits and lacked the ability to respond to a downturn, eventually going bankrupt. This example shows that a company looking strong today does not mean it can survive cycles. A company with real long-term value needs not only high growth and high margins, but also the ability to adapt to change. If investors look only at beautiful current data, they can easily ignore structural risk.

Another important point in Doerr's investment philosophy is letting portfolio companies help one another. Venture capital does not invest in a company in isolation. It places the company into a network of relationships. Portfolio companies can share experience, resources, talent, and customers, thereby improving the overall probability of success. This made me realize that what top venture capital truly provides is not only capital, but an ecosystem. Money can come from many places, but an investment institution that can place entrepreneurs into a high-quality network is far more valuable than a pure provider of capital.

The core reflection this letter left me with is that venture capital is not a simple bet, but a systematic judgment about the future. A good investor must look at the founder's ability as well as industry timing; opportunity as well as risk; an individual company as well as whether that company can enter a larger ecosystem. Truly high-level investing does not chase hot opportunities that have already been proven. It identifies people and companies with the potential to become great while the future is still unclear.
