> Busyness does not equal value creation; the key is whether effort increases wealth or fights over distribution.

In Lecture 020, Xue Zhaofeng explains rent seeking. Many people assume that if everyone works hard, a country naturally becomes richer. Economics asks a sharper question: is the effort creating new value, or merely fighting over benefits that already exist?

The beggar example is direct. When a passerby gives a beggar one yuan, it looks like free charity. But beggars may compete for better locations, perform misery, or even harm themselves to attract sympathy. The money looks free, but the competition for it consumes real time, energy, and health.

Rent seeking appears whenever benefits come not from creating value but from winning special qualifications, monopoly rights, policy favors, or distribution opportunities. The individual winner may gain, but society may not become richer. Worse, everyone may spend resources fighting for the prize.

A government favor worth one hundred yuan can illustrate this. If Zhang San spends fifty yuan competing for it and Li Si also spends fifty yuan, and Li Si wins, he receives a benefit worth one hundred yuan. But society has already spent one hundred yuan in competition. Those resources were not used to make better products or services.

Theft follows a similar structure. A thief transfers wealth, but society then spends on locks, cameras, guards, and investigation. The thief also spends effort learning how to steal and avoid detection. Production resources are diverted into mutual defense.

This is why some societies can be very busy without becoming wealthy. Doctors developing medicine, entrepreneurs improving products, workers increasing efficiency, and artists creating work can expand total wealth. But if smart people spend their energy on connections, approvals, subsidies, monopolies, and special treatment, society becomes busy in a low-quality way.

For individuals, the distinction is equally important. Seeking opportunity is necessary, but one must ask whether the effort improves capability or merely searches for tickets and shortcuts. Long-term effort should raise productivity: learning, training, experience, credibility, and better work. Advantages built only on access, labels, packaging, or special channels are fragile.

The value of the rent-seeking concept is that it separates two kinds of busyness: creating increments and fighting over stock. Wealth is not won by getting closer to privilege. It is created by making what one provides more useful.
