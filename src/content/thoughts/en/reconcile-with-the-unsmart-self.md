> Being slow is not a reason to give up; it is where steady work begins.

After reading Letter 009, "God Likes The Slow People, Part I," from Wu Jun's *Silicon Valley Letters 2: The Google Methodology*, my biggest reflection was this: the so-called "slow person" is not someone who has no ability. It is someone who acknowledges the limits of their ability and still chooses to do things carefully and steadily. By contrast, the truly dangerous thing may not be being slow, but believing too much in one's own cleverness.

In this letter, Wu Jun describes some of his own working habits. On the surface, these habits do not look "smart." Some even look clumsy. For example, in investing, he does not fully rely on tools that help manage investment information. He is willing to organize the information by hand. At first glance, this seems like a waste of time, because computers can clearly process data faster. But his reason is not that he rejects tools. It is that the process of manual organization is itself a process of thinking. A tool can produce results quickly, but it can also make a person skip understanding. Organizing information by hand is slower, but it forces him to look at the data again, review his original investment judgments, and even summarize lessons from wrong decisions. In other words, he is not trying to get the answer as quickly as possible. He is training judgment through a slower process.

Another example is that he says his memory is not good. So for important letters or packages, he would rather make a separate trip than carry them along while doing something else. This looks troublesome and not very flexible, but the reason behind it is realistic: if he does it "on the way," he may forget once, then twice, and eventually cause a larger loss. Instead of believing "I will definitely remember next time," he acknowledges that he may forget and uses a clumsy method to handle the task separately. This is not inefficiency. It is paying a small cost now to avoid a bigger problem later.

He also does not use many money-saving tricks and does not like taking free things. Many people may think this is uneconomical: if something can be saved, why not save it? But the letter explains clearly that everything has a cost, and free things are not truly free. To get a small cash reward, one may have to open an account, deposit money, close the account, and remember various deadlines. All of this consumes attention. Buying cheap goods is similar. If someone buys a pile of unnecessary things just to save 20 percent, then on the surface they saved money, but in reality they wasted money, space, and energy. So when he says he does not chase bargains, it is not because he does not care about money. It is because he values the cost of attention and time more.

His habit of arranging activities early follows the same logic. Many people like to handle things at the last minute because it feels more flexible. But Wu Jun believes he is not good at dealing with complex situations, so he would rather plan ahead. The reason is that he knows human ability is limited. If everything is left until the last moment, conflicts, omissions, and even damage to other people's impression of you become more likely. Early planning may look clumsy, but it reduces chaos and lets things move forward in order.

From these examples, Wu Jun's "slowness" is not a lack of ability. It is a clear awareness of his own limits. He knows he can forget, become lazy, and be distracted by complex information. He also knows that human attention and energy are limited. So he does not imagine himself as someone who is always smart, always efficient, and never makes mistakes. Instead, he uses methods that look clumsy to reduce the probability of errors. Put differently, he does not rely on cleverness to avoid problems. He relies on systems, habits, and patience to reduce them.

This makes me think of myself. I also often say that I am not smart. Especially when studying or thinking through a problem, there are times when I have already spent a lot of time but still cannot grasp the key point. The more I think, the more confused I become, and eventually I get angry at myself. That feeling is not only because the problem is difficult. It is because I start doubting myself: why do other people seem to understand so quickly while I need to think again and again? Why have I worked hard but still not gotten the result immediately? At that moment, "not smart" is no longer just an adjective. It becomes a denial of myself.

But this letter helped me understand "slowness" differently. Maybe being slow is not frightening. What is frightening is giving up on doing things because I think I am slow. Anger is anger, but life still continues, and effort is still necessary. A person can admit that they do not react that quickly, understand that easily, or remember that well, but that does not mean they have no way forward. On the contrary, precisely because I know I am not smart enough, I need more solid methods to compensate: if others understand after reading once, I will read several times; if others grasp the key point quickly, I will break the problem apart and organize it step by step; if others rely on inspiration, I will rely on planning, repetition, and patience.

This is a more realistic view of effort. Many people imagine effort as passion and success, but effort is more often a repeated process of living with one's own slowness, confusion, and anxiety. I do not work hard because I believe I am definitely smart. I work hard because even when I feel I am not smart enough, I still do not give up on myself. Reconciling with myself does not mean persuading myself, "I am already good enough, so I do not need to change." It means admitting, "I do have many weaknesses, but I can still keep becoming better."

The inspiration this letter gave me is that being slow is not necessarily a flaw. It can also be a basic attitude toward work. Smart people may find shortcuts more easily, but if slower people can admit their limits, respect costs, and keep making steady effort, they can still find their own path. For me, the most important thing is not to stop saying I am slow. It is to stop letting "I am slow" become a reason to escape. I need to learn how to live with this less-smart version of myself and keep moving forward with it. Life will not stop just because I cannot understand something for a while, and I cannot give up becoming better just because I am not smart enough.
