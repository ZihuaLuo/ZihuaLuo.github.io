> Effective communication is not about appearing smart, but about understanding the matter and doing it correctly.

After reading Letter 145 from Wu Jun's *Silicon Valley Letters 2*, I was reminded of a simple but often ignored truth: there are no stupid questions, only questions that have not been made clear. Many people are afraid to ask basic questions because they worry about appearing ignorant. But in real work and real learning, the greater risk is pretending to understand and then moving in the wrong direction.

The letter mentions scientists such as Lawrence Rabiner and Frederick Jelinek, who were willing to ask seemingly simple questions in serious technical conversations. Their questions did not make them look weak. Instead, they often exposed hidden assumptions and helped everyone return to the essence of the problem. Truly strong people are not afraid of asking because their goal is not to protect face. Their goal is to understand.

Many complex problems become confusing because important assumptions are left unsaid. When discussing currency valuation, option pricing, or business decisions, people often use familiar words while meaning different things. If nobody clarifies the definition, the conversation may sound professional but remain internally broken. A basic question can force the group to reveal what is being assumed, what is known, and what still needs evidence.

This is also true in learning. Andrew Wiles's proof of Fermat's Last Theorem was not built on pretending every step was obvious. Deep understanding requires asking again and again where the logic comes from. A person who does not dare to ask will often memorize conclusions without grasping the structure. Once the context changes, the borrowed conclusion stops working.

In the workplace, asking clear questions is even more important. Before accepting a task, it is worth asking what the goal is, who the audience is, what the standard is, and what constraints must be respected. These questions may look simple, but they prevent wasted work. Many failed projects are not caused by weak execution. They are caused by unclear requirements that nobody dared to challenge at the beginning.

The real value of a question is not whether it sounds advanced. It is whether it reduces ambiguity. A good question protects cooperation, improves judgment, and keeps people from paying the cost of false understanding. The lesson for me is direct: never use silence to disguise confusion. A person who asks clearly may look slower for a moment, but usually moves faster in the end.
