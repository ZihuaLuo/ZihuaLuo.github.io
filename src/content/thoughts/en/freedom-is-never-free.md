> A person may have freedom of expression, but that does not mean they may freely occupy other people's venues, platforms, equipment, and attention.

Lecture 055 discusses a concept that is often confused: freedom does not mean free of charge. In English, free can mean liberty and also no payment. This overlap often leads people to believe that political freedom naturally includes economic free access. Reality is the opposite. Political freedom matters, but once freedom is expressed in the real world, it often occupies real resources, and resources do not appear out of nowhere.

A person has freedom of speech, and that matters. But speaking needs space, writing needs paper or a platform, a speech needs a venue and equipment, publication needs channels, and transmission needs other people's attention. The speech itself may be free, but the resources carrying it are not. If someone can occupy another person's mall, TV station, website, meeting room, or attention merely because they have freedom of speech, then that freedom has become an infringement of someone else's property rights.

Many public discussions slide past this distinction. When people talk about freedom, they often feel morally elevated. When they talk about property, they treat it as mere commercial interest. But economic rights are not lower-grade rights. Property rights protect not only objects one owns, but the foundation on which one can live independently, judge independently, and express independently. If society can seize someone's property for a supposedly nobler expression, the loss is not only to merchants. It is to every ordinary person's security.

A shopping mall, for example, is a privately operated commercial space. It is open to the public, but that does not make it ownerless. Customers enter to shop, rest, and consume. Lighting, cleaning, safety, and order all cost money. If someone enters the mall for political campaigning, fundraising, protest, or petitioning, even if the cause is sympathetic, one fact cannot be ignored: they are using someone else's commercial space and customers' attention.

The same applies to television advertising. Inserting ads during programming is not only an interruption of the viewer experience. Advertising is a key revenue source that keeps a station operating. Programs require hosts, editors, equipment, rights, and venues. If the law simply bans inserted ads, it may seem to protect viewers, but it may weaken future content supply. Viewers may see fewer ads, but they may also see fewer good programs later.

Websites face the same issue. Many people want online content to be free, clean, adless, and undisturbing. But content production is not costless. Reporters, editors, servers, technical maintenance, and content rights all cost money. If users refuse to pay, refuse to see ads, and still expect high-quality content to exist long term, there is a contradiction. Free access is not free supply, and dislike of advertising does not erase a platform's survival logic.

This does not mean every ad is reasonable or every commercial platform should be unconstrained. The key is that we cannot deny resource costs simply because we dislike a business arrangement. Nor can we force others to bear costs unconditionally just because an expression has a public or political character. Freedom of expression should be protected, but protecting expression does not mean forcing others to provide tools for expression free of charge.

The important step is to separate two questions. Does a person have the freedom to express a view? Yes. Does someone else have a duty to provide resources for that expression for free? Not necessarily. The first is political freedom. The second involves economic rights. Both matter, and one cannot simply override the other.

Many people habitually rank economic rights low, as if money, venues, platforms, and business models are less noble than political rights. In reality, political freedom often depends on economic foundations. A person without stable income, independent property, or time and space of their own is more dependent when expressing opinions and more easily controlled. Economic freedom is not the enemy of political freedom. It is one of its foundations.

This points to a broader truth: many things that look free have only shifted their costs. Free education may be paid by taxpayers. Free health care may be paid by fiscal budgets, doctors, or people waiting in line. Free content may be paid by advertisers, platforms, or creators. Free expression may be paid by venue owners and people forced to receive the disturbance. As long as resources are scarce, there is no truly costless free.

Freedom therefore cannot mean doing whatever I want. Sustainable freedom means choosing while recognizing other people's boundaries. A person can express a view, but cannot force others to provide a microphone. They can criticize society, but cannot occupy someone else's shop. They can spread an idea, but cannot requisition another person's platform for free. Freedom becomes a stable social order only when it respects property.

This also makes me reflect that people often understand only their own freedom. They want space to speak, but forget that others have a right to refuse occupation. They want their cause to be seen, but ignore that other people's attention is valuable. They want public values to be realized, but do not want to face the private costs behind those values.

The hardest task for a mature society is to protect both expression and property, to recognize the importance of public discussion while also recognizing that resources have owners. Saying freedom is not free does not belittle freedom. It reminds us that the more we cherish freedom, the more seriously we must treat its costs. Once freedom can casually shift costs to others, it stops being a right and becomes a privilege.

Real freedom is not occupying others for free. It is bearing the cost of one's own choices within clear boundaries.
