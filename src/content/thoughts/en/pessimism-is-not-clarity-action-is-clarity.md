> Truly strong people do not fail to see problems; they are not trapped by them.

After reading Letter 065, "Why Does Pessimism Become Popular?" from Wu Jun's *Silicon Valley Letters 2: The Google Methodology*, my biggest reflection was this: pessimism becomes popular not only because the world is truly getting worse, but because pessimism often looks deeper, more mature, and more able to capture attention. Compared with optimism, pessimism looks like "seeing through reality." Compared with action, complaint also feels easier. But this letter reminds me that real clarity is not describing problems as severely as possible. It is seeing problems and still believing that people can do something.

The letter mentions that when facing global warming, economic fluctuations, and social changes, many people easily fall into pessimism. Pessimism has a natural infectious power because it fits the human instinct for anxiety. People are more sensitive to danger, bad news, and failure, so a pessimistic judgment is often easier to believe than an optimistic one. For example, if a scholar predicts a crisis and it truly happens, people will think he has foresight. If it does not happen, people may still think the crisis has only been delayed. Pessimism easily gains an advantage in logic because it can always explain disasters that have not happened as "not yet."

But Wu Jun also points out that many huge historical panics did not eventually happen in the way people feared. In the past, some worried that population explosions would exhaust resources, some worried that oil would soon run out, and some worried that various diseases and economic crises would destroy the world. The fact is that human society has always had problems, but it has also kept solving problems through technology, institutions, and forms of organization. The biggest problem with pessimism is not that it sees danger. It is that it often underestimates humanity's ability to solve problems.

This makes me think of an important distinction: pessimism is not the same as rationality. A rational person sees risks and evaluates risks. But a pessimistic person may see only risks and not the possibility of responding to them. Global warming, for example, is indeed a serious problem. But if one therefore concludes that humanity is inevitably heading toward disaster, one ignores the possibilities of new energy, policy adjustment, technological progress, and international cooperation. A truly mature attitude is neither blind optimism that says "it is fine," nor complete pessimism that says "it is over." It is admitting that problems exist while continuing to search for ways to solve them.

The letter also mentions that pessimism has a "twin brother," skepticism. Moderate skepticism is certainly necessary. It helps us avoid being deceived and keeps us from easily believing unverified information. But if skepticism becomes a habit, a person stops believing in any goodwill, opportunity, or future. This kind of skepticism is not wisdom. It is a form of mental closure. If a person always believes everything will fail, they will naturally give up action. Once they give up action, reality may truly become worse.

This letter made me realize that the most dangerous part of pessimism is not only that it makes people feel bad, but that it makes people lose the ability to act. If a person believes the future has no hope, they will not be willing to study for the long term. If they believe effort has no meaning, they will not be willing to take responsibility. If they believe the world can only get worse, they will easily trap themselves in complaint. Pessimism looks clever on the surface, but it may actually be an escape. As long as one says in advance, "it will not work anyway," one does not have to truly do difficult things.

By contrast, real optimism is not naivety. It is a form of responsibility. Optimism does not deny problems. It believes problems can be improved little by little. Buffett has been called permanently bullish not because he cannot see economic cycles and market risks, but because he believes that in the long run, human society and businesses still have the ability to create value. A long-term thinker must have some optimistic spirit. Otherwise, they would not be willing to invest time, resources, and effort into the future. Without basic confidence in the future, it is difficult for people to persist in anything truly important.

The core reflection this letter left me with is that pessimism does not necessarily mean clarity, and optimism does not necessarily mean naivety. What truly matters is whether a person can see problems clearly and still keep the courage to act. The world certainly has risks, and life will not always be smooth. But if we focus only on bad outcomes, we lose the power to change reality. Truly mature cognition is not believing that everything will automatically get better. It is believing that as long as people are willing to act, things still have the possibility of becoming better.
