> Understanding something fully often requires time, cost, and historical context to test it together.

After reading Letter 107, "A New Understanding Of DDT," from Wu Jun's *Silicon Valley Letters 2: The Google Methodology*, my biggest reflection was this: many things cannot be simply judged as good or bad at the beginning. An invention, a choice, or a system may save countless lives in one historical stage, and later reveal serious side effects after long-term use. Mature judgment does not rush to conclude. It admits the complexity of the world and understands that the same thing may look completely different across different times and environments.

In the letter, Wu Jun first quotes Bai Juyi's lines: "Testing jade requires three full days of burning; identifying timber requires seven years." The meaning is that we cannot judge truth, falsehood, good, or bad only by the present moment. Time must test it. This idea is especially important for scientific inventions. When many inventions first appear, people only see how they solve problems and cannot see the side effects that accumulate over time. Some things are also misunderstood at first and only later prove to have great value.

DDT is a very typical example. It was first seen as a remarkable insecticide. Near the end of World War II and after the war, it helped Italy, India, and other countries control diseases such as malaria, yellow fever, and typhus. In India especially, DDT helped reduce malaria cases from 75 million to 5 million. It also helped increase agricultural output. After World War II, the world's cultivated land did not increase much, but grain production rose significantly. From the historical environment of that time, DDT really did save many lives and solve practical problems in food production and disease transmission.

But later, the other side of DDT gradually appeared. Rachel Carson wrote in *Silent Spring* that DDT enters the food chain and accumulates in higher animals, disrupting bird reproduction and even making spring silent. More seriously, DDT may also bring cancer, heart disease, brain damage, and other risks to humans. Its harm did not break out immediately. It became visible through long-term accumulation, environmental spread, and biological magnification. This made me realize that many risks are frightening exactly because they are not obvious at the beginning.

This letter made me think of many modern problems with similar logic. When a new technology, tool, or method first appears, people often see its efficiency, convenience, and benefits first, but rarely understand its possible long-term costs. Some tools that improve efficiency may save time in the short term but weaken independent thinking in the long term. Some convenient consumption methods may satisfy needs in the short term but create dependency in the long term. Some quick fixes may work immediately but damage the balance of the original system over time.

Wu Jun also reminds us that we should not completely deny DDT's historical role just because its harms were later discovered. It did save many people and had a historical mission in a specific era. Some African countries still face severe malaria problems today, and the World Health Organization has also allowed DDT use under specific conditions, such as indoor residual spraying to reduce environmental contamination. This shows that judgment cannot be separated from context. Criticism detached from history may be unfair; praise detached from long-term consequences may be irresponsible.

This also made me reflect on my own way of looking at problems. Sometimes I want to quickly decide whether something is right or wrong, good or bad. But this letter reminds me that many things are not binary. What matters is what problem it solves and what problem it creates; whether it is necessary now and sustainable in the long run; who enjoys the benefits and who bears the costs. A mature judgment is often not a simple conclusion, but a set of conditions, boundaries, and time scales.

So the core reflection this letter gave me is that any powerful tool or invention can be a double-edged sword. It may solve real problems, and it may also create new risks over a longer period of time. We should not reject all progress because of risk, and we should not ignore all costs because of benefit. A rational attitude is to recognize historical contributions while continuing to observe side effects, to use tools while preserving boundaries, and to embrace new things while staying alert to long-term consequences.

In the future, whether in study, career choices, or when facing new technologies and social changes, I hope I will not draw conclusions too quickly. Many things need to be tested by time and understood within their specific environments. Real wisdom is not instantly judging right or wrong. It is keeping patience amid complex information and seeing the positive side, the negative side, and the conditions of the era around it. Only then can I avoid being misled by short-term results, and also avoid using today's standards to dismiss past choices too simply.
