> Truly efficient people do not spread effort evenly; they first find the key dividing point.

After reading Letter 059, "Quicksort: To Improve Efficiency, Do Fewer Things," from Wu Jun's *Silicon Valley Letters 2: The Google Methodology*, my biggest reflection was this: the key to improving efficiency is often not doing things faster or investing more time, but first thinking clearly about which things do not need to be done at all. People often understand efficiency as acceleration, as if more diligence, harder work, and more intensity can get things done well. But this letter reminds me that truly efficient thinking uses reasonable classification and fewer useless comparisons to break complex problems into smaller problems that are easier to handle.

The letter uses traffic routes to show that there is no absolutely best method in the world. Usually, from the East Third Ring Road to the airport, the shortest route may be the fastest. But if there is severe traffic, taking a detour may save time. This example is very concrete, but it illustrates an important truth: what we call "best" only exists under specific conditions. This is true for algorithms, and also for life. A method being effective under normal conditions does not mean it is effective under all conditions. A choice that looks most direct is not necessarily optimal in a complex environment. Mature judgment does not cling to one answer. It adjusts the method according to conditions.

The core idea of quicksort is also illuminating. Facing a pile of unordered numbers, it does not make every number compare with every other number. It first randomly chooses a "pivot" and divides the numbers into two groups: those greater than it and those less than it. In this way, one chaotic pile of data is first divided into two smaller piles with more order. Then the same process is repeated for each smaller pile until the whole set becomes ordered. It is efficient not because each step is magical, but because it eliminates a large number of unnecessary comparisons.

This makes me think of many sources of inefficiency in real life. We are often inefficient not because we do too little, but because we have not first classified things or judged priorities, so we process everything together. For example, when studying, if one does not first distinguish core concepts from details, one will fall into evenly spreading effort. At work, if one does not first judge which tasks truly affect results and which are only formalities, one will consume a large amount of time on unimportant things. The essence of low efficiency is often not slow speed, but too much comparison, hesitation, and repetition.

The letter also connects quicksort with social organization and management, which is a deep point. If a society, company, or school wants to improve efficiency, it must make reasonable distinctions based on people's abilities, task types, and resource conditions. If everyone is required to do things in the same way, it may look fair, but it may actually be highly inefficient. For example, if twenty thousand students directly compete together, the management cost will be very high. But if they are first roughly classified by school, grades, or ability level, and then compared within each level, much useless consumption can be reduced. Distinction is not meant to create inequality. It is meant to let resources function more effectively.

However, there is also something to be careful about. Classification can improve efficiency, but the classification itself must be reasonable. If the standard is wrong, people will be placed in the wrong positions and resources will be directed toward the wrong places. Quicksort depends on the choice of the pivot. Organizational management also depends on the choice of key judgment points. A wrong dividing point can make all later effort inefficient. Therefore, real efficiency is not simply separating things. It is finding the most meaningful boundary. One must first ask: what is the most important variable? What determines the later path? What can be excluded first? This is where efficiency begins.

This letter also made me reflect on the way I work. Sometimes when facing many tasks, I instinctively want to push all of them forward at once, feeling that this counts as effort. But the result is often that I touch a bit of everything without truly finishing the key part. Quicksort inspires me to see that when facing complex tasks, the first step should not be immediate action, but organizing the structure first: which things matter most? Which things can wait? Which things are not worth doing at all? Without this judgment, so-called diligence may only be inefficient busyness.

The core reflection this letter left me with is that efficiency is not making every task faster, but reducing unnecessary tasks. Truly efficient people are not those who are always busy. They are those who can first identify key points, divide levels, and reduce useless comparison. Whether in study, work, or life planning, the most important thing is not to invest effort desperately at the beginning. It is to first find the correct way to divide the problem. When direction and structure are right, effort truly produces efficiency. Otherwise, the more one does, the more one may simply waste.
