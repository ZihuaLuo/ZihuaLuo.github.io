> Commerce can become the largest charity not because merchants are kinder, but because markets turn what goodwill struggles to do into sustained cooperation.

In Lecture 008, Xue Zhaofeng compares charitable poverty relief with commercial mechanisms. The title, that commerce is the largest charity, can easily be misunderstood because people often see charity as kindness and commerce as profit seeking. But if we look at results, many improvements in human life come not from direct sympathy, but from stable and sustainable commercial cooperation.

The contrast from The White Man's Burden is sharp. Over decades, the West poured enormous aid into Africa, yet many basic problems remained unsolved. Cheap mosquito nets and low-cost vaccines could have saved many lives if delivered accurately, but huge aid systems often failed. By contrast, when a new Harry Potter book was released, the market could deliver millions of copies quickly and restock stores fast.

This does not mean publishers are kinder than charities. It means commerce has a stronger feedback mechanism. Readers decide whether to buy. Stores immediately know where books are missing. If a book does not sell, publishers and bookstores bear losses. Each link in the market is tested by results.

Charity often struggles at exactly this point. Donors provide money, recipients receive help, and executors in the middle may not directly bear the result. Did the goods arrive? Were they useful? Were they wasted? The feedback chain can be long and unclear. If recipients are dissatisfied, they may not have choice. If executors are inefficient, they may not be replaced quickly.

This does not deny the value of charity. In disasters, illness, and hardship, society needs aid. The hard question is how to turn goodwill into effective help. Money spent does not equal problems solved. Goods delivered do not equal lives improved. Without selection, supervision, and feedback, charity can misallocate resources or create dependence.

Commerce is special because it does not require strangers to love one another. A baker does not make bread because he loves me; a delivery worker does not bring food because he knows me; a phone manufacturer does not care personally about my life. But to pursue their own interests, they must satisfy other people's needs.

This is the value of business that is often missed. A company that earns money for a long time usually continues to provide products and services people value. The value of commerce is not purity of motive. It is that prices, competition, and feedback turn self-interest into service for others.

So saying commerce is the largest charity does not belittle charity. It reminds us that improving the world requires more than love; it requires mechanisms. Kindness can start action, but mechanisms determine whether action can continue, scale, and work.
