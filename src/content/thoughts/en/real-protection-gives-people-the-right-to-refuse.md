> The core of property protection is not keeping resources unchanged forever, but making change happen through voluntary consent as much as possible.

Lecture 059 discusses the first principle of property protection: the property rule. In its simplest form, the rule says that if you want to take someone else's thing, the only legitimate way is to offer a price high enough that the owner is willing to accept.

This sounds like common sense, but it contains a deep civilized order. It recognizes a person's right to judge their own property. The value of my watch should not be decided by bystanders or by the person who wants to buy it. It should be decided by me. You may think it is worth one thousand. I may think it carries memory and will not sell for less than one hundred thousand. Under the property rule, the key question is not whether there is a reasonable market price, but whether the original owner has the right to say no.

This is the most precious part of the property rule. It protects not only objects, but also the freedom to refuse a transaction.

People often interpret efficient resource movement as whoever can use it better should take it. That sounds efficient, but without the property rule it becomes dangerous. Who uses something better is often judged not by the owner but by a third party. Today one can say a developer uses land better than residents. Tomorrow one can say a large company uses a block better than small shop owners. The day after, one can say the strong use opportunities better than the weak. Once efficiency is detached from voluntary exchange, it can become a reason for deprivation.

The value of the property rule is that it sets a threshold for efficiency. You may believe you can use the resource better, but you must persuade the original owner through exchange. You cannot only prove that you are useful. You must pay a cost that makes the other person willing to give it up.

Of course, the real world cannot always operate only through the property rule. If a traffic accident has already occurred and someone's arm is injured, we cannot let the victim name any price afterward. If the victim could set the price completely, they might demand two million, twenty million, or two hundred billion. This is not because the pain is unreal, but because after-the-fact bargaining no longer has the conditions of normal trade. The accident was not the result of prior negotiation, so society retreats to a second-best solution: courts, insurers, or other third parties decide compensation. This is where the liability rule enters.

But precisely because the liability rule is second-best, we should see the importance of the property rule more clearly. The property rule applies where negotiation was possible. The liability rule applies where negotiation costs are too high, prior bargaining was impossible, and harm has already occurred. The first respects choice. The second compensates for losses after choice was impossible. Neither fully replaces the other. They serve different situations.

This is useful in daily life. Many conflicts look like price disputes, but at their core they ask whether the other person's choice has been respected. Some people think another person's home price is too high, rent is unreasonable, or service fee is unfair, and want outside force to push the price down. But if the resource is legally owned by that person, a high price is not itself an error. Real market exchange does not mean everyone can afford everything. It means everyone can choose whether to sell, buy, refuse to sell, or refuse to buy.

Without the right to refuse, consent has no meaning. Without voluntary exchange, price is only an order wearing market clothing.

This also explains why crime is not only about causing damage. If someone steals another person's thing, the harm is not only the loss of an object. More seriously, the thief changes the rule without permission. The object should have been protected by the property rule: anyone who wants it must offer enough for the owner to agree. The criminal skips that process and downgrades the relationship into a liability rule: I take it first and compensate later if necessary. That change itself damages social order.

Criminal punishment is therefore not only compensation for the victim. It prevents people from casually rewriting rules. Otherwise all relationships that should require consent become after-the-fact compensation relationships, and all transactions that should be voluntary become remedies after harm.

Whether a society respects property is not shown by whether resources are never adjusted. It is shown by whether adjustment respects the original owner's consent as much as possible. The property rule tells us that real protection is not freezing everything in place. It is requiring change to pass through human consent. Civilization does not mean resources can never move. It means resource movement cannot easily bypass human will.

At the bottom, property protection does not only protect the rich, assets, or specific objects. It protects every person's right to say one sentence when facing outside power: this is mine, and unless I agree, you cannot take it.
