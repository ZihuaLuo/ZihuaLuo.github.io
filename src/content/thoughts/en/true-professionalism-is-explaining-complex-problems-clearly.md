> Expression is not making words sound profound, but helping people from different backgrounds understand the essence.

After reading Letter 191 from Wu Jun's *Silicon Valley Letters 2*, I felt that true professionalism is often shown in explanation. Many people think being professional means using complex terms and sounding difficult to understand. But a stronger professional can explain complex problems clearly to people outside the field without distorting the essence.

The letter gives several examples. Instead of describing aircraft thrust with technical parameters alone, one can use an analogy that helps the listener feel the scale. Instead of explaining tax rates through abstract clauses, one can say that Uncle Sam may take roughly half in a certain high-tax situation. Instead of relying only on legal terminology, one can explain second-degree murder by clarifying intent, consequence, and legal judgment.

These examples are not about simplifying for the sake of being shallow. They are about building a communication channel. A professional who cannot be understood may preserve their authority, but they fail to transfer knowledge. A professional who explains clearly helps others make better judgments, which is often the real purpose of expertise.

Good expression also requires knowing what level of precision is appropriate. The rule of 72 and the 4 percent rule are not complete theories, but they are useful mental tools. They allow ordinary people to understand compound interest or retirement withdrawal in a practical way. A good explanation does not include every detail. It includes enough structure for the audience to reason correctly.

This is especially important in cross-functional work. Engineers, lawyers, investors, managers, and designers often need to cooperate. If each side only speaks in its own professional dialect, collaboration becomes expensive. Clear explanation lowers the cost of trust and decision-making. It allows people with different backgrounds to stand on the same problem.

The lesson for me is that expression is part of professionalism, not decoration outside it. If I truly understand a complex issue, I should be able to identify the core, choose the right analogy, state the limits, and help others grasp what matters. Profound language is easy to hide behind. Clear language requires real understanding.
