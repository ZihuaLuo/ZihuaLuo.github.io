> People who truly get things done know how to cooperate first, then contribute.

After reading Reader Q&A 10, "Why Learn To Obey?" from Wu Jun's *Silicon Valley Letters 2: The Google Methodology*, my biggest reflection was this: obedience does not mean blind obedience, nor does it mean giving up independent thinking. Truly mature obedience means understanding how to follow rules, understand division of labor, and complete one's responsibility under a shared goal inside an organization or cooperative relationship. If a person only emphasizes the self but cannot cooperate with others, it is very hard to truly accomplish things in society.

The letter mentions that from childhood to adulthood, we have actually been learning to "follow instructions." When learning addition as children, teachers require us to calculate step by step. Later, when learning arithmetic operations, we also need to follow the rule that multiplication and division come before addition and subtraction. If someone insists on calculating randomly according to their own habits, the result may be wrong. Life is the same. When a doctor tells a patient to drink more water and rest, it seems like a simple instruction, but whether the patient follows it often determines recovery. When assembling furniture, following the manual step by step may look clumsy, but it reduces mistakes. Obedience is not having no thought. It is recognizing that rules and experience exist for a reason.

In society and work, obedience becomes even more important. An organization cannot have everyone acting only according to their own ideas. In software development, for example, if every engineer writes code according to personal habit and refuses to follow common standards, the project will be full of bugs and impossible for others to maintain. Google requiring new employees to practice code that fits company standards is not about suppressing individuality. It is about ensuring the whole system can cooperate and run. If a person refuses even basic standards, no matter how capable they are, they will struggle to become a reliable collaborator.

I think the deeper point of the letter is that it reminds us to distinguish obedience from blind obedience. Blind obedience accepts everything without judgment. Mature obedience is rational cooperation after understanding the larger situation. Obedience does not mean turning oneself into a person without ideas. It means first learning to enter the rules at the appropriate stage. Only after understanding rules does one have the qualification to improve them. Only after completing cooperation can one talk about creation and breakthrough. If one treats every rule as a constraint and every instruction as oppression from the beginning, that is often not independence, but immaturity.

This also explains why many people meet a ceiling in the workplace. Wu Jun mentions that how high a person can rise in an organization depends greatly on the height of their early growth curve. Some people are technically strong, but unwilling to learn obedience and cooperation, so they can only remain individual contributors. Others understand organizational goals, are willing to coordinate with others and support the team, and are therefore more likely to take on greater responsibility. Leadership is not commanding others from the beginning. It begins with knowing how to become a trustworthy collaborator.

The core reflection this letter left me with is that obedience is not losing face, but a basic ability for entering a system of cooperation. A person certainly needs independent thinking, but independence does not mean opposing everything. A person also needs individuality, but individuality does not mean ignoring rules. Truly mature people can understand rules and do their part within them; they can obey the larger situation while offering constructive suggestions at the right time. Learning obedience is not about becoming mediocre. It is about becoming capable of participating in larger cooperation and taking on more important responsibilities.
