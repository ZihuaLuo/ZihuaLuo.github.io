> The higher the cost, the more carefully people choose; the lower the cost, the easier it is to indulge bias.

In the third-week Q&A, Xue Zhaofeng continues discussing discrimination as selection under scarcity. The key question is not whether people can completely avoid standards, but whether the standards are reasonable, consistent, and connected to consequences.

One interesting question is whether people are more likely to discriminate when choosing a president or when choosing a spouse. Intuition may say the president is more important, so people should be more rational there. Economics suggests the opposite may happen. Choosing a spouse creates consequences one personally bears every day. One vote in a presidential election has tiny individual influence, and the cost of error is spread across society.

This shows that people are not equally serious in every choice. The closer the cost is to oneself, the more one is forced to look beneath the surface. The farther the cost is, the easier it is to rely on image, slogans, identity, and emotion.

The same is common in life. People compare carefully before buying something expensive, but casually buy small things. They examine credit, ability, and past behavior when choosing a long-term partner, but may rely on accent, school, appearance, or region in casual judgment. Bias exists partly because people process information cheaply.

Competition and responsibility often reduce discrimination more effectively than slogans. A company that rejects capable people loses talent. An investor who misses a good project because of appearance loses returns. A person who refuses excellent partners because of stereotypes loses opportunity. Those who bear consequences are usually more careful than observers.

The Q&A also discusses equality of opportunity. It sounds reasonable to say outcomes cannot be equal but opportunities should be. Yet opportunity is hard to calculate from one moment. Family background, parental education, language environment, growth atmosphere, and even genetic differences shape opportunity very early.

This does not mean society should stop pursuing fairer opportunities. It means we cannot understand equality of opportunity too simply. Giving everyone the same exam looks equal, but preparation conditions may be very different. The realistic goal is to reduce unnecessary, unreasonable, and improvable barriers.

Mature choice is not the absence of standards. It is knowing that standards carry costs. A wrong standard misses people; an abused standard hurts people; no standard makes scarce decisions impossible. Serious choice gives people less room for empty labels and more respect for real ability and real consequences.
