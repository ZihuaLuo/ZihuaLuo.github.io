> Not every temporary lack of results means the direction is wrong; often the result is simply lagging.

After reading Wu Jun's Letter 207 on the winter solstice, summer solstice, and delayed effects, the most important reminder for me is that change does not show itself immediately after action begins. A new force often has to offset the old trend first, accumulate quietly for a while, and only then become visible as a result.

After the winter solstice, the weather does not become warm at once. After the summer solstice, temperature does not fall immediately either. Temperature is not decided by one day of sunlight, but by the accumulated balance of heat over time. The same logic applies to study, career development, investing, and personal change.

Many people fail not because they did not work hard, but because they denied their effort too early. They try a direction for three months, see no result, and conclude that the direction must be wrong. Then they switch again before the new curve has had time to bend upward. What looks like constant optimization may actually be repeated interruption of compounding.

Real patience is not blind persistence. It is understanding the time structure of results. Between a decision and its visible outcome, there is often a period in which old inertia still works and the new system has not fully formed. If we judge only by short-term feedback, it is easy to mistake the darkest moment before dawn for proof that dawn will not come.

This is why young people need to be slower in changing direction. Choosing a direction deserves careful judgment, because a wrong direction does not become right just because we persist. But once a direction has been chosen after serious thinking, changing it should also be slow. Careers, skills, research, and investing are not instant-feedback systems.

The delayed effect also means that a person cannot seriously verify too many major directions in one life. If one decision needs years before it can reveal its value, then frequent switching creates the illusion of many choices while preventing any curve from reaching its upward turn.

The lesson for me is that long-termism is not passive waiting. It is choosing a direction with enough evidence, improving it gradually, and allowing time to finish its work. Not every lack of result means failure. Sometimes the curve is still below the surface.
