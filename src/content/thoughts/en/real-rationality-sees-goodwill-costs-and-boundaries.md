> People are not rational at every moment, but when the cost is high enough they naturally become cautious; mature judgment uses different standards in different contexts.

In the second-week Q&A, Xue Zhaofeng answers several questions that point to the same core idea: people are not abstract moral symbols. They make choices inside costs, incentives, and environments.

People are neither always rational nor always irrational. They often decide how carefully to think based on the cost of being wrong. If two chicken legs in a cafeteria differ by half a yuan, many people compare carefully because the mistake costs money. If asked whether Earth is three billion or five billion years old, many people do not care because a wrong answer does not immediately affect life.

This explains why poor ideas can stay in the mind for a long time. Bad glasses make vision blurry; bad shoes hurt after a few steps. But a wrong idea that does not create immediate pain can remain uncorrected. Learning is not only adding knowledge; it is becoming more sensitive to the cost of false beliefs.

Even irrational behavior often contains calculation. Losing one's temper is irrational, but people more often lose temper toward subordinates or close family than toward bosses and customers. What looks like total loss of control may be loss of control inside a safe range. Behind many personality problems is also a cost judgment.

There is no fixed answer to whether following the crowd or being different leads to success. A trend can reward followers, but trends can also change too fast. A minority path can look foolish at first and visionary later. After success, people can always tell a smooth story. The real question is not the label, but the environment and conditions.

Market society does not necessarily make relationships colder. In the past, close acquaintance networks often carried insurance, contracting, and service functions because formal markets were weak. Today, commercial insurance, services, and division of labor reduce forced dependence among relatives and neighbors. Relationships are reorganized, not necessarily emptied.

Charity also requires clearer boundaries. Funding a poor and brilliant student may produce visible returns, but that can look more like investment than charity. Real charity often faces people who are harder to change, have fewer opportunities, and are less visible to markets. That does not mean ignoring efficiency; it means distinguishing investment from charity.

The larger lesson is that goodwill, cost, and boundary must be seen together. Good charity is not what makes donors feel kind, but what gives truly weaker people more effective help. Mature rationality does not erase warmth; it asks what standard fits each situation.
