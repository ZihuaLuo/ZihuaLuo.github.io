> Environmental protection is not rejecting all pollution, but finding a better balance among different costs.

In Lecture 024, Xue Zhaofeng uses the Coase theorem to reinterpret environmental protection. Many people assume that if something protects the environment, it should be pursued at any cost. Economics asks who bears the cost, whether the effect is truly better, and whether reducing one kind of pollution creates a larger cost elsewhere.

The comparison between cars and horse-drawn carriages is striking. Today we worry about car emissions and may romanticize a pre-car age. But horse transport produced manure pollution, noise, dead-horse disposal problems, and traffic chaos. Cars did not create pollution from a perfect world; they replaced another dirty system.

Environmental judgment cannot compare today's pollution with an imagined perfect state. It has to compare real alternatives. Few choices are pollution-free. More often, society chooses among different forms of pollution, resource use, health, convenience, and quality of life.

The Yellowstone wolves example shows that more nature is not automatically better. Environmentalists may want more wolves; ranchers may want none because wolves kill livestock. A better solution is compensation: if wolves kill livestock, wolf supporters bear the cost. Then every position faces its real price.

This is the value of Coasean pricing. It makes every preference confront its cost. People who want a more primitive ecology should recognize possible damage to ranchers; producers who want industrial efficiency should pay for pollution and external effects.

Command-style environmentalism can backfire. If factories must install expensive filters, they may stop buying cleaner but more expensive low-sulfur coal and switch to cheaper, dirtier coal because the filter cost has already been paid. A policy may look green while worsening incentives.

Even cloth versus paper napkins requires total-cost thinking. Cloth can be reused, but washing, drying, disinfecting, starching, labor, water, and electricity all matter. Paper is disposable, but may sometimes have lower total cost. At the same time, a fine restaurant's cloth napkin may create experience and memory that also have value.

Mature environmentalism is not refusing enjoyment or returning to the past. It recognizes that environment, time, wealth, health, and emotion all compete for resources. Responsible protection requires calculation, comparison, and willingness to bear cost.
