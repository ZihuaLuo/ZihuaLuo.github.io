> Many mistakes do not come from evil intentions, but from trusting wishes without seeing how people will respond.

In Lecture 004, Xue Zhaofeng explains a major difference between economics and ordinary moral judgment. Many people first ask who is good and who is bad. If a result is bad, they assume a bad person must have caused it. Economics asks a harder question: why do good wishes sometimes produce bad results?

This pattern appears everywhere. When wages are low, people blame capitalists and demand forced wage increases. When housing prices rise, they blame speculators and demand suppression. When inflation appears, they blame merchants and demand price controls. The judgment feels intuitive because it turns a complex problem into a story about someone doing evil.

But economic problems are rarely solved by finding villains. The influence of a single bad person is limited because others react, defend themselves, and resist. The larger force is often a rule created with good intentions that changes everyone's behavior. Once the rule lands, people adjust to it, and the final result may run against the original wish.

Minimum wage laws are a typical example. The wish is to protect low-income workers and raise their income. But if the legal wage is above what firms can bear, firms may hire fewer people or hire only more experienced and productive workers. The people who most need job opportunities may be excluded. The problem is not that protecting the weak is wrong, but that wishes do not automatically become results.

Environmental actions can work the same way. Asking every household to wash bottles before recycling looks responsible. But if the waste facility will wash and sort everything again, the household-level washing may waste more water, electricity, and time. A gesture that looks green may not actually reduce total cost or environmental burden.

This is why economics does not stop at passing a law. Many people see an undesirable phenomenon, demand a regulation, and treat the law as the end of the story. From an economic view, a new rule is not a period; it is a colon. People will adapt, evade, shift costs, and sometimes create new problems.

The same lesson applies to personal decisions. A person wants efficiency and fills the calendar. A person wants discipline and creates an extreme routine. A person wants to help others and makes too many decisions for them. The intentions are good, but if human inertia, capacity, and backlash are ignored, the result may be resistance or collapse.

Mature judgment cannot stop at what I hope will happen. It has to ask what others will do, where costs will move, and whether the opposite result may appear. This is not a rejection of goodwill. It is goodwill accepting reality. Responsible thinking moves from judging intentions to judging consequences.
