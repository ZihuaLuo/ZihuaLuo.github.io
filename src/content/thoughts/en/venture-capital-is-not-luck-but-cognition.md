> Real venture capital does not invest in immediate profit, but in future possibility.

After reading Letter 050, "The Origin and Myth of Venture Capital," from Wu Jun's *Silicon Valley Letters 2: The Google Methodology*, my biggest reflection was this: on the surface, venture capital seems to invest money, but in reality it invests in a judgment about the future. When many people hear "venture capital," they easily think of sudden wealth, bubbles, and luck, as if one successful bet on a star company can produce astonishing returns. But this letter reminds me that good venture capital is not simple gambling. It is the attempt to turn an uncertain future into a risk that can be carried, based on technical understanding, industry judgment, and institutional design.

The letter mentions that J.P. Morgan once invested in Tesla and Edison, and that Mark Twain missed the investment opportunity in Bell's telephone. These stories show that when a new technology first appears, most people find it very difficult to judge its value. Looking back today, we treat electricity, the telephone, and semiconductors as obvious world-changing technologies. But when they were first born, many people did not know where they would go. The hardest part of venture capital is exactly here: it faces not a proven result, but a possibility that has not yet taken shape.

The story of the Traitorous Eight founding Fairchild Semiconductor is especially representative. When Noyce and the others left Shockley Semiconductor, they had technology, a team, and ideas, but lacked capital. Arthur Rock was originally a traditional financial manager, but he was moved by the future prospects of semiconductors and eventually helped bring in the Fairchild family's investment. This process showed me that venture capital is not a one-sided act of giving money. It is a combination of capital, technology, and entrepreneurs. Without capital, technology struggles to become a company. Without technology, capital only circles in place. Without people willing to bear uncertainty, new industries are difficult to create.

What is even more worth thinking about in this letter is the institutional arrangement of early venture capital. Fairchild provided loans, received decision-making power in the company, and could later buy back shares at an agreed price. This design gave entrepreneurs startup capital while allowing investors to control risk. It was different from ordinary lending because the investor carried the risk of company failure. But it was also different from mature venture capital today because the upside for entrepreneurs was limited. Fairchild later succeeded, but Noyce and the others still eventually left. This shows that a healthy venture capital mechanism must build a more reasonable sharing of interests between investors and entrepreneurs.

This made me realize that the core of venture capital is not "high risk must mean high return," but how risk and return are distributed. If investors only want to control risk without leaving entrepreneurs enough room for upside, entrepreneurs will lack long-term motivation. If entrepreneurs only pursue freedom without accepting capital discipline, the company will also struggle to grow. A good venture capital system is, in essence, a cooperative structure built inside uncertainty: capital carries risk, entrepreneurs contribute technology and execution, and both sides share the gains of future growth.

The letter also mentions that more money in venture capital does not necessarily lead to better results. The number of successful projects does not rise in step with short-term increases in capital. When there is too much money, it can push up the prices of projects that were not worth investing in and bring people who do not understand technology or industry into the market. As a result, investment returns may fall. This is very realistic. People often assume that an investment industry can succeed as long as it has enough capital. But the truly scarce resource is not money; it is judgment. When capital is excessive, money chases concepts. When cognition is insufficient, risk gets packaged as opportunity.

This also explains why truly excellent venture investors must understand technology and industry, not just accounting. Venture capital is different from public-market investing. It faces companies that are not yet mature and technologies that are not yet fully verified. If an investor only looks at short-term financial data, it is hard to judge the potential of an early-stage company. What matters is whether the investor can understand technical trends, judge team capability, and see the future space an industry may form. High returns do not come from blind risk-taking. They come from understanding a future that others cannot yet see clearly.

The core reflection this letter left me with is that venture capital is not gambling, but a cognitive competition about the future. It needs capital, but it needs vision even more. It needs the willingness to take risk, but even more it needs an understanding of where risk comes from. Truly valuable investment is not chasing trends or simply believing that "high risk means high return." It is finding a reasonable connection among technology, industry, team, and institutions. Venture capital matters because it gives capital a chance to support things that have not yet been proven, and gives future innovation a chance to move from idea to reality.
