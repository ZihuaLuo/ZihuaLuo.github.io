> The value of institutions is not to make every decision for people, but to make cooperation easier.

In Lecture 025, Xue Zhaofeng formally introduces transaction costs. The Coase theorem often says that if transaction costs are zero, resources will end up in the hands of those who value them most regardless of initial ownership. Reality is not like that. People must communicate, negotiate, verify information, build trust, sign contracts, and monitor execution.

Transaction cost exists as soon as there is more than one person. A one-person society needs no notarization, locks, lawsuits, or monitoring. In a many-person society, trust, communication, and enforcement problems appear immediately. People may not know who the other party is, may not trust what is said, and may not follow through even after agreement.

The pollution-filter example shows the problem. On a blackboard, if five households each suffer one hundred yuan of damage and a filter costs only fifty yuan, everyone can contribute ten yuan and solve the problem. In real life, some households live closer, some farther, some have children, and some are less affected. Each person worries about paying too much or being taken advantage of. A good solution may fail because negotiation costs are too high.

Adding elevators to old apartment buildings is similar. Upper-floor residents benefit more, lower-floor residents may benefit less or even lose light, and neighbors disagree about cost sharing. The solution may be obvious in principle, but agreement among dozens of households can be expensive.

This shows that many failures are not caused by the absence of a good plan, but by the inability to reach and execute a plan cheaply. A solution can be reasonable on paper and still fail in reality because organization and enforcement are too costly.

Because transaction costs are everywhere, law, institutions, customs, and habits matter. If costs were zero, people could constantly renegotiate rights. Since they are not zero, society must set boundaries in advance: who owns property, who bears responsibility, what is forbidden, and what requires compensation.

Drunk-driving rules are a good example. If transaction costs were zero, pedestrians could negotiate with every potential drunk driver before going outside. That is impossible. Drivers know before drinking that they may drive, so they are the lower-cost avoiders. The rule restricts drivers to lower total social cost.

Personal reputation also lowers transaction costs. People who are punctual, reliable, and clear about boundaries are easier to cooperate with. People who change commitments, hide information, or create uncertainty raise the cost of cooperation even if they are talented. Trust is an asset because it makes cooperation cheaper.
