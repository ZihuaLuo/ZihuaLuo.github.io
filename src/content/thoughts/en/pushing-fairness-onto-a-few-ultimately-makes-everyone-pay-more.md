> Real goodwill does not push prices down by command; it admits costs honestly and lets society bear them transparently.

Lecture 046 examines rent control. Its intention is easy to understand: help tenants, especially lower-income tenants, afford housing. But when rent is pushed below the level that reflects scarcity, the cost does not disappear.

Landlords may reduce maintenance, stop building, convert rentals to other uses, or screen tenants more aggressively. Existing tenants who receive controlled rent may benefit, but new tenants face fewer available units and more hidden competition.

In this way, fairness is pushed onto a small group. Landlords are asked to bear costs that society as a whole wants to solve. But because they can respond, the burden spreads back through reduced supply, lower quality, informal fees, and stricter access.

The result can be the opposite of the intention. People who already occupy protected units gain stability, while outsiders, young people, new migrants, and the poor without connections may find housing even harder to obtain.

This does not mean society should ignore housing affordability. It means the cost of helping people should be recognized and shared transparently. If a society believes housing support is necessary, it should design mechanisms that do not quietly destroy supply or push hidden costs onto those with less access.

Goodwill becomes dangerous when it refuses to count cost. A rule can sound compassionate while making the system more rigid, less supplied, and less open to newcomers.

Real fairness is not achieved by commanding a few people to absorb social costs. It is achieved by admitting the cost and building mechanisms that help people without making the whole system poorer.
