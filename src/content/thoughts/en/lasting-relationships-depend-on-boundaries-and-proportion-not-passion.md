> Relationships last not because people are always warm, but because both sides understand boundaries, proportion, and the cost of closeness.

Wu Jun's discussion of long-lasting relationships reminded me that many people overestimate passion and underestimate boundaries. A relationship that begins warmly may not last, while a relationship that seems restrained may endure for a long time because both sides know how to keep proper proportion.

Passion can start a connection, but it cannot manage one. When people are too close too quickly, expectations rise faster than responsibility. Favors become obligations, care becomes control, and disappointment appears once the other person cannot continuously satisfy the imagined role.

Boundaries are not coldness. They are the structure that lets trust survive. If two people know what can be asked, what should be refused, what belongs to each side, and what should not be taken for granted, the relationship becomes easier to maintain. Clear distance can protect closeness.

Many relationship problems come from confusing sincerity with unlimited access. Someone believes that because we are friends, I can ask anything. Because we are family, I can interfere. Because I helped you before, you must respond forever. These assumptions turn affection into pressure.

Proportion also matters in communication. Not every emotion needs to be expressed immediately. Not every favor should be accepted. Not every conflict deserves escalation. Mature people know when to move closer, when to step back, when to speak, and when to leave space.

The lesson for me is that lasting relationships are built not by constant intensity, but by stable expectations. Real closeness does not erase boundaries. It respects them well enough that both sides can remain in the relationship without exhaustion.
