> Housing price is not an isolated number; behind it are people's judgments about opportunity, resources, and the future.

Lecture 049 asks whether rising housing prices are good or bad. The question seems to be about real estate, but it is really about how we understand price. Price is not the cause by itself; it is a signal of underlying conditions.

High housing prices often reflect dense opportunity: jobs, schools, medical resources, infrastructure, safety, networks, and expectations about the future. People are not paying only for walls and floors. They are paying for access to a city and its possibilities.

This does not mean high prices are always good. They can create pressure, exclude newcomers, and increase inequality. But simply treating price increases as the enemy may miss the source of demand. If the city has valuable opportunities, people will compete for access.

Suppressing the visible price does not create more opportunity. It may only change who gets access. If supply cannot expand and demand remains strong, allocation moves to other rules: connections, waiting, qualifications, or hidden payments.

The better question is how to increase opportunity and supply rather than only fight the price signal. Better transportation, more housing, stronger surrounding cities, and broader public services can reduce pressure by changing real conditions.

For individuals, housing price also reveals tradeoffs. A cheaper place may cost more in commute, opportunity, education, or network access. An expensive place may be worthwhile only if the opportunities behind it are real and usable.

Housing prices reflect the density of urban opportunity. To understand them, look beyond the number to the system of resources and expectations behind it.
