> To judge something, do not only look at its intention; look at the consequences it creates over time.

After reading Letter 106, "The Other Side Of Invention Through The Invention Of Heroin," from Wu Jun's *Silicon Valley Letters 2: The Google Methodology*, my biggest reflection was this: many human inventions and choices are not necessarily born from malice. Some may even begin with good intentions. But good intentions do not automatically guarantee good results. Mature judgment does not only ask whether someone wanted to do something good. It also asks what long-term effects the action will create, and whether limited understanding, profit incentives, or loss of control in use may eventually create larger problems.

In the letter, Wu Jun discusses the German Bayer scientist Hoffmann. He invented aspirin to ease his father's pain, and heroin was also developed in the hope of finding a less addictive and more effective painkiller. In that sense, aspirin and heroin began from similar intentions and even similar methods: both started from substances already known in nature, hoping to preserve therapeutic effects while reducing side effects. But the results were completely different. Aspirin became a drug that benefited humanity, while heroin became a deeply harmful narcotic.

This contrast made me realize that the world does not operate simply according to "good intentions" and "bad intentions." Many things become dangerous not because someone wanted to harm people from the start, but because people at the time could not see the full consequences. When heroin was first invented, Bayer conducted animal tests, considered it effective and low in toxicity, and even sold it as a cough medicine. Only later did people discover that it metabolizes into morphine in the human body and causes severe addiction. The side effects of an invention may not appear immediately. They may only become visible after time, scale, and different uses amplify them.

This also made me think of many real-world choices. In technology, business, and personal life, we are often attracted by short-term effects. A method seems to solve a problem quickly. A tool seems to improve efficiency. A choice seems to bring immediate benefits. But whether it creates dependency over time, weakens judgment, or introduces new risks is often ignored. Many times, humans try to solve one problem and accidentally create a larger one.

What is even more worth warning against in the letter is Bayer's behavior. After the harms of heroin gradually became visible, the company did not immediately stop promoting it. Driven by profit, it continued to hide the problem and even sold it to other markets after bans appeared in some regions. This shows that professional knowledge and industry status do not naturally equal moral reliability. The more professional a person or institution is, and the more influence they have over the public, the greater responsibility they should carry. If expertise is captured by profit, the harm can become even greater.

This letter also made me rethink my understanding of progress and innovation. I used to easily see invention, efficiency, technology, and new methods as naturally positive. But this letter reminds me that every form of progress needs to be tested. Responsible innovation does not only ask, "Can it be done?" It also asks, "What consequences will it create?" It does not only look at immediate effectiveness, but also at long-term side effects. The more powerful something is, the more it needs boundaries and constraints.

So the core reflection this letter gave me is that goodwill cannot replace outcomes, ability cannot replace responsibility, and innovation cannot replace judgment. We cannot ignore risks simply because something began with a good intention. We also cannot stop asking about long-term costs simply because an invention looks effective. Just as character is revealed over time, our understanding of things also needs time. Reliable judgment often requires evidence, time, and sustained alertness toward side effects.

In the future, whether in study, work, or when facing new technologies and opportunities, I hope I can add one more layer of thinking: What problem does this solve? What problem might it create? Is it effective in the short term, and is it healthy in the long term? Are there risks hidden behind it? Maturity is not refusing progress. It is embracing progress while keeping clarity and respect for consequences.
