> Cleverness can help people do things faster, but vision determines what they choose to do.

After reading Wu Jun's supplement to *Attitude*, I was struck by the difference between cleverness and vision. Cleverness helps people optimize within a known frame. Vision determines whether the frame is worth entering at all. Many people are capable, diligent, and technically strong, but they still choose problems with limited future because they only see the immediate payoff.

The letter mentions a startup founded by graduates from MIT and Harvard. The team was smart, the technology was strong, and the business was related to high-frequency trading. But Wu Jun did not invest. The reason was not that the founders lacked ability. It was that the ambition of the problem was too small. The company might make money, but it was unlikely to change the world or create broad social value.

This reminds me of John Doerr's way of evaluating companies: if this company succeeds, how will the world be different? This question is simple, but it immediately raises the standard of choice. Google, Apple, and Facebook were not valuable only because their founders were intelligent. They chose problems that could reshape how people search, communicate, create, and organize information.

A famous school background can provide resources and credibility, but it cannot replace vision. Some people use elite education to compete for small advantages in a narrow game. Others use the same education to enter harder, larger, and more meaningful problems. The difference is not merely ability. It is the size of the question they are willing to carry.

For individuals, this lesson is equally important. We should not only ask whether we can do something well. We should also ask whether it is worth doing well. A person may become efficient at tasks that do not compound, or successful in fields that do not create real future value. Without vision, effort can become a refined form of narrowness.

The upper limit of choice is determined by what a person can see. Cleverness may help us win today's competition, but vision determines whether today's competition is the right one. The better question is not always "how can I move faster?" Sometimes it is "is this road wide enough for the person I want to become?"
