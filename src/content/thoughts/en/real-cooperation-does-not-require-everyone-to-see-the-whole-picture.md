> The miracle of modern society is not that one person can do everything, but that countless strangers can complete one task without knowing one another.

In Lecture 007, Xue Zhaofeng uses the story of a pencil to explain the power of markets and division of labor. A pencil looks simple: wood, lead, paint, a metal ring, and an eraser. But once we trace its parts backward, we find timber, mining, transport, metal refining, chemicals, machinery, energy, food supply, and generations of accumulated knowledge.

The astonishing point is that no single person knows everything needed to make a pencil. The lumber worker may not know how graphite is processed. The metal worker may not know how pencils are sold. The transporter may not know that a material will eventually become part of a pencil. Everyone completes a small section of work, and the pieces combine into something cheap and useful.

Modern abundance is not created by a few all-powerful people. It is created by division of labor. A capable hunter may know how to make soap, repair tools, hunt, and start a fire, but that does not make his life richer than that of an ordinary city resident. The city resident stands on a vast cooperative system and can use products and services created by countless strangers.

This is why markets matter. A market is not merely a place of buying and selling; it is a mechanism for coordinating strangers. People who make a pencil may live in different countries, speak different languages, and hold different beliefs. They do not need to know or like one another. By responding to prices, demand, and interests, they cooperate anyway.

People often underestimate ordinary goods because they are cheap and common. Pencils, clothes, phones, coffee, subway rides, and delivery meals look like background items. But behind each is a huge network of knowledge. Low price does not mean simplicity; often it means the division of labor has become mature enough to make complexity feel effortless.

This also changes how we understand personal ability. A person should improve, but nobody succeeds outside systems. Entering a good school, company, city, or industry is partly entering a more efficient division-of-labor network. In a small system, even a capable person may be limited; in a large system, doing one small part well can create more value.

The pencil story is not really about stationery. It reminds us how society works. Prosperity does not require everyone to know everything. It requires people to focus on what they can do well and connect their efforts through trust, rules, exchange, and markets.
