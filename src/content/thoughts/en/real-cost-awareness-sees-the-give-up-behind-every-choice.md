> Judging whether something is worth doing requires looking not only at immediate gains, but also at the opportunities given up.

In the fourth-week Q&A, Xue Zhaofeng connects several questions that look unrelated: why building aircraft may cost China more than producing socks, why medicine becomes more expensive, why repeated attempts are not free, and why sunk cost should not keep controlling a decision. The common thread is cost. To understand cost is not to ask only what I want to do, but what I give up by doing it.

The sock and aircraft example shows the logic of comparative advantage. An airplane looks more advanced than socks, so people may assume producing aircraft is more valuable. But the real question is the best alternative forgone. If workers can efficiently produce socks that the world is willing to buy, then giving up socks has a real cost. If accepted passenger aircraft cannot yet be produced, not producing them may not give up much real opportunity.

Cost has to be an achievable alternative, not an imagined identity. A person cannot say the cost of becoming a teacher is giving up a career as a basketball star if that was never a real option. The same is true for countries and individuals. What matters is not the label that sounds more advanced, but what is most productive under current conditions.

Career choice works similarly. A lower-paying job chosen for interest may not simply be a loss, because satisfaction, autonomy, growth, and lifestyle are also part of return. Preferences are not fixed forever. Sometimes a person develops interest after entering a system. The better question is not only whether I like this now, but whether I am willing to become the kind of person this system produces over time.

Medicine prices also reveal cost structure. Drug development is long, risky, and regulated, but another reason prices rise is that the user and payer are often separated. Patients care about results, doctors care about treatment, and insurers or public systems pay. When price signals are weakened, cost sensitivity declines. By contrast, services like LASIK, often paid directly by patients, can fall in price under competition.

Repeated attempts also have costs. Saying never give up sounds inspiring, but economics asks what each attempt costs. If each attempt has a one percent chance of success, trying again may raise the chance slightly, but it also consumes new time, money, and opportunity. Persistence becomes mature only when the marginal return is still worth the additional cost.

The same idea returns to sunk cost. Airfare already paid should not make a traveler buy unnecessary goods abroad just to feel the trip was worthwhile. The question should always return to the present: from now on, is the next unit of investment still worth it? Real cost awareness frees people from the past and forces every decision to face what is being given up now.
