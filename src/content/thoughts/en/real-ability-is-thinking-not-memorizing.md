> An interview tests not the standard answer, but the process of solving a problem.

After reading Letter 026, "Why Microsoft Asked This Interview Question: Should Manhole Covers Be Round Or Square?" from Wu Jun's *Silicon Valley Letters 2: The Google Methodology*, my biggest reflection was this: real ability is not about how many answers a person has memorized. It is about whether, when facing an unfamiliar problem, they can analyze it in an orderly way, explain it, and form their own judgment. Many people are used to understanding learning as "memorizing answers," but this letter reminds me that what the real world values is often not the answer itself, but a person's thinking process.

The letter begins by discussing interview questions from Google and Microsoft. Many people ask whether practicing problems is useful. If practice only means memorizing answers, its meaning is limited. A person may memorize the answer to a problem, but that does not mean they truly understand the logic behind it. Wu Jun mentions that companies like Google do not simply test students' knowledge points. They examine the thinking process and the ability to apply knowledge. For example, in the question about how to test the height from which a glass ball will break, if the interviewee merely recites the answer, follow-up questions quickly reveal whether they truly understand it. But if they understand the strategy and logic, they can continue reasoning under different conditions. The key is not "what is the answer," but "why should it be done this way?"

This makes me think of much of the education we have received, which often emphasizes standard answers. In exams, a question has one correct answer, and during review we look for fixed routines. This kind of training is useful because it helps people master basic knowledge. But the problem is that if a person is only used to finding answers among existing answers, they may not know where to begin when facing open-ended problems. Many problems in real life and work do not have clear standard answers like exam questions. Whether a plan is good depends on specific conditions. Whether a choice is right requires considering goals, resources, risks, and constraints. A person who only memorizes answers loses direction when facing change.

Microsoft's question, "Should a manhole cover be round or square?" reflects the same logic. On the surface, it is a brain teaser, and the correct answer seems to be "round." A round manhole cover will not fall into the hole no matter how it is placed, while a square cover may fall in if tilted. A round cover is also easier to process, move, and install. If a person can explain these reasons, it shows they not only know the answer but can explain the issue from geometric, engineering, and practical perspectives.

But the interesting part of the question is that it is not meant to produce a single absolutely correct answer. Wu Jun notes that if someone insists a square manhole cover could work and provides strong reasons, the interviewer may also see something distinctive in that person. Engineering problems do not always have one absolute solution. Round covers are more suitable in most situations, but square covers may have advantages in specific scenarios. A good engineer does not simply memorize a conclusion. A good engineer understands under what conditions a relatively better solution can be given.

This letter made me realize that real thinking training is not about making people good at answering questions. It is about making people learn to ask follow-up questions. Why is the manhole cover round? Why is this plan safer? Why is this method cheaper? Are there special situations where another plan would be better? When a person can explain their judgment from multiple angles, the answer is no longer memory. It becomes the result of thinking. By contrast, if someone can only say, "because the internet says so," then even if they answer correctly, they may not truly have the ability to solve problems.

This also makes me reflect on my own learning method. Many times, I unconsciously pursue the "correct answer." I want to know the conclusion quickly and finish the task quickly. But if I stop at the answer, I can easily overlook the thinking process. Truly important learning should continue asking after the answer is known: how did this answer come about? Under what conditions does it apply? Are there exceptions? If the question changes slightly, can I reason it out again? Only then can knowledge move beyond memory and become an ability that can transfer and be used.

The core reflection this letter left me with is that answers themselves are not scarce. What is scarce is the ability to understand the reasoning behind them. Practice problems, exams, and interviews are all forms. What truly matters is whether a person can build a line of thought under uncertainty, find evidence under complex conditions, and clearly explain their judgment. In the future, whether in study or work, I should not be satisfied with "I know the answer." I should pursue "I know why." What allows a person to go farther is not how many problems they have memorized, but whether they can still think clearly, independently, and logically when facing new problems.
