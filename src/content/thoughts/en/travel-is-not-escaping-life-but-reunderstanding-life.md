> What real travel brings back is not photos, but a broader self.

After reading Letter 046, "The Meaning of Vacation Travel," from Wu Jun's *Silicon Valley Letters 2: The Google Methodology*, my biggest reflection was this: the meaning of travel is not only relaxing in another place, nor is it only taking photos, checking in at attractions, and buying souvenirs. Truly valuable travel allows a person to temporarily step out of their familiar life system, then understand the world and themselves again through direct perception, other people's experiences, and changes in environment.

In this letter, Wu Jun mentions that there are roughly three ways to acquire knowledge: direct perception, being told by others, and logical reasoning. Reading mostly belongs to being told by others. Thinking and analysis belong to logical reasoning. Travel is closer to direct perception. It cannot fully replace reading, but it also contains things reading cannot replace. Many things remain at the conceptual level if we only understand them through books and news. But when a person actually goes to a place and sees local people's lives, streets, order, wealth gaps, and cultural habits, understanding becomes concrete. Travel gives abstract knowledge a real texture.

The letter says the first meaning of travel is to broaden one's experience. For example, if a person has never left their own living environment, they can easily take their familiar experience as the whole world. Some people say Chinese people are anxious. Some say Americans are optimistic. Some say a country is backward or advanced. If one only listens to others retelling these things, it is easy to form a one-sided judgment. But if one truly goes out and observes how different societies operate, one will find that many things cannot be summarized in a single simple sentence. Travel makes people realize that there is not only one way to live, and not only the logic they already know.

The second meaning is that travel can help us step outside our usual circles of life and work. When a person stays in one environment for a long time, their thinking slowly becomes closed, and they may not even know where their limits are. The letter mentions that changing environments can help people reexamine themselves and walk out of difficulty. Many artists and historical figures gained new inspiration through changes in environment. This point makes me think that many times we are not incapable; we are trapped by our original environment. Facing the same people, the same tasks, and the same problems every day, it is easy over time to believe life can only be this way. The meaning of travel is that it breaks this inertia and lets people see other possibilities.

The third meaning is that travel helps us understand ourselves. The world is like a mirror. When a person goes to different places and encounters different situations, they may see their own personality, abilities, and boundaries more clearly. For example, during travel one may face language barriers, route changes, disrupted plans, and the need to coordinate with others. On the surface, these are small travel troubles. In reality, they also test a person's organization, communication, adaptability, and emotional management. In familiar environments, people easily assume they know themselves well. Only in unfamiliar environments do they discover what they are truly good at and what they are afraid of.

However, I think the deeper point of this letter is that we should not turn travel into another form of formalism. Today, much travel has become taking photos, posting on social media, and collecting attractions, as if the number of countries and cities visited proves that one's vision has expanded. But if a person simply arrives somewhere without observing, thinking, or recording, travel may be only physical movement while the mind has not really changed. Wu Jun mentions that travel requires records, not only photographs, but also one's own thoughts. What is truly worth bringing back is not a photo proving "I was here," but what new understanding this place produced in me.

This also makes me think about why modern people need vacations. Many people feel they are too busy to travel, and even see vacations as a waste of time. But if a person operates only inside work and daily tasks for a long period, they can easily lose their sensitivity to life. Vacation is not avoiding responsibility; it is helping people regain energy. Travel is not leaving reality; it is looking at reality from another angle. Only by occasionally stepping outside one's original rhythm can a person discover what has trapped them and where they truly want to go.

The core reflection this letter left me with is that the meaning of travel is not distance itself, but how distance changes the way we see the world. Reading lets people know other people's experiences. Thinking helps people organize their own logic. Travel lets people use their bodies to verify the complexity of the world. Truly good travel does not only relax people for a few days. It lets them return with a wider vision, deeper understanding, and a clearer self.
