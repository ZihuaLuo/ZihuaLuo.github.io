> Real intelligence means being willing to become a reliable "fool."

After reading Letter 010, "God Likes The Slow People, Part II," from Wu Jun's *Silicon Valley Letters 2: The Google Methodology*, my biggest reflection was this: people often overestimate the value of small clever tricks and underestimate the power of reliability, kindness, and focus. "God likes the slow people" does not mean that the slower a person is, the better. It means that in real life and long-term cooperation, people who do not play tricks, do not chase petty advantages, and do not try to win at every point often go farther.

The example of buying computers in Zhongguancun is representative. The manager of Company ABC originally thought he had found a money-making opportunity: the other party was willing to accept a price above market value, as long as the extra money was returned as a kickback. The manager thought he could both sell the goods and get a side benefit, so he accepted this seemingly "smart" arrangement. Later, the other party claimed that handing over cash was inconvenient and asked to take part of the kickback first. The manager thought the request sounded reasonable. Because he had already withheld 5,000 yuan, he was even more likely to believe that the deal was still under his control. A few days later, he discovered that the other party had no purchasing ability and had never intended to buy the computers. They had simply used his greed to cheat him out of the kickback.

The most ironic part of this example is that the manager was not cheated because he was simply ignorant. He was cheated because he believed he was smarter than the other party. He thought he could profit from a gray transaction, but he did not realize that once a person accepts improper benefits, he also puts himself into a larger risk. This is the problem with small cleverness: it makes people think they are exploiting the rules, when in reality they may be exploited by someone who understands the rules better. The more a person wants to take advantage of others, the more easily they can be designed into the trap.

When Wu Jun later emphasizes being a "fool," it is not because he does not understand interests. It is because he sees farther. In business and cooperation, if every time a person wants to earn a little more than others and take a little less responsibility, they may seem to win in the short term, but they damage trust in the long term. Once cooperation loses trust, it becomes difficult to continue. By contrast, someone who is willing to protect others' interests and make partners feel secure may look less calculating, but that kind of "foolishness" is itself a source of credit. People continue working with him not because he is the best at calculation, but because they do not need to constantly defend themselves when working with him.

This letter also mentions an important point: people, like computers, are not measured by how many beginnings they create, but by how many endings they complete. Modern society easily creates the illusion that handling many things at the same time means strong ability, and that a fuller schedule means greater value. But in reality, opening too many threads often means attention is fragmented and work is left half-finished. Too many processes can crash a computer, and too many simultaneous responsibilities can also break a person. Truly efficient people do not necessarily do everything. They know what should be done, what should not be done, and they finish what they start.

From this perspective, "foolishness" is actually a wisdom that resists petty cleverness. It is not dullness. It is refusing to over-consume life through calculation, bargain hunting, and ineffective busyness. If a person always wants to take a little advantage from others, what they may eventually lose is trust. If a person always wants to finish many things at once, what they may lose is quality. If a person always thinks they are smarter than others, they may ultimately be deceived by their own arrogance.

The reflection this letter left me with is that real intelligence is not constantly proving that I am better than others. It is knowing how to restrain greed, respect boundaries in cooperation, and respect the basic laws of doing work. Petty cleverness can bring short-term benefit, but reliability, kindness, and focus bring long-term value. Perhaps "God likes the slow people" because behind this kind of "slowness" there is a more stable quality: not being greedy, not being chaotic, not bluffing, doing right by people, and finishing the work.
