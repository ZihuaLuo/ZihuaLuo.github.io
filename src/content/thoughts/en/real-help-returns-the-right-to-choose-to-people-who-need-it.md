> Goodwill that makes all choices for others may end up helping distributors more than the vulnerable.

Lecture 048 compares in-kind subsidies and cash subsidies. The question behind it is practical and moral: if prices allocate goods efficiently, what should society do for people who do not have enough money?

In-kind support gives people specific goods, such as food, housing, or services. It can ensure the subsidy is used for the intended purpose. But it also assumes the giver knows what the recipient needs most. It can create waste, restriction, and opportunities for those who control distribution.

Cash support gives people choice. It respects the fact that each person's needs are different. One family may need food, another medicine, another transport, another time to look for work. Money allows recipients to decide according to their own circumstances.

The tradeoff is that cash may be used in ways the giver dislikes. But that concern reveals a deeper question: is the goal to help people live better, or to control how they live?

Real help should not turn the vulnerable into passive receivers of other people's decisions. If support removes their agency, it may protect them on the surface while weakening their ability to arrange their own lives.

This does not mean all in-kind support is wrong. In emergencies or specific public programs, it may be necessary. But as a principle, help should preserve choice whenever possible.

The best assistance gives people resources, dignity, and room to decide. It returns the right to choose to those who need help, instead of transferring that right to the people distributing help.
