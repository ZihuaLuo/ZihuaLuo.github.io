> The gap between experts and ordinary people is often not who works harder, but who sees which work does not need to be done.

After reading Letter 097, "Another Case For Doing Less: The Median Problem, And Communication Skills," from Wu Jun's *Silicon Valley Letters 2: The Google Methodology*, my biggest reflection was this: the root of efficiency is not making every step faster, but avoiding unnecessary work from the beginning. Many times, a person may look diligent, busy, and strong in execution, but if the direction is wrong, or if they do a large amount of work that did not need to be done, that effort itself becomes a source of inefficiency.

In the letter, Wu Jun uses a Google interview question to explain this idea: if there is a huge array, how can we find its median in the most efficient way? The question looks simple, but it contains many traps. First, the median is not the average. Second, a "huge array" means ordinary small-scale methods may not apply. Finally, "most efficient" means we cannot merely find a method that works; we need to search for a genuinely better method.

Many people first think of sorting the array and then taking the middle number. That method certainly solves the problem, but it does too much unnecessary work. The question only asks for the median; it does not ask for the complete order of every number from smallest to largest. In other words, among the numbers smaller than the median, who is smaller than whom does not matter. Among the numbers larger than the median, who is larger than whom does not matter either. Sorting calculates all of that irrelevant information, so it is naturally inefficient.

The better method Wu Jun mentions uses the idea of partitioning. We can randomly choose a number, put smaller numbers on the left and larger numbers on the right. If the two sides are balanced, it is the median. If they are not balanced, we continue searching only on the side that contains the median. This shrinks the problem each time instead of fully sorting the whole array. For a billion numbers, sorting may require around a trillion calculations, while a partition-based method may require only several billion. The efficiency gap can be hundreds of times.

This example made me realize that true experts do not merely finish the task; they can see which steps are unnecessary. When ordinary people face a problem, they often think first of the most intuitive and familiar method. Experts ask further questions: Does this method do extra work? What does the task truly require? Is there a way to approach the goal directly instead of completing a pile of irrelevant tasks along the way?

This is also useful for my own learning and work. In studying, I sometimes spend a lot of time organizing notes, improving formatting, and trying to be complete, while forgetting that the most important things are understanding the core logic and being able to apply it. In application planning, resume revision, or research work, if I do not understand the requirement at the beginning, I may spend a lot of time producing something that looks complete but does not match the goal. Effort matters, but understanding the problem before effort matters more.

The part of the letter about communication skills also impressed me. Wu Jun says that a good interviewee, if uncertain about the question, will first confirm what median means, or ask whether "most efficient" refers to time or memory. This shows that real ability is not only solving problems, but also communicating, clarifying, and confirming under uncertainty. By contrast, if someone rushes to act immediately, they may work hard and still fail because they misunderstood the problem.

This made me reflect on how I sometimes rush to begin, thinking it is better to start first and adjust later. But this letter reminds me that in important tasks, blind action does not mean efficiency. Asking the question clearly, defining the goal, and judging the constraints first is often the more mature way to work. Diligence in the wrong direction does not save time; it creates more rework.

So the core reflection this letter gave me is that efficiency comes from doing less useless work, not from mechanically increasing speed. What makes someone truly capable is not being busy forever, but being able to grasp the essence of a complex problem and know what must be done, what can be skipped, and what adds no value even if completed. The difference between first-rate people and ordinary people often lies here: ordinary people complete tasks; first-rate people optimize paths.

In the future, whether in study, work, or long-term planning, I hope I will not only ask, "Did I work hard?" I want to ask more important questions: Do I really understand the task? Is this step necessary? Is there a more direct and effective method? Am I solving the core problem, or using busyness to cover inefficiency? If I can keep training this way of thinking, I may truly use my time and energy where they have the greatest value.
