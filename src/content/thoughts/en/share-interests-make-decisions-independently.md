> Mature collaboration means sharing credit with others while carrying responsibility and judgment yourself.

In Letter 110, "Share Interests, Decide Independently," from Wu Jun's *Silicon Valley Letters 2: The Google Methodology*, the most valuable point is that it places two seemingly opposite abilities together: interests should be shared, while decisions should be independent. The first concerns breadth of mind, and the second concerns responsibility. If a person can decide independently but is unwilling to share interests, they may become an isolated strong person. If they only care for everyone's feelings but dare not make decisions, they may become an inefficient nice person.

The letter mentions that when Wu Jun studied at Johns Hopkins University, his advisor Professor Blair asked his senior classmate John to include "personal talk with Jun Wu" in a paper citation. By normal convention, private discussions may not always be formally cited. But Blair insisted because he wanted students to develop the habit of recognizing other people's contributions from the beginning. Later, Professor Jelinek also emphasized that if a colleague's work helped a paper, that colleague should be added as an author; if there was only a dinner or meeting discussion, thanks should still be expressed in acknowledgments. These small rules reflect a professional ethic: do not take other people's help for granted, and do not claim jointly produced results as one's own.

In large organizations, sharing interests is not politeness. It is the basis for forming joint force. Wu Jun later, when applying for patents at Google or publishing internal product news, would try to include collaborators' names. This might reduce his own bonus, but it helped colleagues and subordinates receive recognition. Whether a team can cooperate for the long term often does not depend on slogans. It depends on whether the person in charge is willing to let the light fall on others when credit appears.

The story in *Records Of The Grand Historian* about Chen Ping's comparison of Liu Bang and Xiang Yu shows the same principle. Xiang Yu was gentle and tolerant, but reluctant to reward people. Liu Bang was rough and arrogant, but generous with rewards. Whom people ultimately follow depends not only on who has a better personality, but on who is willing to distribute benefits. No matter how good someone seems, if they keep credit and resources only for themselves at critical moments, the people around them will eventually leave.

But sharing interests does not mean every decision should be made collectively. Another important point in the letter is that seeking opinions is necessary, but the final decision must be made by the responsible person. In American companies, many matters first collect relevant people's views, fill in omissions, and then the person in charge decides. They do not keep meeting until everyone is satisfied. In reality, few plans are perfect. Many decisions only need to be better than before, not solve every problem.

This is equally important in work and study. Discussion can help people see blind spots, but discussion cannot replace judgment. If someone always wants to wait until everyone agrees, they may miss the time to act. If every person is copied on every email and every small matter is pulled into a meeting, it may look respectful of process, but it actually consumes organizational efficiency. Jack Welch's restriction on internal email copying at GE was meant to reduce this kind of ineffective communication and let the people who truly need to be responsible carry responsibility.

Therefore, a mature way to work should be this: when results appear, bring contributors along; when decisions arrive, do not push responsibility onto the collective. Sharing interests reflects breadth of mind; deciding independently reflects ability. The first makes others willing to continue working together, and the second lets things truly move forward. How far a person can go depends not only on how strong they are, but also on whether they are willing to help others succeed. How large a team can become depends not only on how many smart people it has, but also on whether it can fairly distribute credit and efficiently make decisions.

In life and career development, many relationships are not built by one expression, but tested through repeated distributions of interest. Many abilities are not proven by opinions, but revealed when a decision must be made. What is truly worth cultivating is neither grabbing credit nor escaping responsibility: when there is credit, share it; when there is a decision, dare to carry it.
