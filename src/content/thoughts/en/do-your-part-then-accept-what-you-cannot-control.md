> A mature person does what is controllable as well as possible, while accepting that the world contains many uncontrollable variables.

In Letter 112, "Do Your Part, Then Accept What Heaven Decides," from Wu Jun's *Silicon Valley Letters 2: The Google Methodology*, the most important idea is not passive fatalism, but recognizing that human ability has boundaries. A person should of course do their part and do the controllable work as well as possible. But if someone believes every result can be decided by personal will, they may become arrogant in success, obsessive in failure, and unclear when judging the situation.

In his presidential inaugural address, John F. Kennedy proposed grand visions such as exploring space, curing disease, and eliminating poverty. But he finally emphasized that a good conscience is the only sure reward. He did not live to see the Apollo program succeed, but during his term he pushed forward what needed to be done. This attitude is not giving up on results. It is focusing on responsibility and action when results cannot be fully controlled. What people can decide is whether they do their best. What they cannot guarantee is whether the era, environment, and fate will cooperate.

The letter's comparison of failure and success is also worth thinking about. In failure, if someone keeps dwelling on regrets such as "I was two points short" or "if only I had not lost that time," life becomes tied to one result for a long time. After Nvidia founder Jensen Huang lost the lawsuit with Qualcomm, he did not attribute everything to external unfairness. He saw that focusing too much on the lawsuit had made him miss an earlier entry into artificial intelligence. Accepting failure is not numbness. It is moving energy from complaining about results to adjusting direction.

Success also requires accepting what cannot be controlled. Many people easily attribute a success entirely to their own ability, while ignoring luck, timing, and platform. The most dangerous part of retail investing is often interpreting money earned by chance as an inevitable victory of one's own judgment, then giving it all back in a larger risk. Acknowledging luck does not belittle effort. It prevents people from overestimating themselves.

Another core layer of the letter is that for ordinary people, insight should lead and talent should assist. Talent determines whether someone can get things done. Insight determines whether something should be done, when it should be done, and how far it should go. The extremely smart but ultimately unaccomplished genius in the letter shows that ability without insight can be wasted in the wrong direction. Zeng Guofan knew he was not good at commanding troops, did not understand mathematics, and lacked the energy in old age to keep up with the times, so he let more suitable people do more suitable work. That was not retreat. It was clarity about his own boundaries.

People with real insight do not accept everything just because an opportunity is large, and do not try to control everything just because they are capable. Companies such as Intel, Microsoft, and Cisco left some smaller competitors alive not only to avoid antitrust risk, but also to preserve external pressure that forced them to keep innovating. Knowing how to leave constraints for oneself is often more sustainable than simply pursuing expansion.

Therefore, "do your part, then accept what you cannot control" does not mean handing outcomes to fate. It means separating what people should do from what they cannot control. This applies to study, work, applications, investing, and life choices. What can be controlled includes preparation, judgment, execution, review, and long-term accumulation. What is hard to control includes timing, other people's choices, market changes, and chance. A truly stable mindset comes from neither escaping effort nor worshiping effort.

Insight is higher than talent because it lets a person know what to fight for, what to release, what to persist in, and what to follow with the situation. Doing one's part is responsibility to oneself. Accepting what cannot be controlled is respect for the complexity of the world. Only by doing both can a person avoid arrogance in success and collapse in failure.
