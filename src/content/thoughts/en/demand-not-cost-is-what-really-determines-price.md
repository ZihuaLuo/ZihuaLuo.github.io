> Raw materials become valuable only when people are willing to buy the final product.

In Lecture 018, Xue Zhaofeng addresses a common misunderstanding: does cost determine price, or does price determine cost? Many people say housing is expensive because land is expensive, noodles are expensive because rent is expensive, or concert tickets are expensive because the star's appearance fee is high. Economics reverses the causality.

Cost-based thinking says price is built from materials, labor, rent, and a reasonable profit. But if that were true, entrepreneurs should raise costs to raise prices. Reality does not work that way. Painting an old bicycle repeatedly does not make it valuable. Putting labor into an unwanted product does not make people buy it.

What determines price is the supply and demand of the final product. What consumers are willing to pay for a final good determines how much that good can sell for, and that price then determines how valuable the required resources become. Land is not expensive first, making housing expensive. People want to live in a location and bid up housing, which makes the land valuable.

A bowl of noodles in a central business district is expensive not simply because rent is high. More accurately, the area has high productivity, dense work, meetings, and consumption, so food service can sell at higher prices. Because noodles can sell at higher prices there, restaurants are willing to bid more for space.

The same applies to a singer's concert. Tickets are not expensive because the appearance fee is high; the appearance fee is high because many people want to hear the singer and are willing to pay for tickets. A resource is expensive because it helps produce a final product people value.

This also clarifies housing and land auctions. If buyers were not willing to pay high prices for housing, developers would not bid high for land. Land policy matters because it affects supply, but the simple claim that land price pushes housing price confuses the direction of cause. Final demand drives the value of production inputs.

For personal development, credentials, skills, and experience do not automatically have value because they required years of effort. They become valuable only when they produce results others need. The market does not price hardship; it prices demand and substitutability. The better question is not how much I invested, but whether what I create is truly needed.
