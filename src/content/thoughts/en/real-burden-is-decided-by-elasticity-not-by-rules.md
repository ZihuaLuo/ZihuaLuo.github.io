> The side that needs a transaction more has a harder time shifting the cost away.

Lecture 036 explains tax incidence through elasticity. A rule can say who pays a tax, fee, or cost on the surface, but that does not necessarily tell us who bears it in the end. The real burden depends on who can leave, who has substitutes, and who needs the transaction more.

If sellers have many alternatives while buyers have few, sellers can pass much of the cost to buyers. If buyers can easily switch while sellers cannot, sellers may absorb more of the burden. The law may point to one side, but market adjustment redistributes the pressure.

This helps explain why many policy debates are incomplete. People often ask whether a tax is charged to businesses or consumers, employers or employees, landlords or tenants. But the deeper question is elasticity. Which side is more flexible? Which side can wait? Which side has more outside options?

Rent, wages, tariffs, platform fees, and sales taxes all follow this logic. A company may be legally responsible for a payroll cost, yet part of it can appear as lower wage growth. A landlord may be charged a fee, but if tenants have few substitutes, rent may rise. A seller may face a tariff, but if consumers can switch easily, the seller may be forced to accept lower margins.

This does not mean rules do not matter. Rules change the first point of pressure. But people then respond. They renegotiate, exit, substitute, delay, or reduce supply and demand. The final burden is the result of these reactions.

For individuals, the lesson is to build elasticity. The more one depends on a single employer, customer, skill, city, or channel, the harder it is to refuse costs imposed by others. Outside options are not only opportunities; they are protection.

Real burden is not decided by the label on the rule. It is decided by the relative ability to move. Whoever has fewer choices usually carries more of the cost.
