> The truly important things often cannot wait until later.

After reading Letter 012, "The Five Things In A Life," from Wu Jun's *Silicon Valley Letters 2: The Google Methodology*, my biggest reflection was this: the things that truly matter in life are not necessarily the most urgent things. We often give our time to work, grades, income, and external evaluation, while repeatedly postponing longer-lasting things such as love, family, interests, giving back, faith, and legacy. But the problem is that life is not a project that can be postponed indefinitely. Some things, once missed at the right stage, are difficult to make up later.

In this letter, Wu Jun proposes that there are five things a person must do in life: fall in love, marry, and have children; try once to do something one truly likes; give back; have a faith; and leave a legacy. On the surface, these things do not seem as concrete or measurable as career success, wealth accumulation, or social status. But they point to a deeper question: why does a person live? If someone is only completing tasks, chasing income, and satisfying external standards, but does not truly have intimacy, passion, responsibility, spiritual support, or contribution to the world, their life may be efficient without being complete.

The first thing is about love, marriage, and children. Wu Jun believes this is not simply a family choice, but a very instinctive and important part of human life. In modern society, many people keep postponing love and family because they are busy with school and work, and they assume there will always be time later. But the letter reminds us that each person's concrete life is unique. It cannot be replaced by others, and it cannot be fully made up later. "There will be time later" often means there will never be time. Intimacy, in particular, is not a result that can be bought with money or replaced with efficiency. It is an experience built slowly in real life. Love alone does not guarantee happiness, but never seriously entering intimate relationships may also mean missing an important life experience.

The second thing is trying once to do something one truly likes. This point struck me strongly. Many people spend their whole lives doing what they "should" do: what major they should study, what job they should find, how much money they should earn, and what expectations they should meet. But Wu Jun reminds us that at least once in life, a person should seriously try something they genuinely like. The letter mentions that his daughter did not originally show obvious musical talent and did not like some group activities at school, but later, with the inspiration of a music teacher, discovered an interest in musical theater and quickly gained the opportunity to perform on stage. This example shows that interests do not always appear clearly at the beginning. They often need to be discovered through trying. If a person never gives themselves the chance to try, they may never know what they truly love.

Still, interest is not an excuse to escape reality. A real interest is not something that lets a person sink into comfort. It is something that elevates them through the process of improving it. The letter mentions that the problem of many so-called super-rich people is not that they lack money, but that they lack a decent hobby. Wealth without direction can easily become an empty number. Life without passion can easily become mechanical repetition. Therefore, the meaning of interest is not only to make people happy, but to prevent them from becoming mere machines for working and making money.

The third thing is giving back. Wu Jun discusses Rockefeller, who donated extensively throughout his life and helped establish universities and schools of public health. This example helped me see that giving back is not something to consider only after becoming rich. It is a responsibility in life. Many people say, "When I have money, when I am less busy, when I succeed, I will help others." But the letter notes that Rockefeller had already begun donating part of his income when he was still young and not earning much. This shows that giving back is not only about quantity. It is about mindset. Whether a person is willing to share what they have received determines whether their relationship with the world is extractive or constructive.

The fourth thing is having a faith. Faith here does not necessarily mean religion. It means the principles a person truly believes in. In real life, people constantly face choices, and some choices cannot be decided simply by comparing benefits. When morality, interest, courage, and real pressure conflict, a person without faith can easily drift with the current. The letter says faith can give people courage and strength, allowing them to act when they know they are right. I understand faith here as a person's spiritual coordinate. It tells us what is worth holding on to, what cannot be compromised, and helps us avoid becoming completely lost in a complex world.

The fifth thing is leaving a legacy. Wu Jun says legacy is not only money. More importantly, it is what a person leaves to the world. If the world did not have me, would it be exactly the same? This question is sharp and worth reflecting on. Many people are busy their whole lives without thinking about what they are actually leaving behind. A legacy does not have to be earth-shaking. It can be solving a problem in a field, helping some people, creating an influence, or making the lives of people around us better because of us. The letter mentions John Doerr, chairman of Kleiner Perkins, who indirectly created many jobs through investments in companies such as Google and Amazon. This shows that legacy can take many forms: knowledge, systems, opportunities, relationships, and inspiration to others.

The core reflection this letter left me with is that life cannot be measured only by efficiency and success. A person certainly needs to study hard, work seriously, and pursue growth. But if life is treated only as a checklist, it becomes easy to ignore the things that truly create happiness and meaning. Love and family let people experience intimacy. Interests keep passion alive. Giving back connects people with society. Faith gives direction. Legacy lets a life extend beyond the individual. A life worth pursuing is not one that fills every day completely, but one that uses limited time to carefully choose what deserves a lifetime of investment.
