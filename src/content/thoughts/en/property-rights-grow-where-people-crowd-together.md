> When resources begin to affect one another and people move closer together, property rights become a necessary tool for reducing conflict.

Lecture 057 discusses the rise of property rights. On the surface, it is about property. At a deeper level, it asks why human society needs rules, why some things once did not require clear boundaries but later did, and why a more developed civilization often needs more institutionalized relationships among people.

Property rights are not clear from the beginning. They are not labels naturally attached to objects, and they do not automatically exist because someone says this is mine. The emergence of property rights usually means that a conflict has become important enough that people must spend resources defining, maintaining, and enforcing it.

This is a realistic understanding. People often describe property rights as naturally sacred. But in history, property rights are often forced into existence. When a resource is not valuable, conflict is small, and users do not affect one another much, defining property is not very necessary. If two people split an ordinary bun, they can simply break it apart. If they split a gold bar, they need scales, rules, and recognized standards. People did not suddenly become more reasonable. The stakes rose, and the cost of ambiguity rose with them.

The first force behind the rise of property rights is externality. An externality means one person's action begins to affect others. Early hunting in North America is a typical example. When animal pelts were not valuable, whoever saw an animal could hunt it, and society had little reason to define who owned which animal. But once maritime trade made pelts saleable in Europe, animals became more valuable, hunters increased, and a problem emerged: every animal I take is one fewer animal for you.

At that point, property rights stop being theoretical and become practical. Without boundaries, everyone rushes to hunt more because what they do not take may be taken by others. The result is not that everyone becomes rich. It is that animals become fewer and resources are overused. Property rights turn chaotic competition into bounded use. They are not mainly to let one person take advantage, but to prevent everyone from exhausting the resource together.

But defining property is not easy. Animals move, so one cannot simply point to a deer and say it is mine. People therefore use natural conditions to divide rights. In the Northeast of North America, many animals lived in dens and did not move far from them, so people could indirectly define animals by defining hills. Whoever controlled the hill roughly controlled the animals on it. Property rights are not designed out of thin air. They depend on geography, technology, and enforcement costs.

The salmon example shows the same point. Salmon migrate. They are born in streams, grow in the sea, and return to their birthplace to spawn. Fishers do not need to chase salmon across the open ocean. They can wait near the mouth of the stream. Natural regularity reduces the cost of defining and capturing the resource, making property arrangements feasible. Institutions can be built not only because people understand principles, but because reality allows those principles to be enforced.

The American West was different. It had broad plains and animals with large migration ranges, making boundaries hard to draw through hills or dens. Dividing the plains required fences, but walls were too expensive, so early boundaries were maintained by cowboys patrolling on horseback, a kind of human fence. Only after barbed wire became common did large-scale land boundaries become genuinely feasible. Technology changed the possible property arrangement.

This shows that property rights do not exist in isolation. Their boundaries often depend on enforcement costs. If a right is too costly to enforce, it may be theoretically claimable but hard to realize. Conversely, once technology lowers enforcement costs, formerly vague boundaries may become clear. Barbed wire was not only a tool. It was an institutional condition. It turned land that could not be effectively divided into assets that could be owned, used, and traded.

The second force behind property rights is population concentration. As transportation costs fall, people and goods can move across long distances, cities form, and contact among strangers becomes denser. When people lived far apart, many conflicts never arose. When people live close together, share planes, crowd into subways, and use the same public spaces, conflicts become frequent. The closer people are, the more important rules become.

The richness of modern cities rests on this dense cooperation. A person can eat a European breakfast, Thai food for lunch, and Brazilian barbecue for dinner. This looks like more consumer choice, but behind it are global transport, property protection, contract enforcement, and commercial specialization. Strangers who do not know one another can cooperate through rules. Restaurants trust suppliers to deliver, customers trust restaurants to provide service, landlords trust tenants to pay, and passengers trust airlines to perform. This is not maintained by acquaintance. It is supported by institutionalized rights.

This prosperity also creates more complex interdependence. A flight delay may be caused by weather at a previous stop. A supply chain break in one city may affect another city's daily life. A platform rule change may alter the income of countless merchants. The more developed modern society becomes, the tighter the links among people become, and the rougher property and rules cannot be.

This also explains why acquaintance societies and stranger societies need different orders. Acquaintances can cooperate through face, favor, and reciprocity. Strangers cannot rely on these. There are too many strangers, relationships are too distant, and repeated interactions too limited. They need property rights, contracts, law, and market prices. The market economy may weaken some warmth among acquaintances, but it also lets strangers cooperate on a far larger scale, allowing us to serve one another without knowing one another.

This does not mean property rights should always be finer or rules should always be more numerous. Property itself has costs. Defining, enforcing, and protecting it requires resources. The key is that property rights rise when the cost of ambiguity exceeds the cost of definition. The purpose of property is not to create distance, but to reduce conflict; not to make people cold, but to make cooperation predictable.

The lesson is that many institutions are not designed from nothing and do not operate on good intentions alone. They grow after resources become valuable, people become closer, and conflicts become more visible. Property rights are not a slogan. They are society's response to real problems.

When the things people compete for become more important and people are more closely connected, ambiguity and goodwill are no longer enough to maintain order. Civilization is not the absence of conflict. It puts conflict inside rules. The market is not the absence of competition. It puts competition inside property rights and prices. The rise of property rights is a step from direct seizure toward indirect cooperation.
