> Scarcity is not pessimism, but a basic fact of how the world works; as human wants keep growing, choice and trade-off never disappear.

In Lecture 009, Xue Zhaofeng places economics on the most basic fact: scarcity. Economics does not have to depend on assumptions that people are always rational or always selfish. People vary. Scarcity is different. It is not an assumption; it is a reality everyone faces every day.

Scarcity first comes from competition. Many things we like are also liked by others. The most attractive products often cost more, housing in popular cities is expensive, popular majors are harder to enter, and good jobs are more competitive. This is not because the world is hostile; it is because valuable things attract more than one person.

Scarcity also comes from growing wants. When people have only wild vegetables, they want bread. After bread, they want meat and wine. After basic life, they want movies, music, travel, comfort, better packaging, and special experiences. Once people become used to higher standards, demand does not stop; it keeps growing upward.

This is why scarcity is misunderstood. Many people think scarcity belongs only to the poor, as if wealth will make it disappear. Wealth only changes what is scarce. Food may become less scarce, while good locations, schools, doctors, time, clean air, trust, and opportunity become scarce.

The difference between land and location is especially clear. There is still a great deal of empty land on Earth. What is scarce is location: the city center, access to transport, proximity to resources, and positions connected to opportunity. Housing prices are often about location, not the absence of land itself.

Trust is also scarce. People enter good schools, companies, and networks not only for knowledge, but for higher trust density. Trust takes time and screening to build. The more important the cooperation, the more trust matters; the harder trust is to build, the scarcer it becomes.

Scarcity is a life judgment as much as an economic concept. Time is scarce, so we cannot learn everything. Energy is scarce, so we cannot maintain every relationship. Opportunity is scarce, so we cannot walk every path at once. Attention is scarce, so unlimited information can exhaust us.

Understanding scarcity is not about becoming utilitarian. It is about becoming clear. The world will not satisfy every wish just because the wish is beautiful. Life will not cancel trade-offs because we work hard. The starting point of economic thinking is to admit that resources are limited, desires grow, and costs are real.
