> When reasoning cannot settle a conflict, the key is often to move resources toward higher-value use.

In Lecture 027, Xue Zhaofeng applies the Coase theorem to everyday conflict. Many disputes look like fights over who is right. The deeper question is often how the same resource can be used to create higher value. If each side only argues principle, both can sound right. If needs are translated into costs and compensation, cooperation becomes possible.

The dorm party example is intuitive. One student needs quiet for an exam; another wants to hold a party. If they only argue rights, one says quiet is basic, the other says the dorm is shared space. Both have reasons. If the question becomes how much quiet is worth and how much the party space is worth, the path is clearer.

This does not make every relationship a cold transaction. It reveals that conflicts have costs. The studying student can leave for a cafe at some cost; the party organizer can rent another venue at some cost. If compensation lets the party happen while giving the student a better study location, both may benefit.

Radio spectrum is another example. Frequencies too close to one another interfere. People once assumed government had to allocate them directly. Coase suggested clearer rights and trade: once spectrum rights are defined, the people who need and can use them most can acquire them through auctions and transactions.

Smoking rules in restaurants also depend on context. A heavy-metal bar and a fine-dining restaurant create different experiences. A single rule for all venues may be less useful than allowing owners to choose based on customers and letting consumers vote with money.

Steven Cheung's study of bees and orchards shows market cooperation. Bees collect nectar and trees receive pollination, but the value differs by crop. In apple orchards, pollination may matter more, so orchard owners pay beekeepers. In orange groves, honey value may be higher, so beekeepers may pay orchard owners. Who compensates whom depends on where value is created.

In personal life, many conflicts do not need a final moral winner. Roommates, teammates, friends, and partners often compete over the same time, space, or attention. Mature communication asks what arrangement minimizes total loss and whether compensation or substitution can make both sides better off.

A cooperative solution does not abandon principle. It puts principle into real conditions. Many conflicts remain unsolved because people fight over legitimacy without comparing costs. Coase's lesson is to ask who values the resource more, who can pay or compensate, and whether a deal can preserve cooperation.
