> A truly good investment looks not only at return, but at the direction of the times.

After reading the supplemental letter "The Operating Principles of Three Top Silicon Valley Venture Capital Firms" from Wu Jun's *Silicon Valley Letters 2: The Google Methodology*, my biggest reflection was this: the truly difficult part of venture capital is not putting money into a company that looks profitable. It is judging whether a company may represent the future while it is still early and not yet understood by the public. What makes top venture capital firms powerful is not only that they have abundant capital, but that they can identify trends inside uncertainty and find truly promising people and technologies inside disorder.

In this letter, Wu Jun first introduces Sequoia Capital. Sequoia has invested in Apple, Google, Cisco, Oracle, Yahoo, YouTube, WhatsApp, and many other companies. Its success was not accidental. Sequoia pays attention to companies at different stages, including seed incubation, early stage, and growth stage, but it especially prefers companies that grow quickly rather than companies that produce short-term profits quickly. This distinction matters. Fast profitability may only mean a business model is temporarily effective. Fast growth may mean the company is entering a much larger market, and may even change an industry.

Sequoia's standards for judging a company are also worth thinking about. It values whether a technology has the meaning of qualitative change, meaning whether it can bring a real breakthrough. It also values whether the company is in an industry others have not yet tried, the kind of company that is "first to eat the crab." Such investments are naturally risky because there are few historical cases to reference and no mature market to verify them. But precisely because of that, if the judgment is correct, the return can be enormous. Real venture capital does not chase consensus in tracks everyone already knows. It sees the future before consensus has formed.

The letter also mentions an important Sequoia principle: the founding team should include first-generation immigrants. On the surface this view seems unusual, but behind it is a judgment about the entrepreneurial spirit. First-generation immigrants are often more willing to take risks and more motivated to change their fate. The fact that they left a familiar environment already suggests adaptability, action, and endurance. Venture capital invests not only in business models, but also in people. Whether an entrepreneurial team can persist through difficulty, attract strong talent, learn quickly, and adjust often matters more than the early product itself.

Compared with Sequoia, Kleiner Perkins works differently. It pays more attention to whether a startup can bring positive progress to the world, not merely reduce costs or make money in a different way. This standard made me realize that good venture capital is not only searching for companies that "can make money," but for companies that are "worth creating." If a company only profits by lowering costs, manufacturing short-term traffic, or repeating existing models, it may be a business, but not necessarily a great investment opportunity. Truly valuable entrepreneurship should push technology, industry, or social efficiency forward.

Kleiner Perkins also has another feature: after investing, it participates deeply and uses companies it previously invested in to help new companies grow. This shows that top venture capital does not end after writing a check. It continues to provide resources, experience, and networks throughout the entrepreneurial process. For startups, money is of course important, but many times judgment, relationships, and strategic support are even more important. An early team may have technology but lack market experience; it may have a product but lack management capability. Excellent investment institutions can help entrepreneurs avoid detours and turn point capabilities into real company capabilities.

Finally, the letter mentions Y Combinator. YC is both like an incubator and an angel investment fund. Unlike traditional venture capital, it emphasizes training and companionship for young entrepreneurs. It does not necessarily provide luxurious office space. Instead, through courses, discussions, and experience sharing, it helps entrepreneurs grow quickly. This model showed me that venture capital does not have only one form. For extremely early-stage entrepreneurs, what is missing may not be a large amount of money, but experience, direction, and judgment. YC's value lies in putting promising people into a high-density entrepreneurial training environment so they can learn faster what they should and should not do.

From Sequoia to Kleiner Perkins to YC, we can see that top venture capital institutions have different styles, but they all care less about short-term noise and more about long-term value. Sequoia focuses more on rapid growth and large markets. Kleiner Perkins focuses more on whether technology pushes the world forward. YC focuses more on the potential and training of young entrepreneurs. Their common point is this: a truly good investment does not chase success that has already been confirmed. It judges whether something is worth believing in before success has been proven.

This is also useful for my understanding of finance. Finance is not only a game of numbers, valuations, and rates of return. Especially in venture capital, what matters is a comprehensive judgment about people, technology, industry, and changes in the times. If one looks only at financial data, many great companies do not look beautiful in their early stages. If one looks only at short-term profit, one may miss opportunities that truly change the world. The essence of venture capital is finding certainty inside uncertainty, and that certainty does not come from the books. It comes from understanding trends and judging people.

The core reflection this letter left me with is that top venture capital firms do not invest in a static company, but in a dynamic possibility of growth. They care not only about today's revenue, but whether a huge market can form in the future; not only whether an idea is fresh, but whether the technology has barriers; not only whether a founder is smart, but whether the founder can keep learning, take risks, and organize a team. A truly good investment does not follow the excitement of the times. It understands the direction of the times before that direction has fully appeared.
