> The maturity of an institution is not whether it sounds fair, but whether it reduces needless competitive costs.

The Week 15 Q&A discusses property rights, competition, and rule design. The topics appear scattered: whether a gold mine belongs to the discoverer or the miner, whether a shipwreck salvage company should have exclusivity, whether state-owned assets count as clear property, whether punishment should be light or heavy, who pays when a boat damages a dock, and even why fairy tales became gentler over time. Behind all of them is one core point: rules are not moral slogans. Rules are arrangements of cost.

The real problem for a society is not making everyone feel that they have won. It is reducing waste during the struggle for resources. Many debates over fairness are actually debates over preferred forms of competition. Some people like first come, first served because their time is cheap. Some like exams because they are good at studying. Some like relationships because they have connections. Some like highest bidder wins because they have money. Rule design cannot ask only which group feels comfortable. It must ask where the rule directs the whole society.

The gold mine example is typical. If whoever discovers the mine owns the whole mine, that defines a stock. If whoever digs out gold owns only the gold they dig, that defines a flow. The second rule may look more hardworking and fair: whoever labors obtains. But if everyone can gain gold only by rushing to mine, people will dig desperately, compete for speed, mine chaotically, and waste tools, labor, and time. The gold may be extracted, but society may have paid an excessive cost to compete for it.

The benefit of stock ownership lies here. If the discoverer owns the whole mine, they have an incentive to plan slowly: when to mine, how much to mine, how much to sell when prices are high, and how much to keep when prices are low. If the mine is a forest, the owner considers the balance between cutting and planting. If it is a fish pond, the owner considers the rhythm of fishing and breeding. Once property rights are clear, resources are no longer only about who grabs them now. They become about how to use them best over time. That is the deepest power of property institutions: they move people from short-term seizure to long-term calculation.

The shipwreck salvage case follows the same logic. Several companies want to salvage a wreck. If all companies spend large amounts of labor and capital racing for it, the final result may be that only one succeeds while all the others waste their investment. When a court allows the company best able to locate the wreck to exclude others, it may look like limiting competition, but it actually reduces ineffective competition. The key is not protecting one company. It is protecting social resources from repeated waste.

This reminds me that much so-called competition in reality does not create value but consumes it. Several people compete endlessly for one slot, dozens of companies duplicate investment for one small opportunity, students take harder and harder exams merely to separate themselves from others, and firms spend more and more on lobbying for policy resources. Many competitors work hard, but effort itself does not necessarily create social wealth. The task of rule design is to direct effort toward production rather than consumption.

So the joke that one should not race Liu Xiang sounds playful but is deep. If there is a bag of gold one hundred meters away and everyone wants to run for it, but one runner is Liu Xiang, the others waste energy by continuing. Recognizing ability differences is sometimes not unfair. It reduces pointless loss. Institutions do not need to pretend everyone has exactly the same chance. They need to identify who is most likely to use the resource well and reduce unnecessary running alongside them.

Punishment works similarly. Discussions of crime often fall into two extremes. One side thinks punishment should always be heavier to create deterrence. The other thinks lighter punishment is more civilized and tolerant. Economics is cooler: punishment should relate to the harm caused by the violation and the probability of being caught. The harder a violation is to detect, the heavier the punishment may need to be. If punishment is too light, offenders treat it as a cost. If it is too heavy, it creates new distortions.

For example, if home robbery and murder receive exactly the same punishment, a robber may become more inclined to kill the witness, because the marginal punishment has not increased. Punishment is not emotional release. It is incentive design. Its goal is not to make bystanders feel satisfied, but to make potential offenders recalculate before acting. A good punishment system cannot make wrongdoing too cheap, and it cannot erase differences between wrongs of different severity.

The damaged dock in an emergency also shows that rules must examine cost allocation. A boat caught in a storm ties to a dock for safety. That is reasonable. But if the boat damages the dock, who should pay? Intuition says the boat owner should, because the boat used another person's dock and caused damage. But if the dock is in an area where storms are frequent, perhaps the dock owner is better able and better positioned to reinforce it in advance. Should passing boats bear the cost, or should the dock operator bear it? Which arrangement lowers future total loss?

No moral slogan can answer this. We must return to cost: who can prevent the harm more easily, who has more information, and who can avoid the loss at lower cost? This also explains why malls, supermarkets, and hotels often bear many safety responsibilities. It is not because customers are never responsible. It is because operators can reduce overall risk more cheaply through unified design, maintenance, and management.

The discussion of fairy tales may seem unrelated, but it also concerns institutional evolution. Early fairy tales were often cruel and frightening because public security was poor and risk was high. Stories warned children. Later, as society became safer, fairy tales gradually became gentler and turned into carriers of kindness, beauty, and imagination. If even fairy tales change with the cost structure of society, law, property rights, and institutions certainly do as well.

This may be the most important lesson of the week: many rules do not fall from the sky and are not designed by a saint. They form through repeated conflicts, losses, errors, and corrections. Property rights do not exist to make one person sound more reasonable. Punishment does not exist to satisfy an emotion. Competition does not exist to give everyone a feeling of participation. The value of institutions is ultimately whether they reduce waste, lower conflict, and move resources away from struggle and toward creation.

Good rules may not please everyone. They may make those running alongside quit, make inefficient people disappointed, and make people used to the old order uncomfortable. But if they reduce needless loss, increase long-term planning, and encourage real creation, these less charming rules may be closer to the essence of civilization.
