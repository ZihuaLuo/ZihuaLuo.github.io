> When resources are limited, the key is not comparing total importance, but whether the next unit of input is worth it.

In Lecture 030, Xue Zhaofeng explains one of the core concepts of economics: the margin. The margin means the added effect of one more unit. One more bite of food creates marginal utility; one more product creates marginal cost; one more hour of study creates marginal return.

Economics cares about the margin because real choices are often not about whether to take everything or nothing. They are about whether to continue one more step.

The diamond and water story illustrates this. Water is far more important to life than diamonds, yet water is usually cheap while diamonds are expensive. The reason is not that water lacks value. In ordinary life, water is relatively abundant, so the next unit of water adds little satisfaction. Diamonds are scarce, so the next unit may bring more display, aesthetic, or symbolic value.

This changes how we understand life choices. Family is more important than a course; health is more important than work; sleep is more important than study in total terms. But real choice asks where the next ten minutes, next hour, or next hundred yuan creates the greatest additional return.

If a person has already spent a long time with family, another ten minutes may add less value than listening to a lecture. If a person has studied for too long and is exhausted, another hour may produce little, while sleep may restore more. Mature allocation is not ranking one thing first forever; it is judging where the next resource belongs now.

Learning works the same way. Raising a score from 60 to 80 may be easier and more valuable than raising 99 to 100. If the final point consumes enormous time and sacrifices other subjects, health, or rest, it may be poor allocation. Good students do not push every area to the limit; they know where to add and where to stop.

Marginal thinking protects people from the belief that more is always better. More study, work, socializing, or effort is valuable only if the next unit creates enough return. If one direction is saturated, more input may add little, while a new weakness may produce far greater improvement.

The marginal revolution changes the unit of judgment. It brings us back from grand total comparisons to each concrete next step: what to do with the next hour, where to spend the next dollar, where to put the next effort, and whether the next opportunity is worth taking. Long-run differences often accumulate from these marginal choices.
