> The key to investing is not reading every chart, but knowing that public information is no longer an advantage.

After reading Wu Jun's supplement on investment information, I felt that many retail investors spend too much energy staring at the wrong things. They study K-lines, draw patterns, watch short-term fluctuations, and hope to find certainty in public charts. But if the information is already public and easy to see, it is usually no longer a real advantage.

The letter uses information theory and the Australian Open as examples. Before a match, some information may help estimate probability. Once the result is known to everyone, that information no longer creates an edge. Markets work similarly. If all participants can see the same price chart, then the chart itself cannot easily become a stable source of excess return.

This does not mean all market analysis is useless. It means we must distinguish between information and advantage. A piece of information becomes valuable only if it is not fully reflected in price, if one can interpret it better than others, or if one can act with a time horizon and discipline that others cannot maintain. Simply seeing the same K-line as everyone else does not create superiority.

Duan Yongping once said that Buffett does not draw K-lines. This statement is useful not because every investor can copy Buffett, but because it reminds us where the real problem lies. Long-term investing should care more about business value, cash flow, competitive advantage, management quality, pricing power, and the relationship between price and value. These are harder to analyze than charts, but they are closer to the source of return.

Of course, there are quantitative firms such as Renaissance that use price and market data effectively. But their advantage comes from data scale, models, execution, technology, and discipline that ordinary investors do not have. A retail investor imitating the surface form of professional trading is usually not gaining the underlying capability.

The lesson for me is to respect the boundary of my own information edge. If I cannot obtain or interpret information better than the market, I should not pretend that repeated chart watching will create insight. For most ordinary investors, broad index funds, long time horizons, and disciplined allocation may be more reliable than searching for patterns that everyone can see.
