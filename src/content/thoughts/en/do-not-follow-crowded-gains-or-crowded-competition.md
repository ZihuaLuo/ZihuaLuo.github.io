> A mature choice is not going wherever the crowd is, but seeing the risks and costs behind the excitement.

After reading Letter 109, "Do Not Take Crowded Gains, Do Not Go Where Everyone Competes," from Wu Jun's *Silicon Valley Letters 2: The Google Methodology*, the point most worth expanding is this: many opportunities that look universally approved and fiercely contested may not be truly worth pursuing. People are easily carried away by consensus. They think that if everyone says something is good, it must be good; if everyone is fighting for it, it must be valuable. But in reality, when something has already been seen by everyone, its best timing has often already passed.

The letter mentions that in the U.S. Congress, people often raise objections. Even when something seems to have no controversy, it still goes through long debate. On the surface, this looks inefficient. But at a deeper level, the value of opposing views is exactly that they help people see blind spots hidden by optimism. When everyone believes something has no problem at all, the biggest risk is often not too much opposition, but that nobody can point out where the problem is.

"Do not take crowded gains, do not go where everyone competes" is especially clear in investing and entrepreneurship. Wu Jun says that if all partners in a fund think a project is incredibly good, they should be especially cautious, because it may mean they have not really seen the risks, or that the best investment window has already passed. Similarly, many Chinese startups used to copy trends: when YouTube appeared in the United States, a group of video sites appeared in China; when the Groupon model became popular, thousands of group-buying companies appeared. Everyone thought entering the track meant sharing the benefits, but in the end it often became mutual compression.

Truly valuable opportunities are usually not understood by everyone at the beginning. New technologies that can disrupt large companies are often not the hottest concepts everyone praises. They are directions that some people are optimistic about, some people do not understand, and many people even look down on. Once everyone has already rushed in, competitive advantage no longer comes from judgment. It comes only from consuming resources.

This also applies to personal development. When applying to schools, choosing careers, or cultivating strengths, if we merely follow the majority, we easily fall into homogeneous competition. If everyone competes on grades, grades become less scarce. If everyone learns the same specialty, the specialty loses what makes it special. A real advantage is not copying what others are already doing, but starting from one's own characteristics and finding a position that others have not fully valued, but where one can accumulate for a long time.

The second point in the letter, "do not forget the greater good because of small bads," is also important. Wu Jun mentions that many students who went to the United States early completed their studies with school scholarships. During the process, professors might assign them miscellaneous tasks or even delay graduation, but compared with the tuition, living expenses, and development opportunities the school provided, these unpleasant things were only small bads. If someone denies the whole opportunity because of local frustration, the larger future may be lost.

The example of one of Facebook's first 100 employees is especially regrettable. A friend left before staying for half a year because of some small matters, so he did not receive stock options and missed a later opportunity that might have been worth hundreds of millions of dollars. Many times, an environment is not perfect, and a team cannot always be fair and smooth. But if it still provides enormous growth and return overall, one should not erase all of its value because of a temporary grievance.

The *Book Of Rites* says, "To love someone and know their faults, to dislike someone and know their merits - such people are rare." Being able to see flaws when we like someone, and acknowledge strengths when we dislike someone, is itself a kind of maturity. The mistake people most easily make is total affirmation or total denial because of a local feeling. Rational judgment is not having no emotions. It is not letting emotions cover the whole picture.

Therefore, whether choosing opportunities or evaluating an experience, we should not look only at surface excitement or temporary feelings. Places where everyone is fighting may not contain real room. Small problems in one thing should not cover its larger value. A mature way to act is to stay calm when things are crowded, preserve judgment when dissatisfied, and choose with less herd mentality and more independent thinking.
