> Data has value only when rights, boundaries, and pricing mechanisms are clear enough to support real transactions.

The idea that data is an asset sounds obvious today, but Wu Jun's discussion made me realize that data does not automatically become an asset just because it is useful. An asset must have clear ownership, usable rights, boundaries of use, and a way to be priced.

Many companies collect large amounts of data and assume that volume itself equals value. But raw data without structure, permission, and application scenarios may become a cost rather than an asset. It needs storage, cleaning, security, compliance, and interpretation. If nobody can use it legally and effectively, it is not truly productive.

Ownership is the first condition. If data comes from users, platforms, transactions, devices, or public environments, who has the right to use it, sell it, combine it, or delete it? Without clear rights, data transactions are fragile. The more valuable the data becomes, the more likely disputes will appear.

Pricing is the second condition. A thing becomes an asset only when others can evaluate what it contributes. Data may improve recommendations, reduce risk, support credit decisions, train models, or optimize operations. Its price should come from the value it helps create, not from the size of the database alone.

This is why data governance is not a bureaucratic burden. It is the foundation of value creation. Clear rules about consent, privacy, security, access, and accountability make data more usable, not less. Unclear data rights may create short-term convenience, but they limit long-term trust and transactions.

The lesson for me is that the future value of data depends not only on technology, but also on institutions. Data becomes an asset when it can be trusted, governed, used, and priced. Before that, it is only potential value waiting for a structure.
