> In a world full of uncertainty, many outcomes are not decided by the smartest people, but selected by the environment.

In Lecture 005, Xue Zhaofeng discusses Armen Alchian's essay on uncertainty, evolution, and economic theory. The question is fundamental: does economics depend on the assumption that people are perfectly rational? If people in reality are not always rational, can economics still explain anything?

Many critics say economics assumes people calculate precisely and pursue the optimum. Real entrepreneurs and consumers do not make decisions with formulas every day. Entrepreneurs may not calculate marginal cost and marginal benefit exactly; consumers may not know the optimal solution behind each choice.

Alchian's answer is interesting because he does not insist that people are always rational. He changes the angle. In a world full of uncertainty, there may be no optimal answer that can be calculated in advance. The real question is not how to be absolutely optimal, but which behaviors, organizations, and institutions can survive environmental change.

The gas station example makes this clear. Several people open gas stations. One opens in his backyard, another on a mountain, and one happens to open by the road. The one that survives may simply be the roadside station. Whether the owner was the smartest or had the most complete plan is less important than the fact that the location matched demand.

This is similar to leaves growing more densely on the sunny side of a tree. The leaves do not need to understand optimization. Through selection, the part that receives more light grows better. Businesses, careers, business models, and institutions often work the same way. They may not be perfectly designed at the start; those that adapt remain, and those that do not are eliminated.

This perspective breaks a common success narrative. People like to hear successful people explain their path as if copying those actions will reproduce the result. But success may be the product of environment, timing, rules, and luck, not just ability. A method that worked in one era or market may fail in another.

Failure deserves the same kind of analysis. A person may fail not because they were lazy or unintelligent, but because their choice did not fit the environment. A business model may not match the market, a product may not match demand, and an organization may not match the rules of competition. Many failures are adaptation problems.

For personal growth, the lesson is not to abandon effort. It is to avoid treating life as a game that intelligence alone can win. What matters is observing changes, adjusting one's position and abilities, and keeping room to revise. In uncertainty, the mature goal is not one perfect decision, but a continuing ability to survive and adapt.
