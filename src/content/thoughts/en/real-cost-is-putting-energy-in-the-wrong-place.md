> A society's deepest waste is not that people do not work hard, but that effort fails to flow toward creating value.

The Week 12 Q&A returns to price controls, but the deeper topic is effort. People will always compete. The question is where institutions direct that competition.

When prices are controlled, people do not stop trying to obtain scarce goods. They may spend time queuing, cultivating relationships, searching for loopholes, or bargaining in informal markets. The effort is real, but it may create little value.

This is one of the largest hidden costs of bad rules. A society can look busy while wasting human energy. People may work hard not to produce better goods, but to navigate distorted systems.

Opportunity cost applies here at the social level. Every hour spent fighting for access through inefficient channels is an hour not spent building, learning, serving, or innovating. Every talent redirected toward privilege-seeking is talent pulled away from creation.

This also explains why some systems feel exhausting even when people are diligent. The problem is not laziness. The problem is that effort is rewarded in the wrong places. If the rule rewards connections, people invest in connections. If the rule rewards waiting, people wait. If the rule rewards creation, people create.

For personal development, choosing the right game matters as much as working hard. If the arena rewards the wrong behavior, effort can become consumption rather than accumulation.

Real cost is not only money spent. It is energy placed where it cannot create value. Good institutions make effort productive; bad institutions make effort disappear into friction.
