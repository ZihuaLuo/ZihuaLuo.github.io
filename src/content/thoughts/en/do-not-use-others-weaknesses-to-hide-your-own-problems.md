> Mature people do not prove themselves right by comparing others' mistakes, or prove excellence by defeating others' weaknesses.

In Letter 111, "Do Not Use Others' Shortcomings To Defend Your Own, And Do Not Use Others' Shortcomings To Show Off Your Strengths," from Wu Jun's *Silicon Valley Letters 2: The Google Methodology*, the most important warning is about a very common mentality: when people make mistakes, their first reaction is often not to face the problem, but to look for evidence that others have made mistakes too. When they gain a small advantage, they may not pursue a higher standard, but compare their strength against someone else's weakness.

The letter mentions an example from traffic court. Someone was caught speeding, but argued that other people were speeding too, so why was only he caught? The judge asked one key question: Were you speeding? If he was indeed speeding, whether others were caught is another issue. The logic is simple but important. The rule discusses the present matter itself, not whether someone else's mistake can cancel one's own responsibility.

This is also common at work. When someone is told they have a problem, they may immediately say someone else made a similar mistake last time, or that the other person did something first. If every discussion of a problem keeps tracing blame backward, it becomes mutual accusation rather than problem solving. The example of Google director David handling Jonathan speaking too loudly is representative. Jonathan argued that David had also spoken loudly before. David did not fall into debate. He admitted that if he spoke loudly next time, Jonathan could remind him, but today's discussion was about Jonathan's issue. This response brought the conversation back to the matter itself and prevented it from becoming emotional confrontation.

The essence of "do not use others' shortcomings to defend your own" is not using other people's mistakes as excuses for your own. Someone else running a red light does not mean I can run a red light. Someone else being careless at work does not mean I can lower my standard. Someone else speaking irresponsibly does not mean I can express myself emotionally. If a person always focuses on where others did poorly, they easily escape their own requirements. Real growth often begins with admitting, "I really could have done this better."

The second point in the letter, "do not use others' shortcomings to show off your strengths," is also worth attention. If someone beats a tennis champion at chess and then beats a chess champion at tennis, it may look like using one's strength against another's weakness, but nobody will see that person as a true master. What deserves respect is not using one's advantage to compare with another person's weak point, but competing with experts under the same standard.

This also explains why phrases like "the product manager who writes code best" or "the programmer who understands product best" are not always valuable. If someone exists as an engineer, engineering ability itself must be strong enough. If someone exists as a product manager, product judgment must be solid. Additional skills can add value, but they cannot replace core professional ability. Real advantage is not knowing a little more than outsiders. It is reaching a high standard in one's own field and then using other abilities as complements.

Applied to study and career development, this principle is very direct. Learning cannot excuse inefficiency because others also procrastinate. Work cannot lower standards because others also make mistakes. In applications and career competition, I cannot be satisfied with being stronger than weaker people. I need to ask whether I have accumulated real core ability. Comforting myself with others' shortcomings only keeps me in place. Proving excellence through others' weaknesses does not create real competitiveness.

So this letter is not merely moral preaching. It is a standard for handling things: when facing mistakes, discuss the matter itself; when facing advantages, return to the real arena. Do not use other people's problems to hide your own, and do not use other people's deficiencies to decorate your excellence. Whether someone can keep improving depends on whether they are willing to place the standard back on themselves, rather than always looking at where others are worse.
