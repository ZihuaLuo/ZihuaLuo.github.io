> Price is not the only allocation mechanism, but if it is removed, another screening rule will appear.

The Week 11 Q&A uses the disappearance of the U.S. oil shortage to make a broader point: when resources are scarce, society must allocate them somehow. If allocation does not happen through money, it will happen through other standards.

Many people dislike price screening because it feels unequal. But replacing price does not remove screening. It can create queues, quotas, ration tickets, political favor, personal connections, identity requirements, or first-come-first-served rules.

Each rule has costs. A queue rewards people with time. Connections reward people with access. Quotas reward people who fit categories. Administrative approval rewards those who understand the system. These costs are real even when no extra money changes hands.

Price has its own problems, especially for people with low income. But it has one advantage: it is visible and often allows resources to move toward those who value them enough to pay. When price is blocked, the new screening rule may be less visible and less honest.

The real question is not whether screening should exist. Scarcity makes screening unavoidable. The real question is who screens, by what rule, with what transparency, and with what consequences.

This matters for fairness. A society that refuses to admit scarcity may produce a distribution that looks moral in language but privileges people with time, power, or relationships. The poor may still lose, only through a less visible mechanism.

If prices do not screen, society will screen in other ways. Mature judgment compares these mechanisms honestly instead of pretending one can avoid choice altogether.
