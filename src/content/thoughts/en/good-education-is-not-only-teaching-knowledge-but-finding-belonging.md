> The meaning of the college system is turning a university from a place of study into a community of growth.

After reading Letter 072, "The College System at Oxford and Cambridge," from Wu Jun's *Silicon Valley Letters 2: The Google Methodology*, my biggest reflection was this: truly good education is not only passing knowledge to students, nor is it simply providing courses, exams, and diplomas. It should shape and support students inside a long-term and stable community, and ultimately help them grow into people who can enter society. The reason the Oxford and Cambridge college system is special is not only its long history. It is that it truly combines learning and living.

In this letter, Wu Jun mentions that the colleges at Oxford and Cambridge are not schools of literature, engineering, or science in the way we usually understand them. They are more like small universities. For example, St John's College at Cambridge has its own housing, dining hall, library, chapel, living areas, recreational facilities, and even its own finance and management system. Students do not just come here to take a few classes. They eat, live, study, socialize, and form a sense of belonging inside the college over a long period. This is very different from many modern universities that treat students as mobile individuals.

What touches me most about the college system is that it turns a university into a "home." In many universities, the relationship between students and the school is relatively loose. Students attend classes, take exams, graduate, and then their connection with the school may slowly fade. But at Oxford and Cambridge, students often first see themselves as members of a particular college, not only as members of a university. This identity can last for a long time and even influence their lifelong social circles, values, and sense of belonging. Education here is not only knowledge training. It is life in a community.

This system also makes me rethink the meaning of admission. In ordinary universities, admission usually mainly asks whether a student meets the school's standards for grades, ability, and character. But under the college system, admission is more like asking whether a family is willing to accept a new member. Students must not only prove that they are excellent, but also explain why they fit this college and how they can contribute to this community. This logic is illuminating: entering a good environment is not only about enjoying resources. One should also think about what one can bring to that environment.

The letter also mentions that colleges have fairly independent management and financial authority, and both professors and students participate in college governance. This shows that a college is not a cold administrative unit, but an organization for shared living and shared governance. In such an environment, students learn not only professional knowledge, but also how to get along with others, take responsibility, and participate in public affairs. Good education should not only care whether students can solve problems or write papers. It should also care whether students can become mature, reliable people who know how to cooperate with others.

This also makes me think about a part of modern university education that is easily missing. Today, many people go to university mainly to get a diploma, find a job, and increase competitiveness. These are certainly important, but if university is left with only utilitarian goals, it becomes thin. What a student truly needs is not only knowledge and skills, but also role models, community, discussion, support, and long-term spiritual connection. The value of the college system is that it provides a more complete growth environment, allowing students not to study in isolation, but to grow inside relationships.

Of course, the college system is not perfect. It carries strong traditional features and may produce closure and a sense of class. But it at least reminds us that education cannot pursue only scale and efficiency. If a university simply admits students in batches and sends them out in batches, what it provides may be training, not necessarily education. Real education should let students be seen, guided, required, and supported. It must cultivate ability, but also shape character.

The core reflection this letter left me with is that the deepest value of a university is not only providing knowledge, but providing the environment a person needs in order to grow. The college system at Oxford and Cambridge matters because it turns school into a community, and turns students from isolated learners into people with belonging, responsibility, and relationships. Good education ultimately should not make students only good at exams and job hunting. Before they enter society, it should help them learn how to become whole people.
