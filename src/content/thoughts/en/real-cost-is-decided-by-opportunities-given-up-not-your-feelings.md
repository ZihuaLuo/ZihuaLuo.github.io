> Cost is not hardship itself, but the best opportunity given up for a choice.

In Lecture 014, Xue Zhaofeng continues explaining cost. Many people think cost means hardship, pain, trouble, or money already spent. In economics, real cost is the largest value given up. This small distinction changes how we understand many choices.

The swimming pool example is clear. Building a pool in the backyard creates discomfort: noise, mess, worry, and effort. But those feelings are not the true cost. The true cost is that the yard can no longer be used for a tent, flowers, a pavilion, or something else. Cost is not how painful the activity feels, but what better alternative it replaces.

This is easy to miss. People often treat immediate discomfort as cost, while ignoring the lost opportunity. Studying is hard, but the cost of studying is not the hardship itself; it is the work, recreation, social time, or rest that could have happened instead. Work is tiring, but its cost is the other path that the same time could have taken.

Cost is also not decided by the owner alone. It is shaped by other people's valuation of the resource. If a family owns a storefront on Chang'an Avenue and uses it to sell tea eggs, it may feel rent-free. But if someone else would pay high rent for that location, continuing to sell tea eggs has a cost: the rent given up.

Owning something does not make using it free. A house one owns can still be rented out. Time one owns can be used differently. Ability one owns can earn higher return elsewhere. Ownership may be personal, but opportunity cost is determined by social valuation.

This is why staying true to one's original aspiration can become expensive. When young, a person may have few options, so persistence costs little. As ability grows and opportunities expand, staying with the original path may mean giving up much larger alternatives. Love for a field is not costless; it means willingly bearing the best opportunity forgone.

Career choice works the same way. Spiritually, one may say my life is mine. Economically, society strongly shapes the range and cost of choices. What society needs, what it pays for, which industries grow, and which skills are valued all affect cost. Choosing literature over programming may mean giving up programmer income and opportunities.

This does not mean everyone should choose the highest-paying path. Meaning, freedom, stability, dignity, and interest matter. Mature choice simply cannot pretend there is no sacrifice. Clear people are not those who always choose the most profitable road. They are those who know what they have given up and accept the result.
