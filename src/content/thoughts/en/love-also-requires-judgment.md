> What is truly worth choosing is not always the most dazzling thing.

After reading Letter 021, "Shakespeare's Wisdom, Part II," from Wu Jun's *Silicon Valley Letters 2: The Google Methodology*, my biggest reflection was this: love and marriage are not only emotional matters. They also require reason, judgment, and responsibility. Many times, we think love is intense attraction, love at first sight, or being drawn to the light on someone. But through *The Merchant of Venice*, this letter reminds me that the more intense the emotion, the easier it is for a person to lose judgment. Mature love is not only being attracted. It is seeing the other person clearly, choosing them, and being willing to bear the cost after that choice.

In the letter, Wu Jun first discusses the two main threads of *The Merchant of Venice*: one is the love story around Bassanio's courtship of Portia, and the other is the conflict around Antonio, Shylock, and the contract for a pound of flesh. What impressed me most is the test of the three caskets left by Portia's father. Suitors must choose among a gold casket, a silver casket, and a lead casket. Only the casket containing Portia's portrait allows the suitor to marry her. On the surface, this looks like a game of chance, but Wu Jun points out that it is actually Shakespeare's summary of life wisdom.

The gold casket says, "Who chooses me shall gain what many men desire." This sentence is tempting, because most people are attracted by "what many men desire." It symbolizes fame, wealth, appearance, and worldly recognition. Many times, when people choose a person, a job, or even a way of life, they are unconsciously attracted by this gold casket: what everyone wants seems like it must be good, and what looks shining seems like it must be worth pursuing. But the letter reminds us that not everything that glitters is gold. What the public expects may not truly suit us, and what looks beautiful on the surface may not have real value.

The silver casket says, "Who chooses me shall get as much as he deserves." This sentence looks more rational than the gold casket because it emphasizes "deserving." But the problem is that people often overestimate what they deserve. The Prince of Arragon chooses the silver casket precisely because he believes he is noble enough and deserves Portia. Through this choice, Shakespeare satirizes a kind of arrogance: if a person always thinks they deserve the best, they may turn love and marriage into confirmation of their own self-worth, rather than truly understanding another person. The danger in love is not only blindly worshiping someone else. It may also be believing too much that one deserves everything.

Finally, Bassanio chooses the lead casket. The lead casket says, "Who chooses me must give and hazard all he hath." This sentence has no glamorous promise and no immediate reward. It even sounds heavy. But it reveals the essence of love and marriage: true intimacy is not about taking, but giving. It is not only wanting the other person's beauty, wealth, or status, but being willing to bear responsibility and give part of oneself to the other person and the relationship. Bassanio chooses the lead casket not because it looks good, but because he understands the meaning behind the choice.

This makes me think that many choices in real life are also "three caskets." We are often attracted by the gold casket because it represents external glamour. We are also easily tempted by the silver casket because it satisfies our self-esteem and makes us feel we deserve something better. But truly important choices are often more like the lead casket. They may not be the most beautiful or the easiest, but they require investment, responsibility, and commitment. Whether in relationships, study, career, or life paths, things of real value rarely come without cost. If a person only wants to receive and is unwilling to sacrifice, it is difficult to truly possess deep relationships and lasting happiness.

Still, I think the deeper point of the letter is not to deny emotion, but to remind us not to set emotion and judgment against each other. Shakespeare is not saying love is unimportant. On the contrary, he acknowledges that love has great power, and that people can become impulsive, excited, and irrational in love. Precisely because of this, marriage needs judgment even more. Liking someone may begin as an instant feeling, but whether two people are suitable for long-term life together, whether they are willing to share responsibility, and whether they can support each other in real life all require longer observation and choice. Love can begin with a heartbeat, but it cannot stop there.

This letter also makes me reflect on how many people today understand love and marriage. Some people over-pursue external conditions and turn love into a choice of the gold casket. Some overemphasize what they deserve and turn the relationship into a choice of the silver casket. But a mature relationship may be closer to the lead casket: not perfect, not necessarily aligned with every external expectation, but requiring both people to be willing to bear, restrain, and give for each other. If love has only romance and no responsibility, it fades easily. If marriage has only conditions and no sacrifice, it is hard to last.

The core reflection this letter left me with is that a truly good choice is not necessarily the most dazzling choice, but the choice that can withstand time and responsibility. Love does not completely reject reason, and marriage is not a denial of emotion. On the contrary, mature feeling preserves judgment beyond attraction and accepts cost after the choice. Many important things in life are like the lead casket. They do not look glamorous enough, but they require sincerity, responsibility, and sacrifice. Being able to understand this may be real wisdom.
